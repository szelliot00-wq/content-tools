# The bottleneck might be the air in the room

Source: https://blog.mikebowler.ca/2026/07/03/co2-and-decision-making/

## Summary
High CO2 levels in closed meeting rooms measurably impair the cognitive functions most needed in important meetings — strategy, planning, and decision-making under pressure. Research shows performance degrades significantly at 1,000 ppm (easily reached within an hour in a closed room) and becomes dysfunctional at 2,500 ppm. The problem extends to remote workers in home offices and is invisible from inside: people feel tired or foggy but blame the meeting length or each other, never the air. The fix is cheap — open a window, crack a door, or buy a CO2 monitor.

## Key takeaways
- CO2 above 1,000 ppm significantly degrades decision-making; above 2,500 ppm, performance enters the "dysfunctional" range on multiple cognitive measures.
- A closed meeting room with a few people reaches 1,000 ppm within the first hour — meaning nearly every long meeting is happening in impaired conditions.
- The impairment is invisible to those inside; symptoms (fatigue, foggy thinking) are routinely misattributed to disengagement, poor culture, or individual performance.
- Remote workers face the same problem in small home offices with the door shut, especially by mid-afternoon.
- A CO2 monitor costs less than an hour of a knowledge worker's time and can identify the problem immediately.
- Before blaming team dynamics or meeting culture, rule out the cheapest variable: open a window or door and measure the air.
