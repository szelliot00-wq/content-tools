# Study: "Mommy, do you love your phone more than me?"

Source: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2026.1766665/full

## Summary
Published in *Frontiers in Psychology* (June 2026), this study examined whether U.S. adolescents' perceptions of their primary caregiver's smartphone use — termed "technoference" or "phubbing" — are associated with insecure attachment. Researchers validated a 12-item Device Attachment Interference Scale (DAIS) with a general population sample of 600 adolescents (ages 12–17) and found that higher DAIS scores consistently predicted greater anxious and avoidant attachment to both mother- and father-like figures. The findings extend attachment theory into the digital age, suggesting that even routine, intermittent parental phone distraction may carry relational significance for adolescents.

## Key takeaways
- **Parental phone use is linked to insecure attachment:** Higher perceived caregiver device distraction correlated strongly with both anxious and avoidant attachment styles, regardless of whether the caregiver was a mother- or father-like figure.
- **46% of teens report distracted parents:** A 2024 Pew survey cited in the study found nearly half of teens say a parent is "at least sometimes" distracted by their phone during conversations.
- **A new validated scale (DAIS) was developed:** The 12-item Device Attachment Interference Scale showed strong internal consistency (α = 0.919) and a unidimensional factor structure, measuring adolescents' perception of caregiver attentional unavailability due to devices.
- **Device distraction is a uniquely controllable risk factor:** Unlike other attachment risk factors (neglect, mental illness, abuse), phone-related distraction is entirely within the caregiver's volitional control.
- **Causality remains unresolved:** The study is cross-sectional and correlational — it cannot determine whether device distraction causes attachment insecurity, or whether already-insecurely-attached teens are more sensitive to perceived parental inattention.
- **Clinical utility suggested with caution:** The DAIS may help clinicians identify device-related relational patterns in family therapy, though the authors urge restraint until further validity research is conducted.
