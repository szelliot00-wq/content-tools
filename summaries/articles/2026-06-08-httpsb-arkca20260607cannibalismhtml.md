# Cannibalism

Source: https://b-ark.ca/2026/06/07/cannibalism.html

## Summary
"Cannibalism" argues that the tech/software industry is uniquely vulnerable to AI disruption — more so than any other field — because software is verifiable by nature and the open-source ecosystem has inadvertently provided an extraordinarily rich training corpus for the very LLMs now threatening developers' jobs. The author draws on comments from tech leaders like Jack Dorsey and Sebastian Siemiatkowski to illustrate that, for the first time, the disruption is coming from within the industry itself. The piece explores likely economic and labor consequences, ultimately framing the situation as an unresolved tension the industry must now reckon with.

## Key takeaways
- The tech industry is "cannibalizing" itself: the open-source code developers freely shared has become the training data that enables AI to replace them.
- Software development is especially susceptible to AI automation because code is fundamentally verifiable — it either works or it doesn't — making it easier to automate and validate than most creative or knowledge work.
- Expect a race to the bottom on pricing, alongside a flood of low-quality AI-generated output, making it hard to distinguish quality work from "slop."
- The labor outcome will likely be bimodal: many developers pushed out entirely, while those who remain are expected to operate at dramatically higher throughput (e.g., 50–500 PRs/week instead of 5).
- The tools are here to stay; the open question is how individuals and the industry adapt to this new reality.
