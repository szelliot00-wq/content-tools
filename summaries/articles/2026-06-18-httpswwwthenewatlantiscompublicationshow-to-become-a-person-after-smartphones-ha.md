# How to Become a Person After Smartphones Have Rotted Your Brain

Source: https://www.thenewatlantis.com/publications/how-to-become-a-person-after-smartphones-have-rotted-your-brain

## Summary
Luke Burgis argues that smartphones have created three distinct psychological pathologies that erode a coherent sense of self: counterfeit rites of passage, *curiositas* (shallow, restless consumption of information), and diminished agency (a modern form of the ancient spiritual condition *acedia*). These pathologies respectively damage social identity formation, intellectual depth, and the capacity for purposeful action. Burgis proposes corresponding antidotes — meaningful friction-filled transitions, deep structured learning, and genuine leisure — as ways to reclaim a grounded, authentic self in the digital age.

## Key takeaways
- **Smartphones have eliminated productive friction** from life transitions (asking someone out, navigating without GPS), which historically helped people forge identity. Without that friction, people drift through adulthood without a clear sense of who they are.
- **Curiositas** is the pathology of compulsive, unfocused information consumption — scrolling for novelty rather than seeking genuine understanding. It crowds out serious, sustained thought.
- **Diminished agency** (*acedia*) is a spiritual listlessness that smartphones accelerate — a feeling of being unable to act meaningfully or direct one's own life.
- **Antidote to counterfeit rites**: Seek out real experiences that involve genuine risk, discomfort, or disconnection from technology.
- **Antidote to curiositas**: Join small, intentional learning communities (5–15 people) that study complex topics in depth over time — not casual book clubs, but structured intellectual effort.
- **Antidote to acedia**: Practice true leisure — unhurried meals, attentive listening, celebratory rituals — and, for those open to it, spiritual disciplines like prayer and fasting.
- The underlying question the article poses: how do you rebuild a **solid, real self** rather than the "shape-shifting simulacrum" that digital life tends to produce?
