# Rubin Tracks Skyscraper-Size Asteroids and Failed Supernovas

Source: https://www.quantamagazine.org/rubin-tracks-skyscraper-size-asteroids-failed-supernovas-and-interstellar-visitors-20260515/

## Summary
The Vera C. Rubin Observatory, now operational on Cerro Pachón in Chile, has begun delivering early scientific results ahead of its full 10-year Legacy Survey of Space and Time. The observatory's massive 8.4-meter telescope and world's largest digital camera are already uncovering rapidly spinning asteroids, cataloging supernovas, and detecting interstellar visitors. Scientists expect Rubin to revolutionize astronomy by generating 7 million alerts and 20 terabytes of data per night, enabling discoveries at a scale previously impossible.

## Key takeaways
- Rubin discovered 19 "superfast rotating" asteroids in early data, including 2025 MN₄₅ (~700 meters wide), which completes a full rotation every 1.88 minutes — far faster than expected for an asteroid of its size.
- The observatory's alert system sent 800,000 notifications in a single night during its February 2026 test run; the full survey will produce 7 million alerts nightly.
- Rubin is expected to find 250,000 Type Ia supernovas per year (vs. the ~100 used to discover dark energy in the 1990s), which could help resolve the Hubble tension.
- It can detect failed supernovas — stars that collapse silently rather than exploding — going 100 times fainter than other sky surveys.
- Rubin retroactively detected interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS 10 days before its official discovery, demonstrating its capability to spot rare interstellar visitors; scientists estimate it could find 5–500 such objects.
- Photometric redshift measurements from Rubin will map ~4 billion galaxies, advancing studies of dark energy, dark matter, and fast radio bursts.
- Rubin may detect small "imminent impactor" asteroids days in advance (vs. hours with current telescopes), enabling public alerts and scientific preparation for atmospheric impacts.
