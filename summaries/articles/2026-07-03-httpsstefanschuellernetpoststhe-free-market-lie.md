# The Free Market Lie: Why Switzerland Has 25 Gbit Internet and America Doesn't

Source: https://stefan.schueller.net/posts/the-free-market-lie/

## Summary
The article argues that the framing of "free markets vs. regulation" misses the real reason Switzerland has world-leading residential fiber internet (up to 25 Gbit/s) while the US and Germany lag behind. The core issue is that broadband infrastructure is a natural monopoly — building competing parallel networks wastes resources, while a single shared network with open access creates genuine service competition. Switzerland mandated a four-fiber, point-to-point architecture with open access for all ISPs, enforced it against Swisscom when the incumbent tried to revert to a shared model, and the result is dozens of competing providers over the same physical infrastructure. The US got territorial monopolies, Germany got wasteful overbuild, and both got stagnation.

## Key takeaways
- **Natural monopolies require different rules.** Infrastructure like fiber (or water pipes) should be built once as a shared neutral asset — duplicating it wastes billions and doesn't produce real competition.
- **Switzerland's secret is open-access, point-to-point fiber.** Every home gets four dedicated fiber strands terminating in a neutral hub any ISP can connect to. Switching providers requires only a phone call and an outlet ID number.
- **The US model produced territorial cartels, not competition.** Incumbents own the Points of Presence, blocking rivals from entering, so consumers have one choice and prices rise with no incentive to improve.
- **Germany's heavy regulation failed differently.** It favored infrastructure competition (multiple companies digging separate trenches) over duct-sharing, resulting in wasteful parallel builds and the same lack of consumer choice.
- **Regulation quality matters more than regulation quantity.** Switzerland's light-but-targeted rules (enforced by fines and court orders) outperformed both US deregulation and German over-regulation.
- **Incumbents will resist open access.** Swisscom tried to switch to a shared P2MP architecture in 2020, which would have locked out competitors. Switzerland's regulator (COMCO) stopped it, fined Swisscom 18 million francs, and forced a rollback.
- **Policy levers other countries could adopt:** mandatory duct/dark fiber sharing at cost, point-to-point fiber standards, strong regulatory enforcement, and allowing municipal networks where incumbents fail.
