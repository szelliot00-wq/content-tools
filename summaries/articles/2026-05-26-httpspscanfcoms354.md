# The User Is Visibly Frustrated

Source: https://pscanf.com/s/354/

## Summary
The article explores why coding agents feel so frustrating to use despite being algorithmically generated tools. The author argues that the conversational UX of these agents triggers human social instincts — making them feel like colleagues — but they fail to meet the expectations that come with that framing, such as learning from mistakes, adapting, or taking responsibility. This mismatch between perceived social cues and actual behavior amplifies frustration beyond what the technical failures alone would warrant.

## Key takeaways
- Coding agents are frustrating not just because they make mistakes, but because their human-like conversational style raises social expectations they cannot meet.
- The "helpful colleague" framing triggers instincts around accountability and learning, which makes repeated errors feel like personal failures rather than algorithmic noise.
- The author questions whether a more robotic, clinical UX — one that removes the human pretense entirely — might reduce frustration by resetting user expectations.
- The core tension is a UX design problem: agents are human enough to feel relatable, but not human enough to behave reliably as social actors.
