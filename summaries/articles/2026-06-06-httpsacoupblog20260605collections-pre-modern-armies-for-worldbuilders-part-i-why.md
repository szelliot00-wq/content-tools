# Pre-Modern Armies for Worldbuilders, Part I: Why They Fight

Source: https://acoup.blog/2026/06/05/collections-pre-modern-armies-for-worldbuilders-part-i-why-they-fight/

## Summary
Written by historian Bret Devereaux, this article is the first in a series aimed at worldbuilders seeking to create realistic pre-modern military systems. The central argument is that armies cannot be understood in isolation — they grow directly out of and reflect the civilian societies that produce them. The article focuses specifically on the "why" of military recruitment, cataloging the main principles that motivate soldiers to serve across different historical societies.

## Key takeaways
- **Armies mirror civilian society**: Military hierarchies, status structures, and organization always reflect the civilian social order. You cannot design a plausible army without first designing its society.
- **Ask four questions about your society first**: Is it agrarian? Is it a state? What kind of aristocracy does it have (military vs. warrior)? How do common farmers relate to that aristocracy?
- **Professional (employment-based) armies are the exception, not the rule**: They require a strong centralized state with extensive bureaucracy and revenues — rare in pre-modern contexts.
- **Four main recruitment principles** explain why soldiers actually serve:
  - **Employment** — paid to fight (uncommon as the primary basis)
  - **Entitlement** — service as the obligation of citizenship/political rights (Greek poleis, Roman Republic)
  - **Vocational** — fighting is your social role by birth (warrior aristocracies, all-warrior tribal societies)
  - **Clientage** — service as an obligation owed to a social superior (feudal retainers, levies)
- **"All-warrior" societies don't scale**: Specialization of labor and warfare are linked — complex societies inevitably produce a specialized warrior class rather than universal warriors.
- **Social structure constrains military options**: A weak state with no bureaucracy *must* rely on aristocrats and clientage; a society without citizenship cannot use entitlement recruitment. The options are not freely chosen.
