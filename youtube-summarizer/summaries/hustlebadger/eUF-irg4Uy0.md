# Communication for People Who Think Differently with Dave Martin

Video ID: `eUF-irg4Uy0`

## Summary
This video features Dave Martin, a former CPO turned leadership coach specializing in neurodiversity, speaking to a product management community hosted by Hustlebadger. Dave introduces the concept of "signal drift" — the phenomenon where a leader's message gets lost, weakened, or distorted before reaching its intended audience — and offers a practical 10-minute pre-meeting tool called "Signal Prep" to combat it. He also demonstrates how he uses an AI-powered Claude workflow to surface decisions, flag communication drift, and prioritize his day from dozens of emails, Slack messages, and meeting transcripts. The talk is relevant to product managers, leaders, and anyone who needs to communicate strategically with executives or senior stakeholders, with particular emphasis on those who are neurodivergent (ADHD, dyslexia, etc.).

---

## Key insights

- **Signal drift is the core problem**: When a message is transmitted from a leader to an audience, it often becomes fuzzy, overly complex, or unrepeatable. The result is that stakeholders leave meetings with different interpretations, and the leader loses influence and strategic credibility.

- **Detail is the enemy of influence at the executive level**: Coming from technical or product backgrounds, many leaders instinctively over-explain. They include edge cases, deep context, and exhaustive accuracy — which feels thorough but buries the headline. Executives want a clear decision framing, not a status dump.

- **Influence depends on repeatability**: If someone cannot repeat your message when you leave the room, you have not influenced them. Real influence means your key idea travels without you. This is the litmus test for whether your communication landed.

- **Three common communication mistakes**:
  1. **Not speaking the audience's language** — over-explaining complexity instead of leading with the headline the exec needs to make a decision.
  2. **Not defining the signal** — assuming hard work and shipping will naturally earn alignment, when in fact stakeholders regularly leave meetings with different interpretations.
  3. **Not leading the room** — falling into "fix mode" (jumping to solve problems immediately), equating speed with leadership, or staying quiet to avoid conflict. All of these erode trust and strategic credibility over time.

- **Neurodivergent leaders face amplified versions of these challenges**: ADHD can cause hyperfocus followed by energy crashes (peaks and troughs in rapid succession), making consistency — one of the most important leadership traits — very hard to sustain. Dyslexia and ADHD can also make real-time collaborative decision-making harder, not because of capability but because of how information is processed live.

- **Inconsistency is a career blocker**: Regardless of intelligence or output quality, being perceived as inconsistent will hold leaders back from promotion, especially into senior strategic roles. Signal Prep directly addresses this by creating a repeatable pre-meeting habit.

- **The "no status update" principle**: No executive genuinely wants a status update. They want to know what decisions need to be made to keep things moving. A status update is only relevant as a trigger for a decision (e.g., if things are going backwards). Leaders should reframe all meetings around decisions.

- **Fixing signal drift reduces stress, not just performance**: Dave notes that across seven years of coaching, the most surprising outcome wasn't promotions or better meeting outcomes — it was a dramatic reduction in stress and a recovery of energy for the leaders he worked with.

- **Burnout is rampant**: Dave references Lenny's workforce tech survey, which found over 50% of respondents had experienced or were experiencing burnout, making the energy and consistency aspects of this framework especially urgent.

- **Real-life example of Signal Prep in action**: On the day of the talk, Dave was preparing for a board meeting. The *recommendation* was to kill a legacy product line and go all-in on a new agentic solution that was outperforming it. The *repeatable message* (what he wanted the board to repeat without him in the room) was: "The old product line has diminishing returns and will not drive the exit we want — only the new solution will." These are distinct: the recommendation is the action; the repeatable message is the strategic narrative that justifies it.

- **Reflective vs. reactive thinking styles**: A practical, stigma-free way to accommodate different thinking styles in a team without requiring anyone to disclose neurodivergence. Teams can openly discuss whether their decision-making processes support people who need time to process (reflective) vs. those who can decide in the moment (reactive). This opens the door to accommodating neurodivergent team members organically.

- **Pattern recognition as a neurodivergent superpower**: Dave highlights that people with ADHD, for example, often have exceptional pattern recognition — they may spot something in a meeting that no one else notices — but may struggle to follow or contribute to a live, fast-moving discussion. Processes should be designed to capture that insight, not inadvertently exclude it.

- **AI tool — Claude daily briefing workflow**: Dave built a Claude-powered plugin (a saved prompt with integrations) in approximately 30 minutes with no code. It connects to Gmail, Slack, and Granola (meeting transcription tool). Every morning at 8am it processes the last 24 hours — in his example: 43 emails, 10 Slack messages, 5 meeting transcripts (with 34 filtered out as noise) — and produces a briefing that shows: pending decisions, where signal is shifting or going cold, cross-referenced patterns across workstreams, and a prioritized action list. It caught a DocuSign email he had missed entirely.

- **The "hot seat" team exercise**: In a previous VP-level team, Dave ran a monthly exercise where one person sat in the "hot seat" and every other team member had to say one thing they were grateful for and one thing they wanted the person to stop doing. The person in the hot seat could only respond with "thank you" — nothing else. It was intended as a one-off but the team requested it monthly for over two and a half years. It organically surfaced communication needs (e.g., "let's not decide this now — Ed needs time to reflect, let's pick it up tomorrow") and built strong team trust.

## Use cases

- **Product managers preparing for exec or board meetings** who want to stop over-explaining and start leading with decisions and recommendations.
- **Leaders who feel underestimated or overlooked** for promotions despite doing high-quality work — particularly those experiencing imposter syndrome or being labeled "not strategic enough."
- **Neurodivergent professionals (ADHD, dyslexia, etc.)** who struggle with real-time communication, energy inconsistency, or being misread by stakeholders.
- **Managers running team meetings** who want to reduce context-switching, rework, and post-meeting misalignment.
- **Anyone who regularly leaves meetings with more homework than when they went in** — Signal Prep is designed to prevent this loop.
- **Leaders managing upward** who need stakeholders and executives to carry their message to other rooms and decision-makers they have no direct access to.
- **Teams or managers trying to accommodate different thinking styles** without requiring disclosure of neurodivergence — the reflective/reactive framework applies here.
- **AI-curious professionals** who want a practical, no-code starting point for using Claude to manage information overload and communications across email, Slack, and meetings.
- **Leaders experiencing or at risk of burnout** who need tools that reduce cognitive load and restore energy consistency.

## Patterns & frameworks

### 1. Signal Drift
**What it is**: The degradation or distortion of a leader's message as it travels from them to their audience. The message becomes fuzzy, too broad, too complex, or simply unrepeatable.
**How it works**: It happens when leaders default to detail-heavy communication, don't define a clear signal, or fail to lead the room toward a decision. The result: stakeholders leave with different interpretations, decisions stall, and the leader loses strategic credibility.

---

### 2. Signal Prep (3-Step Pre-Meeting Tool)
**What it is**: A 10-minute preparation exercise done before any high-stakes meeting, built on the "Calm Leadership" methodology.
**How it works**:
- **Step 1 — What do I need from the room?** Clarify the specific outcome or decision you need from the audience, and surface it *early* in the meeting rather than building to it.
- **Step 2 — What is my one-line recommendation?** Know your recommendation cold. It should be short, clear, confident, and deliverable without hesitation. Vagueness signals uncertainty and creates anxiety in the room.
- **Step 3 — What will they repeat when I'm not in the room?** Define the exact narrative or strategic message you want carried forward. Use this phrase/concept as your anchor when answering questions — not always as a direct answer, but woven in as the repeatable takeaway.

---

### 3. Calm Leadership Framework
**What it is**: The broader methodology underlying Signal Prep, developed by Dave's coaching practice (Confidence In).
**Four pillars**:
- **Clarity** — Being unambiguous about what you want
- **Intentional** — Deliberate about what you communicate and why
- **Regulated** — Managing energy to avoid burnout and inconsistency
- **Repeatable** — Ensuring your message can travel without you

---

### 4. Decision–Meaning–Memory Framework
**What it is**: A one-page prep structure for thinking about how to show up in a meeting.
**How it works**: Before a meeting, answer three questions:
- **Decision** — What decision needs to be made?
- **Meaning** — What is the significance or strategic "so what"?
- **Memory** — What do you want them to remember (i.e., repeat) after?
Preceded by thinking about **emotion first**: How do I want people to *feel* when they leave this meeting?

---

### 5. Lead with the Destination, Not the Journey
**What it is**: A communication principle that inverts the typical "context first, conclusion last" structure.
**How it works**: State the outcome, recommendation, or decision need upfront. Then provide supporting context only as needed. This keeps executive audiences oriented and reduces the risk of losing them before the key message lands.

---

### 6. Reflective vs. Reactive Thinking Styles
**What it is**: A stigma-free lens for discussing different cognitive processing styles within a team, without requiring neurodivergence disclosure.
**How it works**: Leaders ask openly — "Are we a reflective or reactive team when it comes to decisions? Does our process support both?" This creates space for people who need pre-reads, processing time, or asynchronous input to contribute fully, without labeling anyone.

---

### 7. AI Signal Brief (Claude Workflow)
**What it is**: A daily automated briefing built as a Claude plugin (saved prompt + integrations), requiring no coding.
**How it works**: Connects to Gmail, Slack, and Granola (meeting transcripts). Each morning it scans the last 24 hours of communications, filters out noise, cross-references signals across workstreams, and outputs: pending decisions, areas of signal drift, pattern analysis, and a prioritized action list. Takes ~30 minutes to set up; runs automatically on a schedule.
