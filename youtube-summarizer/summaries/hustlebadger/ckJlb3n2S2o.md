# Why your strategy sucks and what to do about it, with Martin Eriksson

Video ID: `ckJlb3n2S2o`

## Summary
Martin Eriksson, co-founder of Mind the Product and operating partner at EQT Ventures, presents "the decision stack" — a five-layer framework (Vision, Strategy, Objectives, Opportunities, and Principles) designed to help product teams move with direction rather than just speed. The central argument is that most organizations fail not because they lack vision or ambition, but because they cannot answer *how* they will achieve their vision, leaving teams unfocused and working on the wrong things. Martin identifies strategy and principles as the two most commonly broken layers of the stack and provides practical tools for diagnosing, building, and communicating both. The talk is most relevant to product managers, product leaders, designers, and engineers at any company that is scaling or struggling with alignment, prioritization, and strategic clarity.

## Key insights

- **Speed vs. velocity:** Speed without direction (like lighting jet fuel on a runway) is spectacular but useless. Velocity is speed *with* direction — the scientific definition — and that distinction is the core problem most product teams face.
- **The 95% problem:** HBR research shows 95% of team members cannot accurately describe their organization's strategy. Martin observes this in virtually every organization he visits, making it one of the most pervasive and damaging problems in product organizations.
- **The decision stack answers five questions:** (1) Where are we going? (2) How are we going to get there? (3) What matters right now, and how do we measure progress? (4) What actions do we take? (5) How do we choose between actions? These questions connect top-down *and* bottom-up through the organization.
- **Strategy is not a vision, roadmap, OKR, or AI initiative.** Combining definitions from Michael Porter, Roger Martin, and Richard Rumelt, Martin defines great strategy as: *"A coherent set of choices about the actions you are going to take in order to achieve your vision."*
- **No such thing as a wrong strategy — only bad strategy:** Bad strategy is *not making a choice*. Martin illustrates this with Dropbox vs. Box: both made different but focused strategic choices and achieved roughly equal ROI. By contrast, Huddle (where Martin was VP of Product) tried to build file storage, chat, and task management simultaneously with $90M — and failed to do any of them well.
- **There is no strategy template.** Tools (Porter's Five Forces, Playing to Win, Jobs to Be Done, etc.) don't make strategy — people do. The journey to shared understanding matters more than the framework used.
- **Strategy is a mindset and a hypothesis.** Like any product hypothesis, strategy must be tested, iterated on, and updated when new information invalidates assumptions. It should never wait for an arbitrary end-of-year review cycle to be revised.
- **Principles are the most overlooked layer of the stack.** Martin learned this at Monster.com (1999): every product decision sparked months-long debates about job seekers vs. recruiters. When the founder declared *"job seekers always come first because if we build a great experience for them, recruiters will follow,"* those debates instantly disappeared.
- **The "even over" statement is the best tool for writing principles.** E.g., *"job seeker even over recruiter," "conversion even over revenue," "mobile even over desktop."* These mirror the structure of the Agile Manifesto — the right-hand side still matters, but the left-hand side wins any trade-off.
- **Real-world examples of strong principles:**
  - Google: *"Focus on the user and all else will follow"*
  - Shopify: *"Put merchants first"* (flipping the two-sided marketplace)
  - Klarna (pre-IPO): *"Conversion trumps profitability optimization"*
  - Supercell: *"Long-term retention even over short-term retention"*
  - Pinterest: *"Intuitive, not learned"*
  - Co-op (UK): *"We design for everyone"* (accessibility focus)
  - Google: *"Fast is better than slow"*
- **Principles must cost you something.** A principle like "great UX over bad UX" is meaningless — no one ever advocates for bad UX. A real principle forces a genuine trade-off between two good options.
- **Principles push decision-making to the edges of the organization.** Once codified, teams can resolve recurring debates without escalating, saving leadership time for genuinely novel decisions.
- **Values ≠ Principles.** Generic values ("build a delightful user experience," "excellence") are not actionable. Principles make values specific by embedding the trade-off: instead of "excellence," say "fast is better than slow."
- **Communication is non-negotiable.** Peter Drucker: *"Strategy without communication is just a plan. A plan without communication is just a dream."* Jeff Weiner (LinkedIn): *"When you're tired of saying it, people are starting to hear it."*
- **Make strategy a living artifact, not a shelf document.** Tactics include: messy physical posters with sticky notes, strategy on the first slide of every deck, embedded in retros, all-hands, and planning meetings — not separate alignment sessions.
- **Co-creation is the best communication strategy.** Involving team members (not just leadership) in building the strategy creates alignment *during* the process and a sense of ownership after. In large organizations, use representative democracy (20–30 people from different functions, levels, geos) rather than an ivory tower exec session.
- **Cadence matters:** Vision = long-term, relatively static. Strategy = tied to runway in startups; 3–5 years in mature orgs, but shortening. OKRs = quarterly (Martin prefers 6-week cycles). Opportunities = constantly in flux. Principles = somewhere in between. All layers must update immediately when a core assumption is invalidated — not at the next planning cycle.
- **Type 1 vs. Type 2 decisions (Jeff Bezos):** Most decisions are two-way doors — reversible, and should be made quickly. Only one-way-door decisions warrant prolonged debate. The decision stack gives teams confidence to run fast at two-way-door decisions.
- **Psychological safety is a prerequisite.** Google's research showed it is a stronger predictor of team performance than credentials. Without it, the feedback loops that make strategy adaptive cannot function.
- **The "This or That" game:** A facilitation tool (also available at thisorthat.stack.com) where teams vote on trade-off pairs (e.g., growth vs. profitability) to surface hidden misalignment within leadership before it muddles execution.
- **10x thinking over 10% thinking:** In the age of AI, incremental optimization is insufficient. Teams should ask what they would do differently if a KPI had to improve by 10x — this reframes strategy from iteration to transformation.
- **Fall in love with the problem, not the solution.** Staying problem-focused opens teams to new solutions as technology changes, rather than defending existing features.

## Use cases

- **Startups scaling beyond a single team:** When a company grows past the point where everyone fits around one desk and implicit direction is no longer sufficient.
- **Product leaders joining a new organization:** Using the decision stack to diagnose what's missing (commonly strategy and principles) before proposing solutions.
- **Leadership teams misaligned on priorities:** Running the "This or That" exercise to surface hidden divergence in the leadership team before it propagates down.
- **Product managers who can't explain why their work matters:** Tracing up the stack by asking "why" repeatedly to identify the broken link — missing strategy, unclear objectives, or absent principles.
- **Organizations where the same debates recur every sprint:** Codifying recurring trade-off decisions as principles so teams don't relitigate them repeatedly.
- **Companies undergoing digital transformation:** Large organizations like Novo Nordisk and Bloomberg have used the decision stack to create strategic clarity at scale.
- **ICs (engineers, designers, PMs) without clear strategic context:** Using the stack to ask upward questions and connect their work to organizational goals — without waiting for leadership permission.
- **Leaders managing CEO-level misalignment:** Using a documented, publicly visible decision stack as a commitment device to hold leadership accountable to stated strategy.
- **Teams adopting AI or new technologies:** Using 10x thinking and problem-first framing to avoid incremental AI adoption and pursue transformative strategy instead.
- **Remote or distributed teams:** Embedding strategy in Figma, Notion, slide decks, and whiteboards as a persistent reference since physical wall posters aren't available.

## Patterns & frameworks

### 1. The Decision Stack
**What it is:** A five-layer hierarchy of decisions — Vision → Strategy → Objectives → Opportunities → Principles — that connects organizational direction from the top to individual contributor work at the bottom, and back up again.
**How it works:** Each layer answers a specific question (see Key Insights). Moving *down* the stack answers "how"; moving *up* answers "why." At any layer, if the connection above or below is missing, the stack is broken. Teams use it to identify what's missing, what doesn't belong, and what needs to be decided. The stack is not a rigid org chart — it exists at multiple organizational levels, and practitioners should focus on the stack immediately around their own team.

### 2. Vision → Strategy → Objectives → Opportunities → Principles (VSOOP)
**What it is:** The specific five elements Martin uses to populate the decision stack.
**How it works:** Vision = long-term destination. Strategy = coherent set of choices for how to get there. Objectives = near-term measurable priorities (often OKRs). Opportunities = specific bets or initiatives being pursued. Principles = decision-making rules that encode past trade-offs and guide future ones. Principles have a feedback loop — they connect back *up* to strategy because they reflect what was said "no" to during strategy development.

### 3. The "Even Over" Statement (Principle Writing Tool)
**What it is:** A sentence structure for writing actionable principles, borrowed from the Agile Manifesto format.
**How it works:** Fill in: *"[X] even over [Y]."* Both X and Y must be genuinely good options (otherwise it's a false choice and not a real principle). The statement tells teams which side wins in any trade-off situation. Examples: *"Job seeker even over recruiter," "Conversion even over revenue," "Long-term retention even over short-term retention."*

### 4. This or That (Trade-off Discovery Exercise)
**What it is:** A facilitated team exercise (also a free web app at thisorthat.stack.com) for surfacing strategic trade-offs and misalignment.
**How it works:** Present teams or leadership with pairs of competing priorities (e.g., growth vs. profitability, speed vs. quality). Each person votes. Divergence in results reveals hidden misalignment that is likely already muddling organizational priorities. The output of the conversation can be codified as principles.

### 5. Speed vs. Velocity (Mental Model)
**What it is:** A framing distinction for diagnosing whether a team is moving productively or just moving fast.
**How it works:** Speed = rate of movement, no direction (jet fuel on a runway — impressive, useless). Velocity = speed + direction (jet fuel in a jet — actually gets you somewhere). Use this to reframe conversations about "going faster" toward "going in the right direction faster."

### 6. Type 1 vs. Type 2 Decisions (Jeff Bezos / One-Way vs. Two-Way Doors)
**What it is:** A decision taxonomy for calibrating how much deliberation a decision deserves.
**How it works:** Type 1 (one-way door) = irreversible; requires careful deliberation. Type 2 (two-way door) = reversible; should be made quickly and corrected if wrong. Most decisions are Type 2 but are treated as Type 1, creating unnecessary slowness. The decision stack helps teams recognize which type they're facing.

### 7. The Agile Strategy Loop
**What it is:** Applying agile iteration principles to strategy itself, not just product development.
**How it works:** Treat strategy as a hypothesis. Continuously check: Where are we today? What are possible solutions? Act to learn. Evaluate whether the strategy is still valid. Update immediately when a core assumption is invalidated — don't wait for an end-of-cycle review. This makes strategy a living practice rather than an annual artifact.

### 8. Co-Creation as Communication (Strategy Rollout Pattern)
**What it is:** A method for building organizational alignment into the strategy development process rather than bolting communication on afterward.
**How it works:** In small organizations, involve everyone. In large organizations, use representative democracy — include people from sales, different geos, different customer segments, and hands-on ICs alongside leadership. This eliminates jargon (non-experts will ask for clarification in real time), creates ownership, and dramatically reduces the communication burden after the strategy is finalized.

### 9. 10x Thinking (Goal-Setting Mental Model)
**What it is:** A heuristic for breaking teams out of incremental mindsets.
**How it works:** Instead of asking "How do we improve this KPI by 10%?", ask "What would we do completely differently if we had to improve it by 10x?" This forces genuinely strategic thinking and is especially relevant in the AI era where foundational shifts are possible.

### 10. The "Principle Codification" Pattern (Bottom-Up Principles)
**What it is:** A process for extracting principles from lived team experience rather than top-down mandate.
**How it works:** In retrospectives, identify: What trade-off debates came up this sprint/quarter? What assumptions were challenged? What did we say no to? Each of these is a candidate principle. Once codified, it benefits not just the team that discovered it but any other team facing the same decision across the organization.
