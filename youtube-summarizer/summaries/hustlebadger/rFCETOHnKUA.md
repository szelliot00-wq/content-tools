# What Happens When Product Owns the P&L - Kim Faura

Video ID: `rFCETOHnKUA`

## Summary
This video features Kim Faura, a seasoned product and commercial leader, sharing his experience across GIFGAF, CheckATrade, and Gumtree to argue that product managers already own the P&L whether they acknowledge it or not. His core argument is that product leaders who act like owners — getting close to finance, data, and commercial outcomes — create outsized impact for their organizations. He uses concrete case studies to illustrate how defining the right metrics, building cross-functional partnerships, and developing data maturity can fundamentally transform a business. The talk is most relevant to product managers, product leaders, and CPOs who want to grow into broader commercial and strategic roles.

---

## Key insights

- **Product already owns the P&L.** Kim's central provocation is that if product leaders don't take ownership of the P&L, someone else will do it for them — often suboptimally. Acting like an owner doesn't require being a financial expert; it requires getting close enough to financial functions to influence them.

- **One northstar metric can unify an entire company.** At GIFGAF, the single golden metric was Net Promoter Score (NPS). This felt innovative in 2010–2011 when few companies were measuring it. The entire business — every department — rallied behind it, with the belief that if NPS grew, users, retention, and revenue would follow.

- **Vision matters as much as metrics.** GIFGAF's guiding vision was "Together with our members, anything is possible." Kim cites the book *Act Like a Leader, Think Like a Leader* to emphasize that stepping into broader roles requires acting the part before you formally have it.

- **Member-driven growth can replace expensive traditional operations.** GIFGAF scaled to 4 million users and £600M+ turnover with no call centers. Customer support was handled by community members in forums. Sales came primarily from member-get-member referral programs, with some members earning hundreds of thousands of pounds annually through referrals.

- **Resourcefulness over resources is a startup superpower.** The micro-SIM crisis (iPhone 4 launch, 2010) is the defining example: GIFGAF had no micro-SIMs in stock, couldn't get them from supplier Gemalto for six months, so members organically created templates to cut standard SIMs, bought SIM cutters from China, and even built a dedicated website (MicroGaff) to match supply with demand. This solved the crisis within days and reinforced GIFGAF's community model.

- **The same crisis repeated with the nano-SIM (iPhone 5, 2012).** This time GIFGAF was prepared. Kim personally queued for an iPhone 5 on launch day and provided a working nano-SIM (cut from a micro-SIM into the chip itself) within 24 hours of launch — a direct competitive advantage born from their community approach.

- **Wrong metrics waste significant resources.** At CheckATrade, "web sessions" was a key business goal, but the paid marketing team was bidding on cheap, irrelevant keywords (e.g., "gardeners in Scotland" when there were no gardeners on the platform in Scotland). This drove traffic that never converted, illustrating the danger of vanity metrics disconnected from business outcomes.

- **Data maturity is a prerequisite for meaningful metrics.** When Kim joined CheckATrade (~£40M turnover), the business lacked the data infrastructure to understand the user journey. He worked closely with the data team to map the full journey: search → job description → finding a trade → contact (call/message) → quote → job completion → payment → review.

- **Metric evolution: sessions → contacts → leads → job opportunities.** Kim describes a deliberate ladder of metric sophistication at CheckATrade. A "lead" was defined as a unique homeowner-to-tradesperson connection (not multiple calls to the same person). A "job opportunity" was defined as one homeowner contacting multiple tradespeople for the same job — this became CheckATrade's north star metric.

- **Better data enabled a business model transformation.** The new data understanding allowed CheckATrade to move from a flat monthly subscription model (tradespeople paying a fixed fee regardless of results) to a pay-per-lead model. For example, 8 leads = £120; more leads = proportionally more cost. This aligned revenue directly with value delivered.

- **Make finance your best friend.** Kim specifically names GIFGAF's CFO Sarah Harvey as a crucial ally. He recommends product leaders find a financial sponsor and frame their team's value in terms finance understands: cost of the product team (often £500K–£1M annually) vs. value delivered to the business.

- **Marketplace economics are significantly more complex than B2C.** In a two-sided marketplace like CheckATrade, investing in consumer acquisition (paid marketing) doesn't generate direct revenue — value flows through the tradesperson side. Building an economic engine for a marketplace requires mapping both sides of the equation and understanding lifetime value on each side.

- **NPS is a perception metric, not just a performance metric.** At GIFGAF, despite running on O2's network (identical coverage), GIFGAF consistently scored higher than O2 on coverage satisfaction. Trust and transparency inflated perceived quality even where objective quality was identical.

- **Radical transparency drives NPS.** GIFGAF's "Best Plan Advice" feature actively recommended users move to cheaper plans when they weren't using their full allowance. Finance directors challenged this, but it massively increased trust and NPS. Most users didn't downgrade but loved the honesty — proving that transparent experiences drive long-term loyalty.

- **Telecom pricing complexity kills trust.** Traditional telecoms charged different prices to different customers for the same plan based on tenure or acquisition channel. GIFGAF deliberately unified tariffs so everyone on the same plan paid the same price, regardless of how long they'd been a customer or how they joined. This simplified experience was modeled after Spotify and Netflix rather than telecom competitors.

- **Cascading business outcomes to product outcomes is a core product leadership skill.** Kim describes a cascade: business outcome (revenue/profit) → product outcome (e.g., volume of users, ARPU, retention) → team-level OKRs. This cascade connects day-to-day product work directly to financial results.

- **Conversational AI will make NPS collection easier.** Kim predicts that as conversational AI becomes more prevalent, gathering NPS and related survey data will become simpler and more natural, though he doesn't go deep on the mechanics.

## Use cases

- **Product managers being asked to justify their team's ROI** — use Kim's framing of team cost (£500K–£1M) vs. business value delivered as a starting point for finance conversations.
- **Product leaders joining a new company with poorly defined or immature metrics** — use the CheckATrade case study as a template for auditing existing metrics and building a roadmap toward more meaningful ones.
- **Startups or early-stage product teams defining their first north star metric** — use GIFGAF's NPS story as an example of choosing a single unifying metric rather than a sprawling dashboard.
- **Two-sided marketplace product managers** — use the economic engine concept and CheckATrade's lead/job opportunity framework to understand how to model and track value on both sides.
- **Product leaders trying to build cross-functional influence** — use Kim's advice on partnering with CFOs/finance leaders to gain credibility and align on shared outcomes.
- **Community-driven or platform product teams** — use the GIFGAF member-driven roadmap and micro-SIM stories as examples of co-creating solutions with users rather than solving problems in isolation.
- **Product managers dealing with paid marketing teams** — use the "gardeners in Scotland" example to illustrate how misaligned marketing metrics waste budget and generate misleading traffic data.
- **Organizations transitioning from subscription to usage-based or outcome-based pricing models** — use CheckATrade's shift from flat subscription to pay-per-lead as a framework for tying pricing to delivered value.
- **Product leaders preparing to step into GM, MD, or CPO roles** — the entire talk is a roadmap for expanding beyond pure product thinking into commercial ownership.

## Patterns & frameworks

**1. The Single North Star Metric**
*What it is:* Choosing one primary metric that the entire business — every department — rallies behind, rather than managing multiple competing KPIs.
*How it works:* At GIFGAF, NPS served this role. The logic is: if you move this one metric, downstream business outcomes (users, retention, revenue) follow. It creates organizational alignment and simplifies prioritization.

**2. OKR Framework (Objectives & Key Results)**
*What it is:* A goal-setting methodology popularized by John Doerr and adopted early by Google, which Kim implemented at GIFGAF and CheckATrade.
*How it works:* Teams set quarterly objectives with measurable key results. This forces focus (choosing a small number of meaningful metrics per quarter), creates accountability, and gives teams autonomy in how they achieve outcomes. Kim ran quarterly OKR reviews and would select from many potential metrics the ones teams could realistically move.

**3. The Metric Maturity Ladder**
*What it is:* A progression from simple/vanity metrics toward sophisticated, financially meaningful metrics as data infrastructure matures.
*How it works:* At CheckATrade: Sessions → Contacts → Leads (unique connections) → Job Opportunities (north star). Each step required greater data investment and cross-functional collaboration but delivered progressively more meaningful business insight and enabled better product and pricing decisions.

**4. Teresa Torres' Continuous Discovery Habits — Outcome Cascade**
*What it is:* A framework for connecting high-level business outcomes to product outcomes to team-level work.
*How it works:* Start with what finance cares about (revenue, profit) → identify product outcomes that drive those (user volume, ARPU, retention) → cascade to team OKRs. This creates a clear line of sight from individual team work to business results.

**5. The Economic Engine (Dan Hawkenmire)**
*What it is:* A concept that a business is only truly understood when it can be expressed as an equation linking investment to output to profitability.
*How it works:* Map what happens when you invest £1 in a channel: what traffic does it generate → what is the conversion rate → what is the lifetime value → what is the net profit? For B2C this is relatively straightforward. For marketplaces, it requires modeling both sides (consumer and supplier) and understanding how value flows between them. Kim applied this at both CheckATrade and Gumtree.

**6. Community-Led / Member-Driven Product Development**
*What it is:* A pattern of involving users directly in problem-solving, roadmap prioritization, and even operations rather than treating them as passive consumers.
*How it works:* At GIFGAF, members voted on product ideas via a "Labs" section, handled peer-to-peer customer support in forums, and organically solved operational problems (the SIM-cutting example). This reduced costs, increased NPS, and created a virtuous cycle: better experience → more members → more community value → lower operating costs → savings passed back to members.

**7. Radical Transparency as an NPS Driver**
*What it is:* A deliberate strategy of being openly honest with customers even when it appears commercially counterproductive.
*How it works:* GIFGAF's "Best Plan Advice" recommended cheaper plans to existing customers. While this risked reducing ARPU, it dramatically increased trust and NPS. The insight is that perceived fairness inflates experience scores across all dimensions — even ones (like network coverage) that are objectively identical to competitors.

**8. Unified Pricing / Tariff Simplification**
*What it is:* A structural decision to charge all customers the same price for the same plan, eliminating tenure-based or channel-based pricing variations.
*How it works:* Modeled after Spotify and Netflix rather than traditional telecoms. Eliminates billing complexity, builds trust, simplifies operations, and makes NPS tracking more reliable since pricing perception is uniform across the user base.
