# OKR Solar System with Cansel Sörgens

Video ID: `AH0tk1ErrdA`

## Summary
This video transcript details the shortcomings of traditional cascaded OKRs and introduces the "OKR Solar System" as an alternative. The core argument is that company strategy should act as the central "sun," with various types of teams (the "planets") directly orbiting and aligning with it, rather than rigidly cascading goals down organizational hierarchies. This approach fosters dynamic, cross-functional collaboration, leading to faster execution and more impactful strategic outcomes. It is highly relevant for product managers, strategy execution coaches, leaders in agile transformations, and anyone struggling with OKR implementation due to silos, dependencies, or slow strategic progress.

## Key insights
- **Problems with Cascading OKRs:** Traditional cascading OKRs, often mirroring org charts, reinforce silos, create excessive dependencies, lead to constant synchronization meetings, slow down execution, and drain motivation. Teams feel stuck in "waiting mode," and organizations abandon OKRs due to perceived ineffectiveness.
- **OKR Definition:** OKRs (Objectives, Key Results, Initiatives) aim to close the strategy-execution gap, align teams around shared outcomes, and establish an adaptive strategy execution system through frequent learning and adaptation loops.
- **Inspiration for the Solar System:** The concept is inspired by Christina Wodtke's "Radical Focus" (aligning OKRs to company strategy, not more than one level deep) and Peter Senge's "The Fifth Discipline" (defining a true team as those who need one another to act).
- **The OKR Solar System Model:** The company's strategy and business goals are the "sun." All other teams and their OKRs are "planets" that orbit and align directly with this central strategy, allowing for flexible team structures based on strategic needs.
- **Types of Teams:**
    1.  **Existing Unit Teams:** Autonomous teams (e.g., Marketing, HR) capable of achieving goals independently within their domain.
    2.  **Product Organization Teams:** Teams aligned under a product strategy/OKR (e.g., a checkout team) to prevent priority conflicts and re-siloing within product development. This also includes "enabler teams" (e.g., platform teams) aligning to serve product-facing teams.
    3.  **Cross-Team/Cross-Unit Shared Mission Teams:** Temporary, interdisciplinary teams formed specifically for strategic topics that require collaboration across multiple units or departments (e.g., Marketing and Sales for store hotspots, or Marketing, Sales, and Product for an omni-channel experience). These teams are formed based on skill needs and commitment, not pre-existing org charts.
- **Implementation Strategy:** To implement cross-unit teams, leadership must clarify required resources, form the team, and secure commitment from members to focus on the shared OKR for a defined cycle (e.g., 3-4 months). It's crucial not to overload individuals and to respect different team paces, avoiding rigid quarterly planning for all.
- **Benefits:** The OKR Solar System is org-chart agnostic, allowing strategy to shape structure. It enables dynamic re-teaming, respects varying paces of strategic work (not bound by quarterly calendars), fosters collaboration, and accelerates progress on strategic initiatives.
- **Vestel Case Study:** A home appliance company in Turkey adopted this approach for strategic themes. They formed cross-level, interdisciplinary teams for exploratory topics. The first cycle was a learning phase, gathering data. Re-teaming in the second cycle led to innovative solutions, such as creating an exclusive radio program for non-employee contractors to build loyalty. This resulted in significantly faster product development (3x quicker) and organic adoption across other units.

## Use cases
- Organizations experiencing slow strategic progress due to siloed departments and dependencies in their OKR implementation.
- Companies where OKRs lead to excessive synchronization meetings and a feeling of "waiting" rather than proactive work.
- Product organizations where different product teams face priority conflicts and struggle to align on shared product outcomes.
- When launching complex, cross-functional strategic initiatives like new business models, market entries, or omni-channel customer experiences.
- Leaders and change managers seeking to shift organizational culture towards greater collaboration, adaptability, and outcome-focused work.
- Companies aiming to improve the speed, efficiency, and innovativeness of their strategic projects.
- Any organization where the current hierarchical structure hinders effective cross-departmental collaboration on key goals.

## Patterns & frameworks
- **OKRs (Objectives and Key Results):** A goal-setting framework designed to bridge the gap between strategy and execution. It comprises:
    -   **Objective (O):** The "what and why" – a qualitative, aspirational goal.
    -   **Key Results (KRs):** The "how we know" – measurable indicators of objective achievement.
    -   **Initiatives (I):** The "how we get there" – specific tasks or projects undertaken to achieve the KRs.
-   **OKR Solar System (Cansel Sörgens' Framework):** A holistic strategy execution framework. It positions the company's overarching strategy (like the "sun") at the core, with various types of teams (the "planets") orbiting and aligning directly with it. This model emphasizes flexible, purpose-driven team formation (including temporary, cross-functional teams) over traditional hierarchical cascading to enable dynamic strategy execution.
-   **"Radical Focus" (Christina Wodtke):** A widely recognized book and methodology for implementing OKRs. The speaker references Wodtke's advocacy for *aligning* OKRs directly to the company strategy, rather than cascading them down through multiple layers of management or departments, ensuring direct connection to the highest-level goals.
-   **"The Fifth Discipline" (Peter Senge):** A foundational book on learning organizations. The speaker quotes Senge's definition of a true team as "those who need one another to act," which underpins the idea of forming teams based on interdependence and shared mission rather than rigid organizational boxes.
-   **Impact Map:** (Mentioned in the Q&A) A tool used to define long-term, measurable strategic outcomes, rather than just short-term goals. This map provides a consistent orientation for all OKR teams, allowing them to connect their work to broader, enduring strategic goals.
