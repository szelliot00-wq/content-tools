# Are Your Product Decisions Commercial Enough? with Elisabeth Ling

Video ID: `2TrQs7D1JuM`

## Summary
This video transcript, featuring Elisabeth Ling, details how product managers can make their product decisions more commercially viable and impactful. Ling argues that while user value creation is crucial, equal emphasis must be placed on value capture and commercial outcomes, especially given the high cost of product and technology development. She presents three practical ideas for achieving this: aligning teams on overall commercial goals, strategically de-risking through focused discovery, and ruthlessly allocating resources based on portfolio performance. This advice is particularly relevant for product leaders and teams in B2B scale-ups seeking to drive substantial growth and build thriving, commercially astute product organizations.

## Key insights
*   **Dual Focus:** Product development must equally prioritize value creation (for users) and value capture (monetization). Obsessing solely on user value without considering commercial viability leads to unsustainable products.
*   **Relentless Urgency & Clarity:** Speed to market is critical, especially in the age of AI. Achieving commercial outcomes requires extreme clarity in vision, ensuring everyone in the company, not just the product team, is aligned and "rowing in the same direction" towards shared commercial goals.
*   **"Mountain vs. Mansion" Exercise:** Teams often get stuck refining small features ("the mansion") instead of focusing on big commercial targets ("the mountain"). To counteract this, product and tech teams should collaborate with commercial leadership (CEO, CRO, CMO) to:
    *   Project annual revenue run rate growth over 3-4 years (the "blue curve").
    *   Estimate what sales could achieve with *no new product development* (just P1 bug fixes – the "red curve").
    *   The gap between these curves represents the commercial value product must generate.
    *   Adjust targets forward by B2B lead times (e.g., 6, 9, or 12 months) to emphasize future commercial accountability.
    *   Calculate the required average commercial value per sprint (e.g., "$100k value in every weekly sprint"). Teams often find they are "missing one zero, or two zeros," highlighting a gap in commercial ambition.
*   **Product/Tech as a Cost Center:** Product and technology development is costly, often representing 15-25% of a company's Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR). This necessitates informed bets and efficient resource allocation.
*   **Discovery as De-risking:** Discovery is not a checklist of activities but a process of tackling the largest risks first. Prioritize risks that are both highly likely to cause project failure and about which there is currently little knowledge (the "top-right" quadrant of a risk matrix). This ensures fast and efficient progress by focusing only on what matters most.
*   **Ruthless Resource Allocation:** "What got you here won't get you there." Continuously evaluate product portfolios using frameworks like the BCG Matrix. Be prepared to ruthlessly deploy (or redeploy) squads and effort based on strategic priorities, even if it means significantly reducing resources for well-loved "cash cow" products to accelerate "star products" or new growth areas. For example, a mature product generating substantial revenue might be maintained by only two engineers if five other squads are needed to accelerate a star product against a competitor.
*   **Stay in Love with the Problem, Not the Solution:** Avoid falling in love with specific features or solutions. This detachment allows for more objective and strategic resource allocation and the willingness to sunset products when necessary.
*   **Qualities of a Strong Product/Tech Team:** These teams are known for driving client value and commercial revenue (even if not their North Star metric), explaining technology clearly, being great team players, taking measured risks transparently, knowing customers/competition deeply, and hiring/retaining top talent.

## Use cases
*   **Strategic Planning:** Companies planning their annual or multi-year product roadmaps, especially B2B scale-ups aiming for significant growth (e.g., 50M to 500M ARR).
*   **Resource Allocation:** Product leaders needing to make tough decisions about where to deploy engineering squads, budget, and time across a portfolio of products.
*   **Improving Commercial Alignment:** Product managers and leaders struggling to connect their team's output directly to measurable commercial outcomes or feeling disconnected from sales/marketing goals.
*   **Discovery Process Improvement:** Teams looking to make their discovery efforts more efficient and impact-driven by focusing on de-risking the most critical unknowns rather than just performing activities.
*   **New Product Launch Evaluation:** Assessing the commercial viability of new product ideas before significant investment, ensuring they address a sufficiently large market opportunity.
*   **Addressing Complacency:** Leadership teams in companies with strong "cash cow" products that might be masking inefficiencies or delaying necessary innovation.
*   **Product Management Training:** Guiding product managers, particularly in B2B, to adopt a more commercially savvy mindset and concrete methods for valuing their work.

## Patterns & frameworks
*   **Value Creation vs. Value Capture:** A fundamental mental model for product development, emphasizing that creating utility for users must be balanced with the company's ability to generate revenue from that value.
*   **Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) (Clay Christensen, "Competing Against Luck"):** A thinking framework that focuses on understanding the underlying "jobs" customers are trying to get done, helping build products that truly matter and can form a viable commercial company.
*   **Relentless Urgency (eBay Europe Influence):** A mindset emphasizing speed, momentum, and continuously driving towards goals with a strong sense of priority and timeliness.
*   **"Mountain vs. Mansion" Analogy & Commercial Gap Calculation:** A practical exercise for product and commercial teams to align on long-term commercial targets ("mountain") versus short-term feature refinements ("mansion"). It involves plotting projected growth, baseline sales with no new product, and future commercial value, then calculating the required average commercial value per sprint.
*   **Discovery as De-risking (Marty Cagan, "Inspired"):** A product development philosophy centered on identifying and addressing the largest risks (value, usability, feasibility, business viability) early in the development cycle, rather than simply building out features.
*   **Risk Matrix (Referenced from an unnamed book):** A tool for prioritizing risks by mapping them against two axes: 1) likelihood of causing product failure and 2) current knowledge about the risk. The goal is to focus on risks that are highly impactful and poorly understood.
*   **Ruthless Strategic Allocation (Based on BCG Matrix principles):** A strategy for deploying resources (squads, time, budget) across a portfolio of products by categorizing them into "Stars," "Cash Cows," "Question Marks," and "Dogs." This allows for informed decisions to shift resources from mature or underperforming products to high-growth or strategically important areas.
*   **"What Got You Here Won't Get You There":** A principle that advises companies, especially scale-ups, not to assume the strategies or products that led to initial success will be sufficient for future growth. It encourages continuous exploration of new growth vectors.
*   **"Dealing with Darwin" (Geoffrey Moore):** A book referenced for its insights on how large companies can effectively extract resources from core, mature products ("cash cows") to fund innovation and new ventures.
