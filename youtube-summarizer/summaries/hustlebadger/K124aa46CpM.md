# How Growth Really Happens with Francesca Cortesi

Video ID: `K124aa46CpM`

## Summary
This video details how product growth truly happens, emphasizing continuous product discovery over rapid, unvalidated AI development. Francesca Cortesi, a seasoned CPO, shares her seven years of experience implementing discovery in large organizations, arguing that success in the age of AI (where speed is a commodity) requires returning to first principles: identifying real problems, ensuring market viability, and verifying solvability before building. The talk outlines a four-phase model for discovery adoption, offers counterintuitive lessons learned, and stresses the importance of proving business impact over process adherence, making it highly relevant for product managers and leaders aiming to drive sustainable growth and reduce risk in their organizations.

## Key insights
-   **AI Speed Paradox:** While AI enables rapid product development, 95% of AI ideas fail. This highlights a common pitfall: prioritizing speed of delivery over solving genuine user problems, often leading to "abandonware" – quickly built solutions that provide no value.
-   **First Principles for Impact:** To succeed, especially with AI, product teams must focus on fundamental questions: Is there a real problem to solve? Is the problem large enough to generate revenue for the business? Can we effectively solve it?
-   **Continuous Product Discovery is Essential:** This ongoing process of testing hypotheses helps answer the first principles, reduce development risk, and ensure solutions are customer-anchored and drive business value. It's not a sequential "double-track" but a continuous loop.
-   **Organizational Resistance is Normal:** Common challenges include sales gatekeeping customers, executives dictating solutions, an obsessive focus on shipping fast (measured by features out), and the "solve it with AI" mentality. These are not excuses to skip discovery.
-   **Phased Discovery Adoption:** Francesca identifies four stages:
    1.  **Start:** Curious PMs validate existing backlog assumptions "under the radar." Crucially, *do not block delivery with discovery* at this stage.
    2.  **One Pilot Team:** A product trio (PM, Designer, Engineer) works in ideal conditions to prove discovery's business impact, linking it directly to tangible value.
    3.  **Multiple Trios:** Scale discovery gradually, one team at a time, creating conditions for success. What worked for the first team may not work for all.
    4.  **Full Scale Discovery:** Discovery is used for evaluating all major opportunities (new product lines, revenue models), requiring broad organizational buy-in beyond just the product function.
-   **Build Trust by Showing, Not Telling:** To cross the "chasm" of adoption, demonstrate concrete business value generated by discovery. An example from Hemnet showed how discovery transformed a vague growth goal into a specific, high-converting "free renewal" package for property relisting, significantly boosting revenue.
-   **Impact Over Process:** Focus on the business outcomes of discovery (e.g., insights, validated assumptions, reduced risk) rather than strict adherence to methodologies or product jargon. "Nobody cares about the process" if it doesn't deliver value.
-   **Counterintuitive Lessons Learned:**
    1.  **Don't start discovery on your biggest impact area:** The pressure is too high for both the team and the new discovery practice. Start with lower-stakes projects to build confidence and support.
    2.  **Don't treat discovery adoption as a process:** Avoid getting trapped in process lingo; focus on the value it creates, especially when engaging non-product stakeholders.
    3.  **Don't optimize for perfect scalability:** Allow flexibility. Different teams (B2C, B2B, platform) will require different discovery techniques and rhythms. Embrace the "mess" to achieve true empowerment.
-   **Discovery is a Journey:** Adoption is not a destination. Even after achieving widespread success, be prepared to continuously prove discovery's value, especially with leadership changes or market shifts.
-   **Start Simple with Tools:** Initially, basic tools like Miro for an Opportunity Solution Tree can be sufficient. More advanced tools (e.g., Jira Product Discovery, specialized user insight platforms) can be integrated as maturity grows, but don't overcomplicate the start.

## Use cases
-   Organizations struggling to gain traction with product discovery or looking to implement it more effectively.
-   Product managers and CPOs facing resistance from sales, engineering, or leadership regarding user research and validation.
-   B2B startups highly dependent on a single client, seeking to broaden their market appeal and avoid building an overly niche product.
-   Teams in AI/ML heavy environments needing to shift focus from "can we build it fast?" to "should we build it, and for whom?"
-   Companies with a strong AB-testing culture that need to introduce earlier, less expensive validation methods.
-   Product leaders aiming to connect product development efforts directly to clear, measurable business outcomes and drive revenue growth.
-   Teams needing to balance delivering on immediate demands with exploring broader market opportunities.

## Patterns & frameworks
-   **First Principles:** A foundational thinking approach where complex problems are broken down into basic, self-evident truths. In product management, this involves asking: "Is there a problem here to solve?", "Is the problem big enough for us to make money?", and "Can we solve it?"
-   **Continuous Product Discovery:** An iterative, ongoing process of learning from users and validating solutions throughout the product development lifecycle, as opposed to a separate, upfront discovery phase. It emphasizes continuous hypothesis testing.
-   **Discovery Adoption Phases (Start, One Pilot Team, Multiple Trios, Full Scale Discovery):** A progression model for integrating discovery practices into an organization, from initial, isolated experiments to widespread, strategic application across all teams.
-   **Crossing the Chasm (for internal adoption):** A concept (adapted from Geoffrey Moore's work) applied to internal change initiatives, describing the challenge of moving from early enthusiasts to broader organizational adoption by demonstrating clear, tangible value.
-   **Opportunity Solution Tree (by Teresa Torres):** A visual framework used to connect a desired business outcome to underlying customer opportunities, and then to various potential solutions that address those opportunities. It helps teams explore, prioritize, and articulate their product strategy.
-   **Assumption Testing:** The practice of identifying and testing key assumptions (e.g., about user needs, behaviors, market size, technical feasibility) early in the product development process, typically through qualitative or low-fidelity methods, to reduce risk before significant investment.
-   **AB Testing:** A method of comparing two versions of a product, feature, or design element against each other to determine which performs better based on a specific metric. The speaker highlights its expense and advocates for earlier-stage assumption testing for "two-way door" decisions.
-   **"Showing, Not Telling":** A strategy for driving organizational change, focusing on demonstrating the practical impact and benefits of a new approach (like discovery) through successful case studies and tangible results, rather than just explaining its theoretical advantages.
-   **Two-way Door vs. One-way Door Decisions:** A mental model for categorizing decisions based on their reversibility. "Two-way door" decisions are easily reversible and can be made quickly, while "one-way door" decisions are high-stakes, difficult to reverse, and require more thorough validation. Francesca applies this to product experiments, suggesting earlier discovery for two-way doors.
