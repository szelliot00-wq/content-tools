# Product is Hard. It should still be fun: Charity Ibhadon (WPP)

Video ID: `Pxp4ga--bkM`

## Summary
Charity Ibhadon, a senior product leader at WPP with a background spanning investment banking and e-commerce (ASOS), delivers a talk arguing that product management is structurally hard — not occasionally hard — and that the goal isn't to eliminate that difficulty but to stay good at the job while preserving genuine enjoyment of it. She structures her talk around five stages: Chaos, Scars, Noise, Joy, and Monday Actions. The talk is a direct, experience-grounded pushback against hustle culture, fear-driven product content, and the myth that seniority makes the role easier. It is most relevant to practicing product managers at all levels, product leaders managing teams, and early-career PMs vulnerable to predatory online content.

## Key insights
- **Product is structurally hard, not accidentally hard.** Ambiguity, changing priorities, and accountability without authority are not exceptions — they are the baseline condition of the role, every day.
- **Seniority doesn't reduce the difficulty; it raises the stakes.** Ibhadon expected delegation and decision sign-offs when she became more senior. Instead, the problems just got bigger and the decisions heavier.
- **You can't eliminate uncertainty — you get better at living in it.** The job is not to remove tension or ambiguity but to make both productive.
- **The "CEO of the product" framing is harmful.** It places unnecessary and unrealistic pressure on individuals (from APM to CPO) by implying sole ownership of outcomes. Product is about adaptation, not control.
- **Working harder is not a substitute for clarity.** More meetings, more documents, and longer hours in response to chaos just produce exhaustion — the ambiguity remains unchanged.
- **Resilience is three things: energy, perspective, and recovery.** If any one breaks, everything feels harder. It is not about how much you can tolerate before you break.
- **Protecting your energy matters more than protecting your time.** Constant context-switching (Slack, email, back-to-back meetings) erodes the capacity for deep thinking — and if thinking is the job, burning energy leaves you unable to do the job by Thursday.
- **The pivot and letting go quickly is progress, not failure.** At ASOS, an entire week's work was scrapped on a Monday morning because Rihanna popularized "oxblood red" — the faster you release the old direction, the faster you move.
- **Conflict is alignment in progress.** If nobody disagrees, either the problem hasn't been thought about long enough or it doesn't matter enough. Ibhadon intentionally hired a PM specifically because his personality would productively clash with a difficult stakeholder who needed a debating partner, not a yes-person.
- **Failed launches produce clarity faster than success.** In e-commerce A/B testing, a result of -0.1% still teaches you something. Clarity accumulates faster than wins.
- **Fear-driven product content monetizes insecurity.** A large portion of LinkedIn influencer posts, Reddit threads, and £1,500–£2,000 courses exploit anxiety (e.g., "AI will replace PMs," "product is dead") to drive engagement and sales. Ibhadon called out a specific influencer whose viral post about Google requiring PMs to build prototypes in Cursor in 45 minutes was fact-checked and debunked by a Google product director and a Google ML engineer in the comments — and then reframed the next day as "my post went viral, still buy my course."
- **What you consume shapes how you think about your job.** Constant exposure to fear-based content can flip someone from confident to self-doubting within a day, unrelated to actual performance.
- **Curiosity outlasts confidence as a hiring signal.** The two senior PMs she most recently hired were not the ones who talked loudest about shipping; one asks excellent questions, the other synthesizes multiple viewpoints into a narrative that makes everyone feel heard.
- **Humor is a functional tool for managing tension.** Teams that can laugh together handle pressure better. A wordless eye-roll exchanged with an engineering lead after a three-day Q3 planning marathon made an entire office laugh — it didn't undermine the work, it acknowledged the shared intensity.
- **AI adds complexity to the role of PM, not just to the product.** She mentions AI three times deliberately and briefly — noting it adds both complexity and simplicity to the job itself, not just to what gets shipped.

## Use cases
- **Mid-career PMs feeling burned out or stuck** who are working harder without feeling more effective.
- **Early-career PMs** being targeted by expensive online courses and certification schemes and needing a framework to evaluate which content is genuinely useful.
- **Product leaders managing teams** who want language and mental models to coach resilience and healthy conflict without normalizing overwork.
- **PMs navigating frequent strategic pivots** (e.g., e-commerce, startups) who need to reframe letting go of prior work as a healthy skill rather than a failure.
- **Anyone struggling with stakeholder conflict** who interprets disagreement as dysfunction rather than a sign that the problem matters.
- **PMs suffering from social media anxiety** about falling behind, being replaced by AI, or not being "strategic" enough — this talk directly names and dismantles those fears.
- **Hiring managers** assessing how to evaluate PM candidates beyond their ability to rattle off launches and metrics.
- **Anyone in an organization with heavy meeting culture** who needs permission and a framework to audit and cut calendar bloat.

## Patterns & frameworks

**The Five Stages of Product (talk structure)**
Chaos → Scars → Noise → Joy → Monday. Used as a narrative arc to move from diagnosing why product is hard, through the real sources of learning, through what to filter out, toward what to protect and act on.

**Resilience as Three Pillars: Energy, Perspective, Recovery**
Ibhadon redefines resilience not as tolerance for suffering but as a system with three interdependent components. If any one fails, the whole system degrades. The practical implication is that protecting recovery time (football, PlayStation, whatever works) is as professional an obligation as shipping.

**"Is this making me better or making me anxious?" Content Filter**
A binary diagnostic for evaluating any external input — course, LinkedIn post, newsletter, podcast. The question is not "is this about product?" but "does engaging with this actually develop my skills or does it feed insecurity?" Applied to curating which voices, accounts, and platforms to follow or unfollow.

**Accountability Without Authority as a Structural Feature, Not a Bug**
Rather than treating the accountability/authority mismatch as a fixable problem, Ibhadon frames it as the defining condition of product management. The job is to function effectively inside that tension rather than to resolve it.

**The "Bread Shelves" Scope Discipline**
A mental model for preventing energy diffusion: your job is to stack the bread shelves perfectly — not to wander into the frozen aisle to fix the salmon. Post-burnout, Ibhadon uses this to consciously bound which problems are hers to absorb and which are not.

**Conflict as Alignment in Progress**
A reframe: when disagreement surfaces in a room, it signals that the problem is serious enough to generate competing views and that the group is actively working toward alignment — not that something has gone wrong. The PM's role is to make tension productive, not eliminate it.

**The Monday Five (Weekly Operating Habits)**
Five concrete, low-effort resets to apply each week:
1. Remove one unnecessary meeting (and help others do the same).
2. Talk to one customer — regardless of business model.
3. Reframe one ongoing conflict as progress.
4. Unfollow one source of noise.
5. Create one deliberate moment of enjoyment in the role.

**"What would I still do if nobody paid me?" Enjoyment Audit**
A reflective exercise to identify which parts of the role generate genuine energy. The answer reveals where to invest effort and what to make sure appears at least once or twice a week in the calendar. For Ibhadon, it's cross-functional workshops tackling hard problems collaboratively — the satisfaction comes from the process, not whether the problem gets fully solved.
