# Why is product so hard? Charity Ibhadon (WPP)

Video ID: `vNL0CvWFwx8`

## Summary
Charity Ibhadon, a Product Director at WPP with 15 years of experience, joins the Product Experience podcast to discuss why product management feels harder than ever and how to rediscover joy in the role. The conversation argues that the relentless pressure from layoffs, high public standards, imposter syndrome, and an overload of frameworks and books has stripped fun out of product work — and that reclaiming it is both a personal wellbeing strategy and a professional advantage. Charity draws on her own burnout experience (physical illness, working at a friend's wedding in Slovenia) and recovery (three weeks horse riding in Ecuador) to make the case that product people need to decouple their identity from their job, practice delegation and prioritization, and invest in being genuinely human and enjoyable to work with. The episode is most relevant to product managers and product leaders at any seniority level who are experiencing stress, imposter syndrome, or identity-over-identification with their role.

## Key insights
- **Product is uniquely hard because there is no gold standard.** Unlike medicine, where "I operated, they got better" provides clear feedback, product success is highly context-dependent — budget, company culture, team respect for product all shape what "good" looks like, making it nearly impossible to feel objectively successful.
- **Information overload raises the bar unrealistically.** Charity notes there are ~50 books on product on her shelf alone, and LinkedIn posts claiming "86% of product managers aren't doing XYZ" create a culture of chronic self-doubt regardless of seniority.
- **Product attracts perfectionists.** People drawn to PM roles tend to hold themselves to high standards and want the best outcomes for customers and the business — which amplifies the pressure they put on themselves.
- **Burnout can feel like success.** Charity's pivotal insight: when she was flying New York–LA twice a fortnight, doing an Executive MBA, and managing large teams, she interpreted stress as evidence she was "earning her salary." The physical symptoms (digestive problems, persistent colds, sore throat) only revealed the true cost in hindsight.
- **Being "on" at all times is a burnout signal, not a badge of honor.** Charity describes being on a board call during her close friend's wedding henna ceremony in Slovenia and thinking she was being productive — she now sees it as a clear burnout marker.
- **Recovery doesn't require a dramatic escape, but rest does matter.** Three weeks of horse riding in Ecuador gave Charity her first experience of physical and mental freedom. The practical takeaway: build micro-recovery into regular life — a quieter Friday, a Tate Modern membership used once a month, a five-minute walk outside.
- **It is legitimate to view your job as just a job.** Charity explicitly says this is under-discussed in product circles: your job doesn't have to be your whole identity. She notes she is also a person with friends, family, interests, and that is fine.
- **Job is not life; reframe your mantra.** She suggests repeating: "When I finish this day, I will still be fine, my family will be well, I will still get paid if I do a good job" — not as denial of pressure but as a genuine recalibration of stakes.
- **"We're not building rockets."** If a feature doesn't ship today, the world will be fine. This reframe — offered both by Charity and echoed by the host's story of a publisher saying "nobody's going to die" before a CES launch — is a practical stress-reducer.
- **Being fun and human to work with is a strategic professional advantage.** The most effective senior leaders Charity has worked with (CEO/COO/CFO level) have been the most human. Niceness and likability, when paired with competence, outperform credentials in relationship-heavy work like product.
- **Product is fundamentally about relationships.** Because PM work spans design, engineering, data, and business, being enjoyable to work with, able to disagree professionally, and capable of healthy tension makes you more effective — not just more pleasant.
- **Healthy tension and professional disagreement are underrated skills.** Knowing how to push back on a delivery manager, designer, or engineer from a place of understanding their role gives you confidence and autonomy at the table rather than passively waiting to be told what to do.
- **Commercial mindset will matter more than AI certificates.** Charity predicts that the PMs who thrive long-term will understand the company's commercial context — share price, revenue drivers, growth levers — and connect their slice of the product to those outcomes. Generic frameworks and AI credentials are secondary.
- **Resilience is about tolerating ambiguity, not working harder or smarter.** The ability to sit with uncertainty and ambiguity is the core skill for longevity in product.
- **Watch how unflappable colleagues handle chaos and copy their techniques.** Charity describes a Director of Engineering at Just Eat who walked around smiling through enormous team drama, telling her "PMA — positive mental attitude." Observing and borrowing coping strategies from calmer colleagues is a legitimate development tactic.
- **Individuality is being squeezed out of product.** The proliferation of books, frameworks, and prescriptive "you must do X" content leaves little room for individual approaches. Charity believes this homogenizes the craft and reduces confidence in people's own judgment.

## Use cases
- **PM experiencing imposter syndrome** who feels they don't measure up to LinkedIn advice or book-prescribed standards.
- **Mid-to-senior product leader managing a large team** who is at risk of or recovering from burnout and needs frameworks for energy management.
- **Product person re-entering the workforce** after a layoff who is anxious about job searching while also worried about their identity outside of work.
- **PM transitioning into a leadership role** who needs to learn how to project calm under pressure to avoid a "panic domino effect" in their team.
- **Any product professional** who over-identifies with their job title and needs permission to invest in life outside work.
- **Team leads or CPOs** who want to make their teams more resilient and engaged without resorting to burnout-inducing standards.
- **PMs preparing for long-term career planning** who are evaluating which skills (commercial acumen vs. framework knowledge vs. certifications) to invest in.
- **Product managers in chaotic or high-pressure environments** (rapid scaling, board-level scrutiny, reorganizations) who need concrete de-escalation and prioritization tools.

## Patterns & frameworks

**Eisenhower Matrix**
A prioritization tool Charity uses regularly. Tasks are plotted across four quadrants: Do now (urgent + important), Schedule (important but not urgent), Delegate (urgent but not important), Delete (neither). The key insight Charity emphasizes is the delegation and deletion quadrants — not dumping work on a team, but genuinely asking "does this need to happen today, and does it need to be done by me?" Reduces the accumulation of one-more-thing pressure that leads to 9–10 pm exhaustion.

**Happy High Status (book-based framework)**
Recommended by a former boss. The core idea: when your world feels like it's falling apart, maintain an outwardly calm, confident, "unflappable" presence. As a leader, your team reads your energy — panic is contagious, so projecting calm creates a stabilizing effect even when internally stressed. Charity describes it as the duck: legs paddling furiously underwater, gliding serenely on the surface.

**Reject–Learn–Reframe loop**
Charity's personal approach to handling rejection (job hunting, product failures, setbacks): treat each "no" as data about what experience or skill to develop next, then re-enter with that gap addressed. Avoids framing every problem as an opportunity (she explicitly calls that out as hollow) but does ask: "What is within my control, and what can I act on today?"

**Micro-recovery anchors**
Not a named framework but a repeatable pattern: identify one or two things you enjoy outside work (for Charity: art galleries, horse riding when traveling) and schedule them as non-negotiable. The logic is that if work is your whole life, one monthly Tate Modern visit can reset your baseline. Small, consistent, low-effort — not "quit and go to Bali."

**PMA (Positive Mental Attitude) as a daily operating principle**
Borrowed from a Director of Engineering colleague. The practice: even amid chaos, consciously choose to project and internalize a positive mental attitude. Paired with the Eisenhower Matrix for task control, it provides both an emotional anchor and a practical prioritization system for leaders under pressure.
