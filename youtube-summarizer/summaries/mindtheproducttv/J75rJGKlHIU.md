# How to get the most out of coaching in product

Video ID: `J75rJGKlHIU`

## Summary
This episode of the Product Experience Podcast features hosts Randy Silver and Lily Smith interviewing each other about coaching in product management and business. They cover how each became involved in coaching, what makes coaching effective, when to start and stop coaching relationships, and how AI tools are beginning to intersect with human coaching. The central argument is that effective coaching is not about giving answers but about holding space, asking the right questions, and helping people surface insights they already possess. It is most relevant to product managers, leaders, and anyone considering either hiring a coach or becoming one.

## Key insights
- **Coaching starts from imperfect beginnings:** Randy started coaching "badly and by accident" when someone external asked him to be their coach before he had ever received good coaching himself. Lily had formal training via a 12-week introduction course and triad coaching in a previous company.
- **Coaching vs. mentoring distinction:** Pure coaching avoids giving answers; it reflects questions back to the coachee. Randy explicitly positions himself as a hybrid — part coach, part mentor — and is transparent about this with clients. Most product professionals want that hybrid, not strict coaching.
- **The coachee does the work:** Lily's first coaching session was a wake-up call — she expected to vent and have problems solved, but the coach immediately redirected to "what are *you* going to change going forward?" This forward-looking orientation was initially jarring but ultimately far more valuable.
- **Preparation matters for 1-on-1 coaching:** Spending just 5–10 minutes before a session asking yourself "What's bothering me? What's frustrating me? What's stopping me right now?" is recommended. Walking in cold wastes time finding the topic.
- **Symptoms vs. root causes:** Sessions rarely end where they begin. A presenting problem is often a symptom masking a deeper root cause, and good coaching works through layers to find what is really driving the issue.
- **The optimistic stance (Gestalt):** Rather than telling someone they are wrong (which triggers defensiveness), you highlight what they are doing *well* — even in a dysfunctional dynamic. This helps people organically notice what is not working without being put on the defensive. Lasting change comes from self-noticing, not external criticism.
- **Triad coaching as a low-cost organizational tool:** Three people rotate through coach, coachee, and observer roles. It requires no professional coach — just good attitude, time, and executive sponsorship. Randy ran an intro workshop at a conference using the Liberating Structures Troika Consulting model and found it highly effective even with zero-experience participants.
- **Gestalt team coaching is observational and emergent:** Randy completed a diploma in Gestalt-based team coaching. Unlike 1-on-1 coaching, it is nondirective, involves pairs or groups, and focuses on noticing patterns and behaviors that emerge naturally in the room. The coach stays outside the system to observe it.
- **Leadership teams exhibit "toddler and teenager" behaviors:** Primal needs — wanting to be heard, unmet wants — show up in executives just as they do in children, only expressed differently. Gestalt training helps coaches see and address these without confronting them head-on.
- **When to end a coaching relationship:** It can wrap up when the immediate issue is resolved, when the coachee is in a comfortable place, or when a different coach would better serve new needs. Relationships can also extend for years if new challenges keep emerging.
- **"Unstuck sessions" for ad hoc coaching:** Randy offers one-off sessions for people who are stuck but cannot admit uncertainty to their boss. The insight: the person almost always already knows the answer but needs a safe, non-judgmental space and the right questions to surface it.
- **AI for coaching — useful adjunct, not replacement (yet):** Lily successfully prompted ChatGPT to respond in a coaching style ("only ask me questions, give no answers") and found it genuinely helpful. Randy uses an AI chief of staff (combining Dave Colleen's "Dex" and Lean Dharmad's "RunOC") in Slack for daily check-ins on priorities and end-of-day recalibration. Both agree AI cannot yet replicate holding space, reading body language, or human connection.
- **AI tools to support human coaches:** Transcription tools (Otter, Granola) allow coaches to be fully present during sessions and review trends and action items afterward. Some coaches are experimenting with bot versions of themselves, though Randy is skeptical — advice is contextual, and a "RandyBot" cannot account for what has changed in the relationship since the last real conversation.
- **The value of silence in coaching:** Drawing on a story about celebrity journalist Taffy Brodesser-Akner interviewing an AI actress, the hosts note that a key human coaching skill is sitting with silence until the other person fills it. AI does not feel the awkward pull to fill silence — it simply waits — which removes a powerful mechanism for drawing out deeper responses.

## Use cases
- **Individual contributors or managers** considering hiring a coach for the first time and unsure when or how to start.
- **Product leaders stepping into new roles** who need structured support through a transition period.
- **Managers who want to coach their teams** but lack formal training — triad coaching offers a practical, low-cost starting point.
- **People feeling "stuck"** in a leadership situation where they cannot openly admit uncertainty to their boss — ad hoc "unstuck sessions" are a fit.
- **Organizations wanting to build a coaching culture** without a large budget — peer-based triad programs with executive sponsorship are actionable.
- **Coaches and managers** who want to avoid triggering defensiveness when addressing behavioral issues — the optimistic stance is directly applicable.
- **Anyone using AI tools for productivity or reflection** — the daily check-in / end-of-day recalibration use case is practical and low-effort to replicate.
- **Coaches interested in augmenting their practice with AI** — transcription tools for presence and post-session analysis are a near-term, low-risk application.

## Patterns & frameworks

**Coaching vs. Mentoring Hybrid**
A spectrum rather than a binary. Pure coaching = only questions, no answers, the coachee directs everything. Pure mentoring = sharing experience and advice. The hosts advocate an explicit, transparent hybrid for most product/business contexts, where the coach shares relevant experience while still keeping ownership of decisions with the coachee.

**The Optimistic Stance (Gestalt)**
Instead of pointing out what someone is doing wrong (which triggers defensiveness), name and affirm what they are doing *well*, even within a broken dynamic. This creates psychological safety and prompts the person to self-notice gaps without being told. Rooted in Gestalt coaching methodology.

**Triad Coaching / Troika Consulting (Liberating Structures)**
Three participants rotate through three roles in each session: coachee (presents a real challenge), coach (asks questions), observer (watches the dynamic and gives feedback on the coaching). No professional coach needed. Cycles every session so everyone experiences all roles. Liberating Structures provides a free, structured version of this called "Troika Consulting."

**Gestalt Team Coaching**
A group/pair coaching method that is nondirective and emergent. The coach stays outside the "system" (the group) to observe behavioral patterns as they arise naturally. Rather than directing toward a specific outcome, the coach helps participants become mindful of their own behaviors and their effects. Particularly powerful for leadership teams.

**The "Unstuck Session"**
An ad hoc, single-session format for leaders who are stuck on a specific problem and need a confidential, non-judgmental sounding board. The goal is not a multi-session program but a focused 60–90 minutes to surface what the person already knows but has not yet admitted to themselves.

**Daily AI Check-in Loop**
A lightweight accountability structure using an AI agent: (1) morning prompt — review agenda, state priorities and intentions; (2) evening prompt — what actually got done, what changed, recalibrate. Acts as a structured self-coaching rhythm without a human coach, useful for reducing "shiny object" distraction and maintaining focus on stated business priorities.

**Session Preparation Framework**
Before any 1-on-1 coaching session, spend 5–10 minutes answering: *What's going on? What's bothering me? What's frustrating me? What's stopping me right now?* This is a starting anchor, not necessarily the final topic — but it prevents wasted time and enables a faster descent to meaningful work.
