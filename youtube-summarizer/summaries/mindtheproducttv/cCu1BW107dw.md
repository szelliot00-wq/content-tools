# How the NHS innovates — Ben Cook (Deputy Director of Product, NHS)

Video ID: `cCu1BW107dw`

## Summary
Ben Cook, Deputy Director of Product at NHS England, reflects on what product management looks like at massive scale in a life-critical public institution. Using the NHS app — the digital front door to England's health system used by 30% of the country monthly — as his primary example, he contrasts startup product work with the complexity of a 1.5-million-person organization bound by legacy tech, clinical safety requirements, and fragmented stakeholders. His core argument is that innovation in large, constrained institutions is possible but requires three specific adaptations: deeply understanding every stakeholder's definition of value, front-loading feasibility and viability validation, and refusing to let complexity shrink your ambitions. The talk is most relevant to product managers working in large enterprises, regulated industries, healthcare, or any organization with significant technical debt and multi-stakeholder environments.

## Key insights

- **The "saving lives" reframe:** Cook's mentor's advice — "don't worry, we're not saving lives" — became darkly ironic when they reunited at the NHS, where the stakes are literally life and death. This sets the tone: the same product skills apply, but the consequences of failure are categorically different.

- **NHS app scale:** 70% of eligible people in England have ever used the app; ~30% use it every month. This adoption wasn't organic — it was turbocharged by the pandemic, then rebuilt around genuine recurring use cases.

- **Three user groups, not one:** NHS product decisions consider (1) patients, (2) the 1.5 million NHS frontline workers (40% suffering workplace stress, 30% took a mental health day in the past year), and (3) the system itself (efficiency, safety, equity within a £240 billion annual budget).

- **The 10-year health plan's three shifts:** analog → digital; sickness → prevention; hospital → community. The NHS app appears 42 times in this government document, with many commitments due by 2028, not 2035 — creating real product urgency.

- **Three near-term features Cook is most excited about:**
  - **AI triage in the app:** Trials in Australia showed putting AI at an A&E front door diverts ~30% of patients to faster, more appropriate care. In live NHS pilots, ~40% of people contacting their GP are being redirected to pharmacy, remote messaging, or self-care — reducing front-line pressure.
  - **Virtual hospital (NHS Online):** Opt-in only. An online-only hospital for conditions that don't require physical examination. Projected to deliver 8.5 million additional appointments in the first three years, freeing physical hospital capacity for those who truly need it.
  - **Home-based disease screening:** Targeting eradication of cervical cancer by 2040 and zero HIV transmissions by 2030. App-ordered home test kits, guided results, and next-step advice to reach population-level screening coverage that clinic-only models can't achieve.

- **Innovation blockers specific to large institutions:** Legacy technology (no single replacement strategy possible), organizational fragmentation (NHS is hundreds of semi-autonomous trusts, not one entity), designing for everyone including complex-needs users, mandatory clinical safety sign-off on every feature, and unexpected political stakeholders — illustrated by the surreal experience of watching the Prime Minister announce your product roadmap on Sky News.

- **Heritage organizations carry decades of accumulated constraint:** IBM, Air New Zealand, British Airways, and the NHS all share a pattern: modern digital interfaces layered over decades of accumulated tech debt, cultural process, and founding-era assumptions. Air New Zealand's legacy systems date to when that server rack was the world's most advanced computer. NHS founding principles were designed in a post-war rationing environment. You cannot innovate effectively without understanding that history.

- **Stakeholder value misalignment is the hidden product killer:** Cook's airline example: optimizing for conversion rate increased leisure bookings, which the commercial director hated because leisure customers book earlier and pay less than business customers. Then selling more bags angered the operations director who couldn't handle the volume at Terminal 5. The same feature can simultaneously "succeed" by one metric and fail three stakeholders.

- **Immersive stakeholder presence beats interviews:** Cook spent hours sitting in British Airways' Terminal 5 baggage halls just watching process flow. Staff travel privileges meant he experienced lost luggage firsthand — building genuine empathy for why operations staff push back on product decisions. This unlocked insights no structured interview would have surfaced, and genuine relationships where people drop their guard.

- **Feasibility and viability sink products more often than usability:** In constrained environments, the "sweet spot" of desirability, feasibility, and viability is much harder to find because so many technical and operational constraints shrink the viable solution space. Is the Wi-Fi in that hospital ward good enough? Will the frontline actually adopt it in their workflow? Will it get deployed sustainably?

- **Shift feasibility and viability testing left:** Rather than discovering blockers late, NHS product teams front-load clinical, technical, and operational validation in discovery — alongside user research. High-fidelity AI-generated prototypes now make it faster to test data flows and edge cases early.

- **Think big even inside a constrained system:** A 1% efficiency improvement on a £240bn budget is enormous — but if that's all you aim for, you're leaving transformational impact on the table. Cook frames incremental work as "the runway and the ROI," but argues the mission demands also pursuing radical, life-changing outcomes.

## Use cases

- **Product managers at large enterprises or public sector organizations** who feel innovation is impossible due to politics, legacy systems, or bureaucracy.
- **PMs entering regulated industries** (healthcare, finance, aviation) who need to understand why standard discovery practices need adaptation.
- **Product leaders managing multi-stakeholder environments** where different teams have fundamentally different definitions of success.
- **Anyone planning a digital transformation program** in an organization with significant technical debt and a fragmented operating model.
- **Teams building health tech or govtech products** who need to understand clinical safety review processes and frontline adoption dynamics.
- **PMs struggling with stakeholder buy-in** who are treating stakeholder management as interview-based rather than immersive.
- **Product teams over-indexed on usability testing** who haven't sufficiently validated feasibility and viability before investing in build.
- **Senior PMs debating incremental vs. transformational product strategy** — the "runway vs. moonshot" tension.
- **Founders or PMs transitioning from startups to larger organizations** and adjusting expectations about speed, process, and politics.

## Patterns & frameworks

**1. Three-user-group model**
Rather than optimizing solely for the end user (patient), NHS product teams explicitly consider three constituencies in every decision: the patient/user, the frontline workforce, and the wider system (cost, efficiency, equity). Useful in any context where the person paying, the person using, and the person operating a product are different groups with different incentives.

**2. Three-shift strategic framing (analog→digital, sickness→prevention, hospital→community)**
A government-level strategic framework that cascades into product roadmaps. The lesson: large-scale product work in public institutions is often explicitly tied to published policy documents. Understanding those documents is a prerequisite for prioritization and stakeholder alignment.

**3. Immersive stakeholder embedding (beyond interviews)**
Instead of scheduled stakeholder interviews, Cook advocates physically spending time in stakeholders' environments — baggage halls, hospital wards, terminal floors — to observe real constraints and build genuine relationships. The pattern: spend time where they work → observe real problems → build empathy → earn trust → get unfiltered insight → inform opportunity mapping.

**4. Opportunity Solution Tree / Impact Mapping (referenced, not explained)**
Cook name-checks these standard product frameworks but makes the point that they only work if you've done the immersive stakeholder work first. An opportunity solution tree built without understanding all stakeholder constraints will miss key opportunities or create solutions that fail on adoption.

**5. Shift-left feasibility and viability validation**
NHS discovery practice deliberately moves clinical safety review, technical feasibility checks, and operational viability testing earlier in the discovery phase — running in parallel with user research rather than after it. The pattern: don't build to the point of high fidelity before checking "can this actually run in this environment?" Use vibe-coded/AI-generated prototypes to test data flows and edge cases cheaply and early.

**6. Desirability / Feasibility / Viability sweet-spot model**
Cook references the classic three-circle product framework but specifically argues that in heavily constrained institutions, the feasibility and viability circles are much smaller than in a startup, so the overlap region (where viable products live) is much harder to find — and requires proportionally more effort to locate.

**7. Incremental ROI as runway, not ceiling**
A prioritization philosophy: small efficiency gains on large budgets generate significant returns and fund the organization's appetite for bigger bets. But they should be treated as runway — the thing that keeps the lights on and builds credibility — while simultaneously pursuing transformational initiatives that justify the institution's mission.
