# Why your AI strategy is failing - Barry O'Reilly (Author, and Founder of Nobody Studios)

Video ID: `sFe3_iZb6ng`

## Summary
Barry O'Reilly, founder of Nobody Studios (an AI-focused venture studio), argues that most AI transformations fail because organizations focus on tools rather than behavior change. Drawing on six years of building AI companies and coaching executives, he presents a framework for how individuals and organizations can unlock genuine value from AI by changing *how* they work — not just *what* they use. The video is most relevant to product managers, executives, team leaders, and anyone trying to move beyond surface-level AI adoption toward measurable business outcomes.

## Key insights
- **85% of Gen AI projects fail, 83% of digital transformations fail** — the root cause is treating AI as a tool problem rather than a behavior change problem.
- **"Productivity flex" is noise, not signal.** Social media posts about AI tool usage never tell you if the output was actually good — they're marketing, not evidence.
- **Start with your natural work style, not the tool.** Barry's own breakthrough came from realizing his best content creation mode was *talking*, not typing — so he used transcription to create book chapters in 2 hours instead of a month.
- **Every conversation is a data asset.** Capturing and transcribing meetings compounds over time — Barry built 7 years of coaching conversations into a personal queryable knowledge base.
- **Decision velocity and decision advantage** are the leading metrics that matter — how fast can you make decisions, and how good is the information supporting them? These are more predictive than lagging metrics like time saved or money saved.
- **Only 12–18% of companies are succeeding with AI, and even those see less than 5% profit improvement** — the ROI is still marginal relative to spend, which is why CFOs are skeptical.
- **AI transformation is not the CTO's job alone** — it affects every department (finance, HR, legal, operations), so the entire executive team must role-model behavior change.
- **Psychological safety is a prerequisite.** Progyny CEO Pete Aviensky's directive — "elevate people, not eliminate people" — created the safety employees needed to experiment without fear of training their own replacement.
- **The Harvard/P&G study (776 projects):** Individuals with AI approach team-level output. But *teams paired with AI are 3x more productive* in ideation quality. This makes strategy development a team sport.
- **Thinking time is a productivity asset, not slack.** As admin work shrinks, the goal isn't to fill capacity with more hard tasks — it's to create space for slow, deep thinking (System 2, per Kahneman). Bezos called it "puttering."
- **LLMs are designed to agree with you.** The product managers behind these tools are incentivized to keep you using them — so the tool will tell you the answer you want to hear if you ask for answers. The antidote is asking it to *challenge* your thinking instead.
- **Judgment is the irreplaceable human skill.** Using AI without exercising your own judgment erodes it — like a muscle that atrophies. You must actively practice making decisions under uncertainty.
- **Capability = knowledge + experience + judgment** (framework from Jim Highsmith, one of the original Agile Manifesto signatories). Knowledge comes from reading and learning; experience from making choices with real consequences; judgment from pattern recognition in uncertain conditions.
- **Distribution matters more than ever.** When everyone can build cheaply with AI, the differentiator is marketing and getting your product to the right people — not the product itself.
- **Team size reduction is often premature.** Smaller teams can produce output quickly, but output ≠ quality, and excellence in specific roles (sales, marketing, customer support) is still non-negotiable.

## Use cases
- **Executives and CEOs** who want to role-model AI adoption in a way that creates organizational safety and cascading change.
- **Product managers** who need to justify AI investment with leading indicators rather than vague productivity claims.
- **Coaches and consultants** who conduct frequent high-stakes 1-on-1s and want to turn every conversation into a structured, actionable, compounding data asset.
- **Anyone who produces content** and struggles with blank-page paralysis — reframe the task around your natural working style first, then find the tool that amplifies it.
- **Strategy and ideation teams** who want to run faster, higher-quality design sprints using AI as a live team member that pressure-tests ideas.
- **CFOs and finance leaders** evaluating whether AI spend is generating real returns — the framework helps set realistic expectations and identify the right metrics to track.
- **Hiring managers** assessing judgment in candidates, particularly for senior or product roles where ambiguity and decision-making under uncertainty are core to the job.
- **Startup founders** deciding how to size and structure teams — avoiding the trap of over-relying on agents to replace key human competencies.
- **Executives struggling with context-switching and cognitive overload** from higher-stakes decision loads — the "thinking time" and decision-preparation practices directly address this.

## Patterns & frameworks

**Capture → Transcribe → Synthesize → Act loop**
A four-step personal workflow. Capture all conversations, ideas, and meetings via voice/transcription. Transcribe automatically. Synthesize with an LLM by asking it to find blind spots, generate scenarios, and challenge assumptions — not produce answers. Act by exercising human judgment to make the final decision. The human owns the last step.

**Decision velocity + decision advantage**
Two leading metrics for AI-augmented work. *Decision velocity* = how quickly decisions are made and progressed (measured by forward movement between meetings, fewer reversals). *Decision advantage* = quality and completeness of information available at decision time. Together they predict downstream business outcomes better than lagging metrics like time or cost saved.

**Elevate, don't eliminate**
A leadership communication framework for creating psychological safety during AI adoption. Before asking employees to experiment with AI, publicly commit that the purpose is to free them for higher-order work — not to replace them. This unlocks participation and prevents defensive behavior.

**Knowledge + Experience + Judgment (Highsmith's Capability Model)**
A framework for defining and hiring for capability. Knowledge = what you've learned. Experience = decisions made in high-stakes, consequential scenarios. Judgment = pattern recognition and decision-making under uncertainty. All three must be cultivated deliberately; judgment in particular must be exercised or it erodes.

**AI as challenger, not answer machine**
A mental model for how to prompt LLMs productively. Instead of asking "what should I do?", ask "what's the blind spot in this idea?", "what would a skeptical CFO say?", or "give me four scenarios where this fails." This forces the tool into an adversarial/Socratic role that strengthens your own thinking rather than substituting for it.

**Teams + AI = 3x ideation quality (Harvard/P&G framework)**
Based on a 776-project P&G study. The finding reframes strategy as a team sport: don't let an executive develop strategy alone with AI and hand it down. Instead, bring the team together, surface the AI-refined idea, and collectively use AI to stress-test it. This multiplies output quality and builds team ownership simultaneously.

**5-15-30 system** (mentioned but not detailed — covered in the book)
A structured personal productivity framework Barry references for getting started with AI-augmented work habits. Listeners are directed to the book for the specifics.
