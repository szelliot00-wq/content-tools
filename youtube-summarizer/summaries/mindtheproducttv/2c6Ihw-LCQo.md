# How to Prepare Myself to Become a Product Leader | Petra Wille | ProductTank Cologne

Video ID: `2c6Ihw-LCQo`

## Summary
Petra Wille, a product leadership coach, delivers a talk at ProductTank Cologne aimed at individual contributor (IC) product managers who aspire to move into product leadership roles. Her core argument is that the skills required for product leadership — saying no effectively, giving meaningful feedback, and growing PM mastery — can and should be practiced deliberately before you ever get the title. She presents a 12-responsibility model of product leadership as both a self-development roadmap and a tool for evaluating (and managing up to) your current boss. The talk is most relevant to senior ICs preparing for their first leadership role, though she notes it also offers calibration value for those already in leadership.

## Key insights
- **Product leadership = managing product people**, which Wille defines as primarily product managers, but potentially also product designers, data analysts, and occasionally engineers depending on the org.
- **The shipyard metaphor** (borrowed from Red Fern, ex-CPO at LinkedIn): if product teams build ships, leaders build and maintain the shipyard — focusing on resources, team topology, growth timing, and organizational conditions rather than the product itself.
- **12 leadership responsibilities** span three domains: *People* (role clarity, team building & development, cultural advocacy, networking support, conflict management, empowerment through trust/autonomy), *Product* (directional clarity, product success/goals, peer & executive alignment, integrity & ethical leadership), and *Ways of Working* (product operating model, resources & support, feedback culture & recognition).
- **Leaders don't have to do all 12 things themselves** — but they are accountable for ensuring each responsibility is addressed and sorted by someone.
- **Emphasis among the 12 varies by context**: early-stage startups spend more time on people and product; large orgs in transformation spend more on ways of working.
- **Saying no without a framework feels personal** — if stakeholders can't see the structure behind a refusal, they experience it as a personal rejection rather than a principled decision.
- **The Decision Stack** (Martin Ericsson) is Wille's recommended "no" framework: Vision → Strategy → Objectives/Goals → Epics/Initiatives → Principles. Any request that can't be connected to a current objective or strategic layer can be declined by tracing the stack and showing the gap.
- **The hardest link in the Decision Stack** is connecting objectives back to strategy — teams can usually articulate their goals, but typically cannot explain how those goals make the strategy real. This is a common organizational failure point.
- **Saying no in leadership is harder than in IC roles** because the leader's refusals set the ceiling for what the entire team feels empowered to refuse.
- **The SBI framework** (Situation–Behavior–Impact, from the Center for Creative Leadership) is Wille's recommended structure for giving critical feedback: describe the observable situation, the specific behavior, and the impact it had (on you, the team, or the work). Suggestions are optional and depend on relationship depth.
- **Praise liberally and specifically** — positive feedback (even small: "thanks for putting the cups in the dishwasher") builds a feedback relationship that makes critical feedback land better when it's needed.
- **Giving your boss feedback** using the 12 responsibilities as a blueprint is the "bonus level" — it demonstrates advanced feedback skill and also functions as a form of productive managing up.
- **Behavioral feedback is harder to give than situational feedback** — a distinction explored in depth in Julie Sue's book *Connect*.
- **Radical Candor** is positioned as more relevant once you're in a leadership role and responsible for performance management, not in the IC stage.
- **PM self-assessment** is recommended when your boss lacks a clear development framework — Wille maintains a blog post listing all publicly accessible PM assessments, which function as self-coaching blueprints.
- **The Future Self Canvas** is a lightweight development planning tool: document your current painful "as-is" scenario, describe the desired future state in concrete terms (e.g., fewer prioritization arguments, stakeholders understanding your rationale even if they disagree), identify actions to close the gap, and set a time frame.
- **Zooming out is a meta-skill for leadership**: ICs are necessarily detail-focused, but to communicate with and eventually function as leadership, you must practice narrating at the macro level — the harbor, not the picnic blanket.
- **Everyone should always have a "next big challenge"** assigned — a deliberate stretch that develops a skill they don't yet have (larger product scope, more public speaking, more strategic initiatives).
- **Impact on company success is the career accelerant**: promotions come from demonstrating measurable impact on the business, which flows from making users more successful.

## Use cases
- **Senior ICs preparing for their first people-management role** who want to build leadership muscle before getting the title.
- **Product managers who struggle to say no to stakeholders** and need a structural, depersonalized way to decline requests.
- **Product managers whose organization lacks a clear product strategy or decision-making hierarchy** — the Decision Stack helps surface and fill those gaps.
- **Anyone who needs to give critical feedback to a peer or direct report** and wants a repeatable, low-risk structure to practice with.
- **ICs who want to manage their boss more effectively** — the 12 responsibilities can be used to both benchmark a boss's performance and frame feedback conversations upward.
- **Product managers whose managers don't provide a clear development framework** — PM assessments and the Future Self Canvas enable self-directed growth.
- **New product leaders who inherited a team they didn't hire** and need to quickly develop feedback and performance management skills.
- **Organizations undergoing transformation or scaling** where product operating models and ways of working need clarification — the 12 responsibilities help identify what's missing.

## Patterns & frameworks

**1. The 12 Product Leadership Responsibilities (Petra Wille)**
A coaching model organizing product leadership into three pillars — People, Product, and Ways of Working — with 12 specific responsibilities distributed across them. Used to assess what a leader should be doing, where they're currently investing time, and what an aspiring leader should practice. Also usable as a rubric to evaluate your current boss.

**2. The Decision Stack (Martin Ericsson)**
A layered hierarchy: Vision → Strategy → Objectives/Goals → Epics/Initiatives → Principles. Each layer acts as a filter for decisions and requests. If a stakeholder request can't be traced back up the stack to a current objective or strategic priority, it's a principled basis for saying no. Also clarifies who "owns" each layer, preventing confusion about where decisions are made.

**3. The SBI Feedback Framework (Center for Creative Leadership)**
Situation–Behavior–Impact. Describe the specific observable situation, the concrete behavior you witnessed, and the impact it had. Optionally add a suggestion, but only when the relationship supports it. Keeps feedback anchored in observable reality rather than interpretation or character judgments.

**4. The Future Self Canvas (Petra Wille)**
A back-of-napkin development planning tool: (1) describe the current "as-is" pain scenario, (2) articulate the desired future state in behavioral/outcome terms, (3) identify specific actions to bridge the gap, (4) assign a time horizon. Used in conjunction with a PM self-assessment to translate a learning headline into a concrete development plan.

**5. The Shipyard Metaphor (Red Fern, ex-CPO LinkedIn)**
If product teams build ships, leaders build and maintain the shipyard. A mental model for distinguishing IC work (building product) from leadership work (creating the organizational conditions for product work to happen well). Useful for reorienting new leaders who try to stay too close to the product.

**6. Zoom Out / Macro Narration (implicit pattern)**
A skill to develop before entering leadership: practice describing your work and its impact at progressively higher levels of abstraction. Leadership stakeholders care about the harbor (strategic direction, outcomes), not the picnic blanket (sprint-level details). Practicing this as an IC makes the transition to leadership communication significantly smoother.
