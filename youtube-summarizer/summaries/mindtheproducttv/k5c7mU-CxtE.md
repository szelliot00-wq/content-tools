# The one thing AI can't automate, and how to use it (Sarah Stone, HubSpot)

Video ID: `k5c7mU-CxtE`

## Summary
Sarah Stone, author of *The Round Table* and a partner ecosystem leader at HubSpot, argues that "relational intelligence" — the ability to understand people beyond the surface — is the one human capability AI cannot automate. Drawing on her experience growing up dyslexic at the literal "round table" in the back of a primary school classroom, she reframes that marginalization as an accidental training ground for the skills that will matter most in an AI-saturated economy: reading the room, being present, and building genuine human connection. Her core thesis is that AI is a powerful amplifier of human capability, but when everyone has access to the same tools, the differentiating moat becomes humanity itself. The video is most relevant to product managers, consultants, sales and partnership professionals, and anyone thinking about hiring, team culture, or how to position themselves in an AI-first workplace.

## Key insights
- **The round table metaphor:** In a 1987 Irish primary school, students were sorted by perceived intelligence — triangle tables (top), square tables (middle), and a round table in the back for struggling learners. Kids at the round table, ignored and unable to learn through rote repetition, entertained themselves by relating to each other — accidentally developing presence, listening, and human connection skills that are now the hardest things to automate.
- **AI is an amplifier, not a replacement:** Sarah explicitly used AI (voice memos transcribed to text) to write her book — something previously impossible for her as a dyslexic person. Her framing: AI is a "super soldier serum." It accelerates what's already there. If you bring nothing human to it, it just does the uninspiring thing faster.
- **Ruthless automation leads to commoditization:** When every competitor automates equally, the tools become table stakes. The only remaining differentiator is the human layer — relationships, trust, judgment, empathy. Companies that automate everything and strip out human interaction are already seeing customer churn they can't explain.
- **Context miners:** Humans are uniquely able to extract rich, unstructured context through natural conversation in a way forms and AI prompts cannot. The "ham sandwich" example: a direct question gets a direct answer; a human conversation uncovers preferences, memories, feelings — richer context that makes AI recommendations dramatically better when fed back into systems.
- **Surface communication vs. emotional context:** What people say (words) is only one layer. AI can transcribe words accurately but misses the energy leaving a room, the raised eyebrow, the Irish "oh I will" (which means "absolutely not"), or the ellipsis that means "I'm annoyed." Human emotional context — understanding how someone *feels*, not just what they said — requires presence and awareness.
- **The nurse story:** During COVID, after giving birth alone in hospital, a busy nurse simply sat quietly beside Sarah without saying anything. That presence — understanding the emotional context of the moment — was the most valuable thing anyone could have done. It was not in any protocol or script.
- **Her father the sergeant:** Rather than just managing a noise complaint about a pub, he listened deeply until he understood the woman wasn't really angry about the pub — she was lonely and worried about her son. He organized a weekly social group for her. The noise complaint disappeared. The real problem was loneliness. This is emotional context applied to problem-solving.
- **Her uncle's haulage business:** He treated deliveries as relationships, not transactions. He understood his customers' families, their business struggles, their bad days. He retained customers far longer than competitors because of human connection capital — and he taught Sarah not to react to what someone says, but to understand the context behind why they're saying it.
- **Connection capital:** Trust built *before* you need it. It compounds over time, enables referrals, and allows difficult conversations to be handled well. In partner ecosystems, the partners who invest in relationships — not just transactions — close bigger deals and earn more upsells.
- **Dave the technical guy:** A recurring character in Sarah's framing — the person who used to be able to hide behind specialized technical knowledge and not need interpersonal skills. AI is eroding that hiding place. Dave now needs to show up with humanity, because the knowledge itself is increasingly replicable by machines.
- **Gen Z vs. older generations on AI:** Sarah observes Gen Z is more resistant to AI adoption while Gen X and millennials are embracing it. Her theory: resilience. Older generations have already survived and adapted to major technological disruptions (e.g., the internet) and have developed an adaptive muscle.
- **Future hiring criteria:** Curiosity, judgment, communication, empathy, and agile learning. Not performance metrics but culture carriers — people who create belonging, connect others across an organization, and move fluidly as internal networkers.
- **Ireland's right-to-human law:** Ireland passed legislation giving citizens the right to interact with a human at financial institutions — evidence of a societal pushback against total automation in high-stakes, emotionally loaded contexts.
- **The 321 system:** A personal relationship-maintenance system mentioned in the book (and picked up by the interviewer) — essentially a structured cadence for staying in touch with key contacts. The interviewer built an AI-powered CRM layer on top of this to surface who he hasn't contacted, whose court the ball is in, and relevant content to share as a non-transactional check-in.
- **Non-transactional check-ins:** The simple act of sending "I saw this and thought of you" creates genuine connection. Humans respond to being thought of. AI can help surface *when* and *what* to send, but the human choice to reach out is what creates the relational moment.
- **Accountability as a human differentiator:** AI doesn't lie awake at night worrying about a mistake. Humans do. Customers and partners respond powerfully to human accountability — someone who genuinely cares about making things right.

## Use cases
- **Product managers** evaluating how to balance automation and human touchpoints in product experiences, especially in B2B or high-trust contexts.
- **Partnership and ecosystem leaders** thinking about how to build and measure relationship quality with partners, not just transactional metrics.
- **Consultants and solo professionals** who depend on referrals and repeat business — particularly the 3-2-1 system and non-transactional check-in patterns.
- **Hiring managers and HR leaders** reconsidering what skills to select for in an AI-augmented workforce (curiosity, empathy, judgment, communication over pure technical specialization).
- **Sales teams** learning when to slow down the efficiency drive and invest in relationship depth that produces compounding returns.
- **Anyone building AI-assisted workflows** who wants to think about how to use automation to *create more space for human interaction*, not eliminate it.
- **Neurodivergent professionals** who may see in Sarah's story that traits historically treated as disadvantages (non-linear thinking, verbal rather than written processing) map well onto AI-era skills.
- **Leaders designing team culture** who want a framework for celebrating relational, culture-carrying contributors alongside high-performers on traditional metrics.
- **Anyone anxious about AI job displacement** looking for a reframe: Sarah argues the human response to job loss would be to invent entirely new human-to-human industries.

## Patterns & frameworks

**Five Signals of Relational Intelligence**
Sarah's core framework from the book. The five signals are:
1. *Awareness* — noticing what's happening in a room or conversation beneath the surface
2. *Presence* — being genuinely there with another person, not distracted
3. *Empathetic structuring* — organizing responses and commitments around what the other person actually needs, including accountability
4. *Connection capital* — the trust and goodwill accumulated over time through consistent, promise-keeping behavior
5. *Humanity* — the irreducible human quality that makes all the others possible

**Five Layers of Human Communication**
A stepped model of how humans understand each other, layered from shallowest to deepest:
1. *Surface communication* — the literal words spoken (what AI captures well)
2. *Emotional context* — how the person actually feels, not just what they said
3. *(Implied further layers)* — the book contains additional layers not fully enumerated in the podcast

**The Round Table Metaphor**
A framing device for the book's thesis: the kids at the "dumb table" in the back of class were learning unstructured, relational, human skills — the exact skills AI cannot replicate. Being sidelined from rote-learning drilled them in reading rooms, listening, and connecting.

**Relational Infrastructure**
Sarah's term for deliberately building relationship systems into how a business operates — treating relationship investment as a strategic asset with compounding returns, not a soft nice-to-have.

**Context Mining**
The practice of using deep human conversation to extract rich, unstructured context that AI systems cannot gather through forms or direct queries. This context, fed back into AI tools, enables far more proactive and personalized recommendations. Humans are the miners; AI is the refinery.

**The Quadrant Model (Ruthless Automation vs. Relational Refuge vs. Amplified Excellence)**
Sarah describes at least three strategic postures organizations can take:
- *Ruthless automation* — maximize AI efficiency, strip out human interaction. Risk: commoditization and customer churn.
- *Relational refuge* — lean entirely on relationships, ignore AI. Risk: inability to scale or compete.
- *Amplified excellence* — combine AI-driven efficiency with deliberate human relationship investment ("trust with tech"). This is the recommended position.

**The 3-2-1 Relationship System**
A personal relationship maintenance cadence from the book — a structured system for tracking and timing check-ins with key contacts, ensuring no important relationship goes cold through neglect. Designed to be layered on top of AI tooling (e.g., a lightweight CRM or AI chief-of-staff system) that surfaces who you haven't spoken to and suggests relevant content for non-transactional outreach.

**AI as Super Soldier Serum**
A mental model (borrowed from Captain America) for how to think about AI tools: they amplify what's already there. A skilled, curious, human-centered person becomes dramatically more capable. Someone with nothing to say just does the uninspiring thing faster. The tool doesn't create the value; it multiplies the human's existing signal.
