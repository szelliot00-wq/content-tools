# A deep dive into the state of product in 2026 — Emily Tate (VP Product)

Video ID: `PV7Z9rePUMQ`

## Summary
Emily Tate (VP Product / ex-MD of Mind the Product) joins the Product Experience podcast for a wide-ranging conversation recorded at a product management conference in 2026. The central argument is that AI has not killed product management — it has made it more important, because building is no longer the bottleneck; deciding *what* to build is. Emily covers the state of AI in product, remote work's impact on team culture, speaking at conferences, innovating inside legacy organizations, and positioning SaaS products in an AI-saturated market. The conversation is most relevant to practicing product managers, product leaders, and anyone working in non-tech-native organizations trying to navigate the AI era.

---

## Key insights

- **Building is no longer the bottleneck — judgment is.** With AI coding tools (e.g., Lovable, Claude Code) making software construction trivially fast, the scarce skill is deciding *what* to build and *why*. Product and design become more valuable, not less.
- **AI is a tool, not a product.** Emily draws a direct analogy to blockchain and big data: if your only moat is "it's AI," you will be replaced by anyone who spins up Lovable over a weekend. The question is how AI helps you serve customers better than you could with regular automation.
- **AI is more like the internet than blockchain.** Unlike previous hype cycles, AI represents a fundamental, permanent shift — but the fundamentals of good product practice still apply. "Sprinkling AI on it" doesn't fix bad positioning or bad product thinking.
- **The productivity gain trap.** When AI boosts productivity, teams naturally feel pressure to produce 3x rather than reclaim time and mental space. Emily flags this as a sustainability risk and argues conferences and communities should actively counter it.
- **Remote work is quietly eroding team fun and psychological safety.** Emily argues that the loss of incidental office moments (informal chats, shared jokes, impromptu music) is creating more conflict in teams, because frustrations accumulate without the social release valve of co-presence. Everything becomes transactional on Slack/Teams calls.
- **Conference value is mostly not the content.** The real ROI of attending is stepping out of day-to-day work to think about your craft, get re-energized, and return with fresh perspective. Emily recommends targeting just 2–3 takeaways from 1–2 talks rather than trying to absorb everything.
- **Stop trying to educate stakeholders about product.** In legacy/non-tech organizations, using product language (discovery, iteration, build-measure-learn) sounds arrogant and alienating. Instead, adopt their language, quietly do the right things, and bring people along on the journey in terms that make sense to them.
- **Practical example of dropping the philosophical battle:** Emily's team wanted to "pilot a read-only product" with ops. Ops pushed back because a read-only version adds no value. The reframe: just build it, then say "we have a new shiny thing, want to take a look?" — identical outcome, zero friction.
- **SaaS is not dead.** The "SaaS apocalypse" argument (why buy software when I can build it in Lovable?) ignores that SaaS companies have cross-market knowledge from hundreds/thousands of customers. An internal build is one team's partial solution; a SaaS product reflects an entire dedicated team's 24/7 expertise.
- **AI-first positioning is a liability outside tech.** In non-tech markets there is significant backlash against AI; positioning yourself as "AI first" can actively repel customers. Many people conflate basic automation with AI and distrust it. Know your audience.
- **Speaking tip: you don't need a novel idea.** The most common blocker is "I have nothing new to say." Emily's counter: your *perspective* and *story* are unique, and your framing might be the one that finally makes a concept click for someone else. Start at a local product meetup or ProductTank.
- **Speak in your own style.** Don't try to copy high-charisma speakers. Some great speakers pace the stage; others stand still and speak quietly. You develop your style by doing it repeatedly.
- **AI hype pendulum prediction.** Emily expects 12–18 more months of heavy AI conversation, followed by a "throw stuff at the wall / move fast and break things" phase with many terrible products, before the pendulum settles back toward a balanced middle ground. She does not expect a return to pre-AI norms.
- **Team ratios are unknown.** The old "7 engineers : 1 PM : 1 designer" ratio is obsolete, but nobody knows yet what the new ratio will be — 2:1, 3:1, or unchanged. This uncertainty is part of why the industry is still figuring things out.
- **Book recommendation — *Incorruptible* by Eric Ries.** Argues that standard corporate governance structures (IPOs, shareholder primacy) are designed to extract value from companies and hollow them out. Companies like REI, Patagonia, and John Lewis (employee-owned) have governance structures that resist this. Emily found it "simultaneously inspiring and depressing."

## Use cases

- **Product managers in legacy/non-tech orgs** trying to introduce modern practices without triggering resistance from operations or leadership.
- **Product leaders benchmarking team structures** in an AI-augmented world, unsure what the right PM-to-engineer ratio should be.
- **SaaS founders or PMs worried about the "just build it with AI" objection** from prospects — needs a clear positioning reframe around cross-market expertise.
- **Product managers feeling burnout pressure** to produce more because AI made them faster, who need permission to reclaim the productivity gain.
- **First-time or aspiring conference speakers** who feel they have "nothing new to say" and don't know where to start.
- **Remote-first team leads** noticing rising interpersonal conflict and declining morale with no obvious cause — the remote-work / lack-of-fun connection is worth examining.
- **Product people attending their first conference** who feel overwhelmed — the 2–3 takeaways framework helps them calibrate expectations.
- **PMs pitching AI features internally** who need to explain why "AI is just another technology" and why customer-centric thinking still governs which AI features to build.
- **Founders or executives in regulated, complex, or politically constrained organizations** (e.g., NHS, veterinary, financial services) looking for inspiration on driving innovation despite institutional friction.

## Patterns & frameworks

**1. Building is the bottleneck → Deciding is the bottleneck**
A mental model shift: pre-AI, execution speed constrained output. Post-AI, the constraint moves upstream to judgment — knowing what to build, for whom, and why. Implication: invest more in discovery, strategy, and customer understanding, not more engineering capacity.

**2. AI = Technology, not product (the "blockchain test")**
A heuristic: ask "what problem does the AI solve for my customer?" If you can't answer that without saying "it's AI," your moat is zero. Applied like the blockchain analogy — strip the buzzword and ask if the underlying value proposition survives.

**3. The 2–3 takeaways conference framework**
Rather than trying to absorb every talk, identify 1–2 talks that resonate with where *you* are right now, extract 2–3 points, and spend the next day thinking about them more deeply. The primary value of attending is the mental reset, not content consumption.

**4. Stop winning philosophical battles (the "shiny thing" reframe)**
When introducing product practices to non-product stakeholders: don't explain the methodology, just do the thing and invite people to look. Reframe "we need to run a pilot" as "we built something — want to take a look?" Same activity, zero jargon, zero resistance.

**5. The positioning moat under AI pressure**
For SaaS companies: the defensible moat is cross-market expertise (seeing how hundreds of customers solve the same problem) plus dedicated, continuous product investment. The counter-narrative to "I'll build it myself" is: you'd be building a fraction of what a full team thinks about 24/7.

**6. Audience-aware AI positioning**
A two-segment framework: (a) selling to technologists in enterprise SaaS → AI positioning may help; (b) selling to anyone outside tech → AI-first positioning is often a liability due to backlash and conflation with automation. Know which segment you're in before leading with AI in your messaging.

**7. The speaking progression ladder**
Start at a local meetup → pick one story/nugget that worked → deliver it → build confidence → iterate your style. The goal of early talks is skill development (storytelling, presence), not having a breakthrough idea. Style emerges from practice, not imitation.
