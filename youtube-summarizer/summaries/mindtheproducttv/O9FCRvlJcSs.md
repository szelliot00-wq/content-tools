# Advanced Positioning in the Age of AI: April Dunford at #mtpcon London 2026

Video ID: `O9FCRvlJcSs`

## Summary
April Dunford, author of *Obviously Awesome* and a positioning consultant with 25+ years of experience, argues that while the fundamentals of positioning haven't changed in the AI era, there are now significantly more ways to get it wrong. Drawing on work with 60–70 companies over the past 2–3 years, she covers four specific failure modes and how to address them: lacking a point of view on the future, misidentifying current competitors, hyping too far ahead of where customers actually are, and failing to systematize customer knowledge. The talk is most relevant to product managers, product marketers, and founders building B2B software products navigating rapid market change.

---

## Key insights

- **The fundamentals of positioning don't change with AI.** The five-component framework (competitive alternatives, distinct capabilities, differentiated value, best-fit customers, market category) remains valid — but AI has introduced "a hundred new ways to mess it up."

- **You need a point of view on the future of the market, not just your product.** When markets are in flux, customers freeze purchases because everything looks the same and change feels risky. About half of purchase processes end in no decision. A clear point of view gives customers confidence in your roadmap and helps them decide whether they agree with your direction.

- **Point of view examples from major players illustrate how different these can be:**
  - *Anthropic*: AI models may eventually take over most or all developer tasks — a cautious, B2B-oriented view.
  - *OpenAI*: Intelligence as a utility like electricity or water — a consumer-scale, metered model.
  - *Replit*: SaaS suites become obsolete; agents spontaneously generate the tools you need on the fly.
  - *Microsoft*: Models are commodities; the differentiator is context engineering — and Microsoft owns the context (email, Word, SharePoint, PowerPoint).
  - *Salesforce (Benioff)*: "Our API is the UI" — the back-end data and logic is the real value; buy whatever front end you want.
  - *IBM (Krishna)*: Real-time AI, openness, operational sovereignty, and orchestration — built on the fact that 70% of all transactional data flows through IBM mainframes.
  - *ServiceNow (McDermott)*: Workflows and governance are the whole game; AI intelligence is a commodity; execution and compliance drive adoption.

- **Point of view must be anchored in your actual differentiated value** — it's not invented spin, it's an articulation of why you're building what you're building the way you're building it.

- **Position against who you actually compete with *right now*, not horizon competitors.** In a February 2025 workshop, a CPO insisted their competitor was Replit (a 5-week-old product); the head of sales had never seen it in a single deal. Positioning against a competitor customers aren't comparing you to actively directs their attention toward alternatives they weren't considering, stalling your pipeline.

- **Sell what's on the truck today.** If you position too far in the future, customers say "great, come back in two years" — killing deal urgency. You're taking today's dollars, so you must sell today's value.

- **The product timeline distinction matters:** Every B2B product has a vision (the 10-year dream), the product you actually have now, and a strategy of steps in between. Positioning must reflect the current step, not the destination — and it should be expected to change at each step. (Salesforce started in SMB; they don't go near that market today.)

- **Bridge forward-looking positioning to where customers actually are using maturity models.** One company Dunford worked with sells digital agent technology but their enterprise buyers have zero agents in production and find agents scary. They sell by walking customers through a maturity curve: (1) map and model your processes, (2) apply agents to low-risk, low-hanging fruit with manual oversight, (3) expand, (4) reach full human-agent orchestration. Meeting customers where they are dramatically improves conversion.

- **Internal misalignment on "who do we compete with?" is universal and predictable by role:**
  - *Product*: focused on horizon/future competitors ("who will we compete with in 2 years?")
  - *Marketing*: fixated on whoever spends the most on advertising
  - *Sales*: knows the actual short list but never counts "no decision" / status quo as competition
  - *CEO*: either has a preserved-in-time view from when they ran deals 5 years ago, or is infected by the investor-pitch future-vision lens

- **We are terrible at predicting the future.** Jack Dorsey renamed Square to "Block" in late 2021 going all-in on blockchain. A month later, Zuckerberg renamed Facebook to "Meta" going all-in on the metaverse. Four years later, both bets have largely collapsed. Customer data is the only reliable signal.

- **Most companies don't systematize customer knowledge**, even sophisticated ones. Dunford is repeatedly surprised by how informal and ad hoc it is.

- **Win analysis is underrated.** Most companies obsess over loss analysis, but wins reveal what's actually resonating, who you're really beating, and where your positioning is landing.

## Use cases

- **Product managers** trying to align roadmap decisions with current market realities and communicate direction to stakeholders.
- **Product marketers** developing messaging and positioning for AI-era products where the competitive landscape is shifting rapidly.
- **Founders and CPOs** preparing for customer conversations where "will you still exist in 5 years?" is a real objection.
- **Sales leaders** dealing with high no-decision rates and needing a framework to explain why deals stall.
- **Executive teams** fighting internal battles over who their real competitors are — the maturity model and positioning check-in cadence provide a neutral resolution mechanism.
- **Companies selling to risk-averse enterprise buyers** (banks, insurers, manufacturers, retailers) who are education-heavy and prone to deferring purchase decisions.
- **Any B2B product team** navigating the transition from one competitive set to another (e.g., from legacy/spreadsheet alternatives toward AI-native tools) and unsure how to message during the transition.
- **Companies doing positioning workshops** who need a structured way to run cross-functional alignment sessions.

## Patterns & frameworks

**1. The Five-Component Positioning Framework**
Dunford's core model from *Obviously Awesome*. Defines positioning across: (1) competitive alternatives — what customers would do if you didn't exist; (2) distinct capabilities — features or services competitors lack; (3) differentiated value — the business outcome those capabilities produce; (4) best-fit customers — account characteristics that make them care most about your unique value; (5) market category — the context frame that best communicates your value. Executed sequentially in a workshop setting, often starting with 50–100 raw capabilities and collapsing them into 1–3 value themes.

**2. The "So What?" Drill**
Applied at the distinct capabilities → differentiated value step. For every capability listed, you ask "so what?" — forcing the team to articulate the customer business outcome (revenue, cost savings, risk reduction), not just the feature. Prevents capability lists from becoming 100-item value propositions nobody can remember.

**3. Product Vision / Now / Strategy Ladder**
Mental model for B2B product evolution: the 10-year vision, the product that exists today, and the multi-step strategy connecting them. Positioning lives at the current step only. Useful for preventing premature competitive framing (e.g., don't position against Replit if customers aren't comparing you to Replit yet) while keeping the product roadmap aimed at the right future.

**4. The Point of View (POV) Framework**
A narrative about what the overall market will look like in the future, rooted in your differentiated value. Used to answer the customer question "will you still be relevant in 5 years?" Not product roadmap communication — it's a market thesis. The IBM example is the clearest illustration: their POV (real-time AI, openness, operational sovereignty, orchestration) maps precisely onto capabilities only IBM can credibly claim.

**5. Maturity Model Selling**
A bridging pattern for when your product vision is far ahead of where customers currently are. Draw a staged adoption curve, sell customers on Step 1 (where they are now), and let the later steps create a natural expansion path. Reduces the "this is too scary / too far in the future" objection from risk-averse enterprise buyers.

**6. Operationalized Customer Understanding (Four Mechanisms)**
A set of systematic practices to replace opinion-based internal debates with customer signal:
- *Customer Advisory Board*: Standing group of customers you can poll on strategic questions.
- *Executive Sponsorship Program*: Each exec owns 4 accounts and calls them quarterly — creates deep, relationship-based access to ground-truth perspective.
- *Win/Loss Analysis*: Structured interviews (ideally by a third party) after won and lost deals; win analysis in particular surfaces what's actually resonating.
- *Regular Positioning Check-In*: Quarterly or biannual cross-functional review asking: has anything changed in the competitive landscape? Have we been caught up to on capabilities? Does the positioning still hold? Functions as an early warning system.
