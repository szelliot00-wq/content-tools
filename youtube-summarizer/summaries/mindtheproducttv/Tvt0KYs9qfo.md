# Why 95% of employees can't name their organisation's strategy - Martin Eriksson (The Decision Stack)

Video ID: `Tvt0KYs9qfo`

## Summary
This video features Martin Eriksson, co-founder of Mind the Product and author of the upcoming book *The Decision Stack*, discussing why 95% of employees cannot name their organization's strategy and how his Decision Stack framework addresses this problem. The core argument is that organizations fail not because they lack strategy, but because their strategic layers are disconnected, poorly communicated, or missing entirely — leaving teams unable to make aligned decisions. Eriksson walks through the five fundamental questions every organization must answer, the tools used to answer them, and how to connect them from top to bottom and bottom to top. The conversation covers practical challenges like scaling the framework, handling the "unsexy" work, and fostering empowerment through strategic clarity. This video is most relevant to product managers, executives, team leads, and founders who want to improve strategic alignment and decision-making across their organizations.

## Key insights
- **95% of employees cannot name their organization's strategy**, according to an HBR article Eriksson cites frequently — meaning even a great strategy is useless if it isn't communicated and understood throughout the organization.
- **The Decision Stack answers five fundamental questions:**
  1. Where are we going? (Vision)
  2. How are we going to get there? (Strategy)
  3. What's important right now and how do we measure progress? (OKRs or equivalent)
  4. What actions are we going to take? (Opportunities)
  5. How do we choose between those actions? (Principles)
- **Strategy is the most commonly missing or unclear layer.** Eriksson's go-to diagnostic is asking each executive team member individually what the strategy is — and almost always getting a different answer from each person.
- **Two root causes of strategic failure:** (1) Poor communication — strategy is set but never maintained or updated as teams grow and markets shift; (2) Executive reluctance to make tough choices — many leadership teams prefer optionality ("we can do everything for everyone") over committing to focused bets, which spreads teams thin and leaves no one knowing what's truly important.
- **The "laddering" principle:** From top to bottom, you ask *how* (how do we achieve the vision? → strategy; how do we achieve that? → objectives, etc.). From bottom to top, you ask *why*. When a team member can't answer "why" beyond their immediate goals, that's a clear signal that a strategic layer is missing.
- **Empowerment without context is abandonment.** Eriksson shares a story from one of his first VP of Product roles: when full strategic context was shared with the whole team — including a junior developer three months into the job fresh out of university — that developer immediately identified a solution that delivered 80% of the benefit for 20% of the effort, something the CTO and VP had all missed. This illustrates that strategic clarity unlocks the collective intelligence of the entire team.
- **Strategy timelines depend on business maturity:** Established businesses should have a 5-year-plus strategy; startups should plan as far out as their runway allows (sometimes just 6 months). Regardless of time horizon, **quarterly check-ins on strategy** are essential to validate assumptions and adapt to new information (e.g., the emergence of AI).
- **Strategy is not just about "the next big bet."** A significant portion of organizational effort goes to support tickets, compliance, security, and maintaining existing products. These must be explicitly named in the strategy — including deliberate decisions to *prune* underperforming business lines — or there will always be a gap between what the strategy says and what teams actually do.
- **Principles are the foundation of the stack** and serve as decision-making shortcuts for teams facing recurring trade-offs. Eriksson's example: at Monster (the job board, where he started in 1999), the CEO clarified that "if we build the best experience for job seekers, recruiters have to follow" — instantly eliminating weeks of recurring debate about which user group to prioritize.
- **Principles can be top-down or emergent.** Larger strategic principles often flow from leadership; smaller ones bubble up from teams repeatedly hitting the same decision point. The key is to codify them formally once identified rather than re-debating the same trade-offs endlessly.
- **Communication must be continuous, not a one-time event.** Jeff Weiner's (LinkedIn CEO) quote is cited: *"Once you are sick of saying it, people are starting to hear it."* Organizations often announce a strategy via a 20-page slide deck and never mention it again — this guarantees it won't stick.
- **Co-creation is the best communication.** Involving people from across the organization in building the strategy (or at least having departmental representatives) automatically begins distributing and embedding that strategy more broadly.
- **The "straw man" approach** is a safe, low-threat way for people without authority to surface missing strategic clarity: draft what you *think* the strategy is, share it broadly, and invite challenge — either people agree and you've filled a gap, or you learn something new.
- **The "this or that" game** (credited to Adam War Burton) is a practical exercise for surfacing strategic trade-offs: present binary choices to a team (e.g., retention vs. growth, mobile vs. desktop, new customers vs. recurring revenue) and watch disagreements surface. Eriksson even built a free app for this at **thisorthat.thedecisionstack.com**.
- **The stack becomes "fractal" at scale** — larger organizations develop nested stacks at team and department levels — but Eriksson cautions against giving every single team a full independent vision and strategy, as this creates drift. The right place to split is when the answers to the stack's questions start meaningfully diverging between groups.
- **The biggest mistake when starting out** is trying to build the entire stack at once in a big offsite — instead, Eriksson recommends starting small: fix the layer that's most immediately broken, ensure it ladders up to the next level, and iterate from there.

## Use cases
- **Executive and leadership teams** who have a strategy document that was created once (often at an offsite) and never revisited or communicated, resulting in team confusion and misalignment.
- **CPOs and product leaders** trying to connect product strategy to business strategy and ensure their team's objectives map to a coherent vision.
- **Startup founders** who need to define strategy within the constraints of a limited runway and rapidly changing assumptions.
- **Scale-ups (Series C and above)** with multiple business lines, some growing and some stagnant, who need a structured way to make portfolio-level decisions (including pruning underperforming lines).
- **Product managers and individual contributors** who want to understand how to challenge upward constructively or surface missing strategic clarity without threatening leadership.
- **Cross-functional teams** (marketing, engineering, design, sales) who need shared language and a common framework to align on priorities without constant debate about foundational questions.
- **Organizations introducing OKRs or other goal-setting frameworks** who are finding them disconnected from actual strategy or business vision.
- **Team leads and middle managers** who want to ensure their team's day-to-day work connects meaningfully to organizational goals.
- **Remote or distributed teams** who lack the "wall" of physical artifacts and need intentional processes to keep strategy visible and alive in virtual environments.
- **Consultants and advisors** who need a diagnostic tool to quickly identify which layer of strategic thinking is missing or broken in a client organization.

## Patterns & frameworks

### 1. The Decision Stack
**What it is:** A layered mental model (not a rigid framework) that structures the five core decisions every organization must make, from high-level vision down to day-to-day decision-making principles.
**How it works:**
- **Vision** → Where are we going?
- **Strategy** → How are we going to get there?
- **OKRs (or equivalent)** → What's important right now and how do we measure progress?
- **Opportunities** → What specific actions will we take?
- **Principles** → How do we choose between competing actions?
Each layer is connected: from the top down, you ask *how*; from the bottom up, you ask *why*. When any layer is missing or disconnected, the whole stack breaks down.

### 2. The Laddering Test (How/Why Diagnostic)
**What it is:** A simple diagnostic to identify missing layers in the stack.
**How it works:** Ask "why" repeatedly about any piece of work. If you hit a blank stare at any level, that level is missing or unclear. Similarly, ask "how" from the top down — if a layer can't answer how the one above it will be achieved, the connection is broken.

### 3. The Straw Man Strategy
**What it is:** A bottom-up technique for surfacing or filling in missing strategic clarity without needing formal authority.
**How it works:** Draft your best guess at what the strategy (or any missing layer) should be → socialize it broadly with stakeholders → invite agreement or challenge → refine based on feedback. This avoids the confrontational approach of simply saying "we have no strategy" while still prompting the right conversations.

### 4. "This or That" Trade-off Exercise
**What it is:** A facilitated team exercise for surfacing hidden strategic disagreements and forcing prioritization choices.
**How it works:** Present binary trade-offs relevant to your business (e.g., retention vs. growth, mobile vs. desktop, new customers vs. recurring revenue) — either on a slide or via the free app at thisorthat.thedecisionstack.com — and ask team members to choose. Disagreements reveal misalignment that needs to be resolved at the strategy level before teams run in different directions.

### 5. The Fractal Stack (Nested Stacks at Scale)
**What it is:** A scaling pattern where the Decision Stack replicates at different organizational levels (company → business line → team).
**How it works:** Rather than giving every team a full independent stack, split the stack only where the answers to the five core questions meaningfully diverge between groups. Shared vision and strategy should remain consistent across the organization; objectives and opportunities are where differentiation typically begins at the team level.

### 6. Principles as Decision Shortcuts
**What it is:** Codified, strategic trade-off statements that eliminate recurring debates and empower teams to make consistent decisions without escalating every choice.
**How it works:** Identify recurring decisions where teams keep debating the same trade-off → research and debate the underlying strategic logic → codify the resolution as a named principle → apply it consistently going forward. Example: Monster's "Always optimize for the job seeker; recruiters will follow."

### 7. Continuous Communication Cadence
**What it is:** A practice of embedding strategic context into existing team rituals rather than treating strategy as a one-time announcement.
**How it works:** Integrate references to the stack (vision, strategy, objectives) into all-hands meetings, planning sessions, stand-ups, and quarterly reviews — not as a full strategy session every time, but as a brief, consistent reminder. The goal is to make the strategy feel like the physical wall that co-located teams walk past every day. Guided by Jeff Weiner's principle: *"Once you are sick of saying it, people are starting to hear it."*
