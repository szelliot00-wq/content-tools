# How to build resilience in product - Lindsey Jayne (Product Advisor)

Video ID: `0HZkEj7G12g`

## Summary
This episode of The Product Experience features Lindsay Jayne, then-CPO at the Financial Times, discussing how product managers can build personal and team resilience amid the structural stresses of the role. The core argument is that burnout in product is driven by a structural mismatch: PMs carry full accountability for outcomes but have no direct authority over the people who deliver them, forcing them to operate through influence alone. Lindsay draws on her experience across government (Gov.uk), fintech startups (Monzo), and enterprise media (FT) to offer practical frameworks for stakeholder management, team health monitoring, and career resilience. The episode is most relevant to product managers at all levels — individual contributors learning to influence up, and leaders trying to create healthier team environments.

## Key insights

- **The two-halves rule:** Half the job is building and shipping the product; the other half is *landing* it — making sure people know it happened and understand the context. A product isn't done until it's communicated.
- **The root cause of PM burnout:** PMs have "all the responsibility with none of the power." They are accountable for outcomes they can't directly control, and they often surface organizational friction (e.g., legal/cross-department conflict) only at launch time, making them the bearer of bad news.
- **Two flavors of burnout:** In large non-tech orgs, burnout comes from raging against a machine that doesn't understand product. In VC-fueled scaleups, it came from "ship faster, hire faster"; post-funding-crunch, it shifted to "make money or die." Both are external pressures layered on top of the structural authority gap.
- **Influence is a long game:** Don't try to win a stakeholder over in one meeting. Get curious about what they care about. Meet them where they are. You may need to ship their idea first to build trust before introducing data-driven approaches. It's a marathon, not a sprint.
- **Discovery skills work internally:** The same techniques PMs use for customer discovery — curiosity, listening, not assuming — apply equally to internal stakeholders and partners.
- **Leadership's job is to clear the way:** Senior product leaders must ensure the organization above them understands the value of product in plain language, not tech jargon, and must demonstrate results consistently: "I said we would do this. We did this. Repeat until they're bored of it."
- **Board updates at the FT:** When Lindsay joined the FT, product never updated the board. She instituted at least bi-annual board presentations: here's what we said, here's what we did, what do you need? This built credibility over time.
- **Stakeholder maps — influence vs. interest 2x2:** The classic power/interest grid, reframed as *influence* (how disruptive or shaping could this person be?) vs. *interest* (how invested are they?). High-influence, low-interest stakeholders are the most dangerous — they can sideswipe a project without warning, so communications to them must be tailored to their specific needs.
- **Monitoring burnout at scale:** For a 100-person team, Lindsay uses Pendo (Peon) to survey team sentiment regularly and supplements that with knowing individuals well enough to spot when their resilience is low or they're "not being themselves." Practical interventions include ensuring people take holidays and debriefing after difficult meetings.
- **Passion-driven overwork vs. pressure-driven overwork:** It's fine to lose yourself in exciting work occasionally — the problem is when it becomes a pattern. The signal to watch for is consistent late-night Slack messages. The conversation to have: "It's okay to do this so long as you never *feel expected* to do this."
- **Humans are bad at two things:** Understanding orders of magnitude/scaling, and understanding that life is cyclical — it's not "more, more, more" all the time. Sustainable output looks like a well-tended garden, not a fast-growing but fragile plant.
- **The gardening/plant metaphor:** Think of team members as plants — some grow fast and high but aren't strong; the environment (sun/shade) shapes them. A manager's job is to tend the environment and understand each individual's natural tendencies.
- **When to stop doing day-to-day product work as a leader:** Lindsay's model: group PMs at the FT do not own a product team themselves. But good product leadership still requires knowing what's happening in the work — not just managing people — so teams get the benefit of years of product experience, not just generic management.
- **Product reviews (not retrospectives):** A ritual where a team brings a live problem and work-in-progress (not finished work) to a cross-functional leadership group. The group asks: What options did you reject? What else might you test? Who else should you talk to? This keeps leadership contextually grounded without creating overhead.
- **Design critique as a model ritual:** Design's critique format — "I'm at this stage, I've tried these solutions, I need feedback on X" — is held up as a beautiful knowledge-sharing ritual that product should emulate.
- **The 15-minute dashboard habit:** Start the day with 15 minutes reviewing product analytics dashboards. Getting familiar takes energy; maintaining familiarity is almost effortless once the habit is in place.
- **Operating above your pay grade:** PMs routinely negotiate with people much more senior than themselves. Skills that help: understanding what's on the CEO's desk that morning, knowing the stakeholder's context before entering the room, body language, preparation, and asking for feedback afterward.
- **"Don't die on every hill":** Advice Lindsay received from a founder-CTO. She now runs an internal filter in meetings: "Is this a hill that makes the cut?" This has been her primary personal burnout-prevention tool.
- **The serenity prayer for PMs:** Accept the things you cannot change. You are one person against a system. You'll ship more over a two-year horizon by working with the environment than by blasting through it.
- **Framing difficult exits in interviews:** People extrapolate company failure onto individuals at a deep psychological level. Coach: separate personal wins from company outcomes, and explicitly frame the work done to *make* your job possible (not just what shipped). If conditions were terrible, the story is: "I started in a hole and worked to ground level — here's what that enabled."
- **"We" vs. "I" in interviews:** In cultures that de-emphasize individualism, candidates tell team stories. Interviewers must push past "we did this" to "what specifically was your role?" The framing to aim for: "If I can produce this result in a chaotic context, imagine what I can do in a high-functioning environment."
- **Company-value match as a filter:** Rejections from companies that don't value resilience or the ability to try-and-fail are probably a feature, not a bug — those environments wouldn't have rewarded you anyway.

## Use cases

- **Individual PMs** who feel frustrated by organizational resistance and are tempted to "rage against the machine" — the influence and stakeholder management advice applies directly.
- **PMs preparing for senior stakeholder meetings** (board, C-suite) — the preparation mindset, "what's on their desk today," and plain-language communication advice is immediately actionable.
- **Product leaders managing teams of 5–100+** who want to monitor burnout without creating a surveillance culture — the combination of survey tooling, personal relationship awareness, and environmental design is a usable model.
- **Group PMs or Heads of Product** transitioning from owning a team to managing other PMs — the discussion of when to stop doing day-to-day product work and how to stay substantively engaged is directly relevant.
- **PMs leaving difficult or failed companies** who need to reframe their experience for job interviews — the "started in a hole" metaphor and separating personal contribution from company outcome is practical coaching.
- **Product organizations** wanting to establish better cross-team knowledge sharing — the design critique and product review rituals are ready-to-adopt practices.
- **Aspiring PMs** who are passionate and working excessive hours — the framework for distinguishing healthy enthusiasm from unsustainable patterns is useful for self-monitoring.

## Patterns & frameworks

**Influence vs. Interest Stakeholder 2x2**
A classic stakeholder mapping grid where one axis is *influence* (how much could this person shape or disrupt the outcome?) and the other is *interest* (how invested are they in what you're doing?). The actionable insight: high-influence, low-interest stakeholders are the highest risk — they can kill a project without warning — and require proactive, tailored communication even when they seem disengaged.

**"I said we would / We did" Communication Loop**
A repeating board and leadership communication cadence: state what you committed to, then report back on exactly what you delivered. Repeat every six months until credibility is established. Counters the tendency for product to be seen as "the weird tech people in the corner."

**Product Review Ritual**
A recurring meeting where teams bring live, in-progress problems (not finished work) to a cross-functional leadership group. The format requires presenting the problem, options already rejected, and current approach — then receiving coaching questions rather than directives. Keeps leadership contextually grounded and models the multidisciplinary collaboration that disappears at senior levels.

**The 15-Minute Dashboard Habit**
A daily morning ritual of reviewing all product analytics dashboards. The insight: initial familiarization is high-effort, but ongoing maintenance of that familiarity is nearly effortless. Consistent passive context is more valuable than episodic deep-dives.

**The Hill Filter (Anti-Burnout Mental Model)**
Before speaking up or pushing back in a meeting, run an internal check: "Is this a hill that makes the cut?" Derived from the advice "you don't have to die on every hill." Helps PMs conserve political and emotional capital for the fights that actually matter.

**The Marathon / Serenity Frame**
Two related mental models for working within organizational constraints: (1) product change is a marathon, not a sprint — influence builds over time, not in a single meeting; (2) the serenity prayer applied to product — accept what you cannot change in the organization, and direct energy toward what you can. The argument is that working with the grain of the organization over two years outperforms frontal assault in the short term.

**The Hole-to-Ground-Level Career Narrative**
For PMs exiting difficult or failed organizations: reframe the story not as "we failed to ship X" but as "I entered a situation where the baseline was negative, and here is the work I did to create the conditions for anything to ship at all." This separates individual contribution from company outcome and makes adverse experience legible to hiring managers.

**Discovery Applied Internally**
An extension of customer discovery methods (curiosity, listening without assumption, not trying to solve in one go) applied to internal stakeholders. Treat colleagues and senior leaders as users whose needs must be understood before you can influence them. Meet them where they are; don't lead with your solution.
