# Why the Student Loans Company designs for its hardest users first — Vonny Laing

Video ID: `WVlRJ-w8JQs`

## Summary
Vonny Laing, User Centred Design Lead at the UK Student Loans Company, argues that designing for the hardest, most edge-case users first — rather than the "happy path" — produces better services for everyone, a principle borrowed from inclusive/accessibility design. The interview covers her pyramid model for user segmentation, the case against synthetic users, lightweight field research tactics, and why designers should learn to speak in business terms. It is most relevant to product managers, service designers, and UX practitioners working in complex, high-stakes, or government-adjacent services where the full population must be served rather than a chosen niche.

## Key insights
- **Flip the pyramid**: Most commercial products are designed top-down — core/average users first, edge cases later. Vonny advocates the inverse: design for the most struggling users first and the benefits cascade upward to everyone (analogous to curb cuts and closed captions).
- **Government has no niche option**: The Student Loans Company serves millions of mandatory users — people cannot opt out if they want higher education. Service assessments by peer panels require designers to justify every excluded population, making happy-path-only design impossible to defend.
- **Real gap found in one hour of field research**: A single-day ethnography at a further education college in a deprived area revealed that students on Universal Credit faced a funding gap between their student loan landing and their Universal Credit being suspended — something that never appeared in any system data and was not the original research question.
- **Invisible users are not in the data**: People who fall out of a service, never engage with it, or struggle silently leave no signal. The only way to find them is to physically leave the building and talk to people.
- **Synthetic users are a dangerous shortcut**: LLMs can produce a "credible facsimile" of a person but cannot replicate lived complexity, protected characteristics, or the rapport-dependent disclosures that surface only in real conversations. Synthetic research gives "warm fuzzies" without delivering genuine insight.
- **Synthetic tools have one legitimate use**: They can verify that known problems haven't been reintroduced (akin to test-driven development) but cannot surface unknown problems — that distinction matters.
- **"Eat your greens" — wants vs. needs**: Users often don't want what they actually need (e.g., a 25-year-old not wanting to think about pensions). Responsible design means seeing around the corner for the user and surfacing uncomfortable-but-necessary information at the right moment.
- **Users are people moving through time**: Design must account for who a user is today, in two years, and in five years — not just the current interaction snapshot.
- **Disaster thinking as a design prompt**: Flip the happy path and ask "what is the absolute worst thing that could happen here?" Social media harms, for example, were arguably foreseeable. This reframes risk as a design input rather than an afterthought.
- **Complaint logs and call data are a goldmine**: Users are constantly telling organizations what is broken through support channels; mining those logs for patterns is one of the fastest ways to find systemic problems.
- **Guerrilla research is cheap and fast**: Cancel meetings, set up a trestle table at a university with a box of chocolates, and talk to 10–30 people in a day. The cost is a train fare and sweets. It won't be statistically robust but provides directional signal and can surface critical issues in under an hour.
- **Proxy users from support teams**: Colleagues on support/service desks are excellent proxy users — they hear user emotion firsthand. Involving them early in design reviews (e.g., "what phone call will we get after this screen goes live?") is fast, free, and high-value.
- **Observational insight without talking**: Watching someone use your product at a bus stop with a child in their arms tells you the product must work in a low-data environment, be operable one-handed, and be interruptible. Context of use is invisible without fieldwork.
- **Storytelling over dashboards**: Executives remember stories and verbatim quotes, not numbers. The Barbara Minto Pyramid Principle structure — situation, complication, result, recommendation — is sufficient for a three-page deck that drives decisions without needing an agency.
- **Designers need business language**: Vonny did an MBA specifically to be able to defend design decisions in business terms. Designers who can speak credibly about strategy, finance, and organizational goals become "secret weapons" — they no longer have to retroactively justify design; they embed business thinking into the process from the start.
- **The civil service's peer assessment structure engineers good practice**: The obligation to defend design decisions before trained peer assessors forces teams to think beyond the happy path from the outset — a structural mechanism that private-sector teams lack.
- **Cross-government knowledge sharing**: UK civil service uses a shared Slack and community of practice spanning HMRC, Ministry of Justice, DSTL, and others, effectively giving individual designers tens of thousands of colleagues willing to share patterns and research.

## Use cases
- **Government / public sector product teams** designing mandatory services where the entire population must be served and service assessments require justification of every excluded group.
- **Designers stuck in happy-path thinking** who need a framework to make the case for investing in edge cases and underserved users.
- **Product managers facing stakeholder pressure to ship fast** who need lightweight, low-cost research methods to maintain user contact without lengthy formal studies.
- **Teams evaluating AI/synthetic user tools** who need a clear-eyed framework for what those tools can and cannot replace.
- **Designers seeking executive buy-in** for user research or design changes who need to improve their storytelling and business communication skills.
- **Organizations with large support/service desk operations** who want to extract design signal from existing complaint and call log data.
- **Senior designers hitting a career ceiling** who want to move into executive-level conversations by learning business language.
- **Teams designing services with long user lifecycles** (financial services, education, healthcare) where the user's needs evolve significantly over time.
- **Product teams in financially constrained environments** ("no budget for research") who need to justify guerrilla research as a standard part of the design process rather than a separate budget line.
- **Organizations designing for populations affected by multiple intersecting services** (e.g., welfare, education, healthcare) where seams between services create user-invisible failure modes.

## Patterns & frameworks

**The Inverted User Pyramid**
A four-layer model with core/typical users at the top and underserved/edge-case users at the bottom. The conventional approach designs top-down; Vonny advocates designing bottom-up. "Underserved" means user groups the service could theoretically reach but hasn't; "edge case" means the most disenfranchised, who may never have considered the service relevant to them or who engage under duress. Designing for the bottom layers produces a halo effect that improves the experience for everyone above them.

**"Eat Your Greens" (Wants vs. Needs)**
Borrowed from service designer Vic. Users articulate what they want today; designers must also address what users need over time — the things they don't want to think about yet (e.g., pension auto-enrollment). Operationally: treat the user as a person moving through time, model their future self, and surface uncomfortable-but-necessary information in a managed way rather than waiting for users to ask.

**Disaster Thinking / Inverted Happy Path**
A heuristic for risk-surfacing: take the happy path and flip it. Ask "what is the absolute worst thing that could happen to a user here?" and design backwards from that scenario. Frames risk as a design input rather than a post-launch consideration.

**Short, Sharp, No-Frills (Guerrilla) Research**
Informal, fast field research modelled on Erica Hall's "just enough research." Cancel meetings, go to a relevant location (university, college, service centre), set up informally, use small incentives (chocolate), and talk to 10–30 people in a day. Not statistically robust but reliably surfaces directional signals, critical failures, and unknown user populations. Cost: travel and snacks.

**Continuous Discovery (Dual-Track Agile)**
Moving away from front-loaded discovery toward a model where a dedicated team is always in the field, feeding insights continuously back to multidisciplinary delivery teams — aligned with the Teresa Torres / Triad agile model. Described as an aspiration the Student Loans Company is actively working toward.

**Barbara Minto's Pyramid Principle (for research communication)**
A four-part narrative structure for presenting findings to executives: (1) Situation — what is the context, (2) Complication — what is the problem or tension, (3) Result — what the research found, (4) Recommendation — what to do. Frontloads the most important information, fits in three slides, and is accessible without an agency.

**Proxy User Panel (Support Staff as Designers)**
Involves service desk / support colleagues as proxy users and early design reviewers. Because they absorb user emotion and complexity daily, they can predict failure modes ("what phone call will we get?") faster than any synthetic method. Recommended both as an internal research resource and as a source of systemic pattern recognition when combined with complaint log analysis.

**Service Assessment as Quality Engineering**
The UK government's mandatory service assessment process — where design decisions must be defended before a trained peer panel — is framed not as bureaucratic box-ticking but as a structural mechanism that forces teams to design for the full population. The knowledge that you will have to justify exclusions in public engineers inclusive thinking from the start.
