# How to lead when you don't fit in - Dave Martin (CPO, Fractional)

Video ID: `jcccW-wfL9Y`

## Summary
This episode of the Product Experience podcast features Dave Martin, a CPO with over two decades of product leadership experience, discussing how neurodivergent leaders can thrive without masking who they are. Dave — who is dyslexic, ADHD, and likely autistic — argues that neurodivergent professionals face a uniquely high cognitive cost to correct communication drift, not a unique set of problems. He introduces the CALM framework and a practical "Signal Prep" tool to help neurodivergent (and all) leaders project authority and influence without reverting to detail-dumping. The episode is most relevant to neurodivergent product leaders, ICs aspiring to leadership, and anyone who has ever felt ignored in meetings only to hear their idea credited to someone else.

## Key insights
- **Masking vs. authenticity**: Typical "be authentic" advice backfires for neurodivergent leaders because their authentic default (assuming everyone sees the same logical answer, struggling to empathize with those who don't) actively undermines leadership effectiveness. The real goal is *calibrated* authenticity, not unfiltered authenticity.
- **The core difference is cost, not symptoms**: Everyone loses the room sometimes. What differs for neurodivergent people is how expensive it is — cognitively and emotionally — to detect signal drift and correct it. Neurotypicals fix this cheaply; neurodivergent people pay a much heavier tax.
- **The IC-to-leadership trap**: Skills that make neurodivergent people exceptional ICs (hyperfocus, deep context, edge-case thinking, pattern recognition) become liabilities in executive settings. Going into the boardroom and listing every edge case to demonstrate credibility gets you labeled "not strategic" almost immediately, and that label is very hard to reverse with the same stakeholders.
- **The "retail" concept**: Before any meeting, you must know what you want people to say about it when you are no longer in the room. If you haven't designed that takeaway, you are just reacting to the room rather than leading it.
- **Signal drift**: Your executive presence "signal" naturally drifts if you don't actively manage it. Recognizing when drift is happening — and having a correction mechanism — is the core skill. This parallels CBT's approach of identifying triggers before changing reactions.
- **~45–50% of tech workers are neurodivergent** (per Microsoft research), meaning in many tech orgs it is closer to typical than atypical. Labeling matters less than finding tools that work for you.
- **AI as a spell-checker for influence**: Just as spellcheck turned Dave's Fs to As without changing his intelligence, AI tools can help neurodivergent communicators translate their thinking into language that lands — not doing the thinking for them, but bridging the communication gap.
- **The "idea credit" problem**: A recurring, deeply frustrating pattern where a neurodivergent person raises an idea, gets ignored, and then hears someone else rephrase it to applause. The reframe offered: if you successfully made it someone else's idea, especially upward, that *is* the win in a product leadership role.
- **Mia's case study**: A head of product with genuine strategic capability was labeled "not strategic" because her communication wasn't landing. Over 2–3 months of coaching using signal prep and trigger recognition, her stakeholders' vocabulary about her changed, she stopped having her ideas plagiarized, and she ultimately led international growth for her company.
- **Burnout is a leadership fault**: Referencing a tech workforce survey where ~two-thirds reported recent burnout, Dave frames this as a leadership failure to set sustainable expectations — not an individual resilience problem.
- **Checking Slack before getting out of bed** is called out explicitly as an unsustainable momentum pattern, with noted negative effects on cortisol and the ability to lead effectively.

## Use cases
- **Neurodivergent ICs preparing for their first leadership or executive role** who fear losing the credibility they built through deep, detailed work.
- **Neurodivergent leaders who feel chronically ignored or underestimated** in stakeholder and board meetings despite being highly capable.
- **Anyone who has been labeled "too detailed," "not strategic," or "not executive material"** and isn't sure why.
- **Leaders preparing for high-stakes meetings** (board presentations, funding asks, structural changes) who need a fast, repeatable prep process.
- **Leaders who coach or manage neurodivergent team members** and want to understand the cognitive load differential they are navigating.
- **Leaders experiencing or trying to prevent burnout** — in themselves or their teams — who want a sustainable operational pace.
- **People considering returning to an IC role after struggling in leadership**, to diagnose whether the problem was fit or fixable communication/influence skills.
- **Product leaders in B2B environments** where attaching commercial numbers to initiatives is hard and stakeholders default to skepticism.

## Patterns & frameworks

**CALM Framework**
A leadership acronym Dave uses to describe the four qualities of effective leadership, especially for neurodivergent leaders:
- **C — Clarity**: Lead with the outcome and the ask, not with updates or detail.
- **A — Authority**: Command the room through clear signal presence, not justification of your existence.
- **L — Learning**: Model vulnerability, hold strong opinions loosely, and create psychological safety for teams to experiment and fail.
- **M — Momentum**: Operate at a sustainable pace; protect your own well-being first so you can protect your team's. Unmanaged cognitive/emotional tax leads directly to burnout.

---

**Signal Prep (3-question pre-meeting framework)**
A practical, fast (as little as 10 minutes) exercise to enter any meeting with authority rather than reacting to the room:
1. **What do I need from this room?** — Define the outcome you need, not the update you plan to give.
2. **What is my one-line recommendation?** — Distill your position to a single sentence so you can return to it under challenge without spiraling into edge-case justification.
3. **What will they repeat without me in the room? (Design for retail)** — Craft and seed a specific phrase or framing you want to travel after you leave. Example from the transcript: "minutes not months for our customers."
- *Bonus question* for high-stakes meetings: What is the emotional state of the room, and how do you want them to feel when you leave?

---

**Signal Drift + Correction (Presence Management Model)**
The idea that your executive presence signal will naturally drift toward default neurodivergent behaviors (detail-dumping, over-explaining, defensive responses to questions) unless actively managed. The correction process mirrors CBT:
1. Identify the *trigger* moment where drift begins (e.g., feeling challenged by a stakeholder question).
2. Reflect on *why* it happened and what it cost.
3. Use that recognition as motivation to run Signal Prep before the next similar situation.

---

**"Retail" Mental Model**
Before entering any meeting, ask: *What do I want people to say about this when I'm not in the room?* This reframes preparation from "how do I explain my work" to "how do I design a message that travels." It is the difference between reacting to a room and leading one.

---

**AI as Influence Spell-Checker**
A practical mental model for neurodivergent professionals: just as spellcheck removed the barrier between Dave's intelligence and his written output, AI tools (Dave references an open-source Claude plugin) can remove the barrier between your thinking and the language needed for it to land — without replacing the thinking or judgment itself.
