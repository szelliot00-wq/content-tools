# Why you should prioritise trust over short-term profit – Simonetta Batteiger

Video ID: `eYaw52mSrr0`

## Summary
Simonetta Batteiger, a product leadership coach with 15 years of experience, argues that building trustworthy products is not just an ethical imperative but a superior long-term business strategy. Interviewed backstage at MTPCON London, she challenges the Silicon Valley "move fast and break things" mentality by reframing trust as a measurable, definable competency rather than a vague aspiration. Drawing on established research and real-world case studies (including a Starbucks AI marketing failure that cost 26% revenue in one week), she makes the case that trustworthiness must be baked into product strategy from the governance level down to daily decisions. The talk is most relevant to product managers, CPOs, and founders navigating AI product development who want to build sustainable businesses rather than chase short-term hype.

## Key insights
- **The Silicon Valley "disrupt first, fix later" model is running out of road.** Without a deliberate approach to trustworthiness, teams ship risk fast alongside features — and it eventually catches up with them.
- **Trust has two dimensions, per Stephen Covey's *The Speed of Trust*:** character (intentions + integrity) and competency (results). Both matter. You trust a surgeon because of competency, not just good intentions — the same logic applies to AI agents.
- **Charles Feltman's *The Thin Book of Trust* adds a third element:** care. Trust requires that the other party acts with your interests at heart, not just their own. Violations of this show up in AI as hallucinations, "slop," psychosis-inducing chatbots, and dark patterns.
- **New vocabulary signals new trust breakdowns.** Terms like "slop," "hallucinations," "psychopens," "AI psychosis," and "jailbreaking" all represent named categories of trust violation that didn't exist three years ago.
- **The Starbucks "tank day" case is a concrete, boardroom-ready example:** an AI marketing failure led to the CEO being fired and a 26% revenue loss in a single week — extrapolated, that's a $580 million annual mistake. Trust failures have direct financial consequences.
- **AI foundation models are not trustworthy by design.** They require explicit guardrails: input filtering (what prompts are acceptable), output evals (human-in-the-loop vs. agent-judged outputs), and accuracy/error categorization in tracing.
- **Geopolitical trust is now a product risk.** European companies are scrambling to find European alternatives to US vendors after US government decisions (e.g., restricting access to certain AI models outside the US). Vendor dependency is a trust architecture question.
- **Vibe-coded, AI-generated apps are not production-ready.** Without compliance, GDPR, privacy, and transparency thinking, fast-built tools carry hidden risk that will eventually surface.
- **Skill atrophy is a trust and product design problem.** When AI removes human skill-building opportunities (doctors losing diagnostic ability, pilots losing situational judgment), workflows should be designed with intentional friction to preserve skill development, not just maximize throughput.
- **Diversity and psychological safety are trust-adjacent and research-backed.** Amy Edmondson's work on psychological safety and research on diversity in decision-making both show clear links to better outcomes. 90%+ of shareholder motions in the US to remove diversity board requirements were voted down — boards understand the business case.
- **Product-market fit still wins through word of mouth** even in a sea of AI hype. Tools like Cursor, Lovable, and Anthropic itself grew because they solved real, desperate problems well — not because of hype alone.
- **Anthropic's governance structure is cited as a positive example:** board seats are partially reserved for an ethics committee whose members cannot hold shares, structurally insulating ethical oversight from financial incentive.
- **Blockchain has a legitimate use case in trust architecture:** creating an immutable, traceable audit trail linking inputs → decisions → outputs for AI systems.

## Use cases
- **CPOs and senior product leaders** who need to make the business case for trust investment to boards and executive teams — use the Starbucks example and revenue-loss framing.
- **Founders setting up a new company** who want to build ethical governance in from day one (ethics boards, governance structures like Anthropic's).
- **Mid-level product managers** who want to start conversations about ethics and trust without having formal authority — bring an article, ask curious questions, reference the canvas on operationalizing ethics.
- **Product teams building AI-powered features or agents** who need a framework for thinking about input controls, output evals, explainability, and human-in-the-loop design.
- **Product leaders in regulated industries** (finance, health, legal) where trust failures carry legal and compliance risk — the framework helps quantify and prioritize that risk.
- **Teams doing zero-based budgeting reviews** who face pressure to cut trust/safety-related line items — use the reputational risk and lagging revenue impact argument.
- **Designers and engineers** concerned about becoming "quality assurance for AI" rather than creative contributors — the skill atrophy framing gives language to that concern.
- **European companies** evaluating vendor lock-in risk with US AI providers — the geopolitical trust lens applies directly.

## Patterns & frameworks

**1. Trust = Character + Competency (Stephen Covey, *The Speed of Trust*)**
Trust is not just about good intentions. It requires demonstrated results and capability. Applied to AI: an agent earns trust through competency built via context. Ask: what context does this agent have, and does it have the competency for the task at hand?

**2. Trust Requires Care (Charles Feltman, *The Thin Book of Trust*)**
Trust breaks when one party's interests are prioritized over the other's. In product terms: are you designing for the user's benefit or extracting value from them? This is the lens for evaluating dark patterns, addictive design, and data exploitation.

**3. Martin Eriksson's Decision Stack + "Trust Over Short-Term Profit" Principle**
Simonetta references Martin Eriksson's decision stack (principles → strategy → tactics) and proposes inserting "trust over short-term profit" as a foundational principle. If it's visible in the strategy layer, teams encounter it naturally and are prompted to ask what it means in their context.

**4. The Ethical AI Canvas (Simonetta's own, published ~18 months prior)**
A structured canvas for operationalizing ethical thinking into product development — applicable to both AI and non-AI products. Covers how to ID agents, bake in explainability, define fallback behaviors, and handle user complaints. (Referenced but not detailed in full during the interview.)

**5. Prompt Ledger Architecture for Trust**
A multi-layer trust architecture referenced in Simonetta's talk slides, covering: agent identity, explainability baked into product design, blockchain-based input-output tracing, fallback and escalation paths, and scoring mechanisms for trust over time.

**6. Governance-Layer Trust (Eric Ries, *Incorruptible*)**
Before the first funding round, founders can structurally protect ethical decision-making by: creating an ethics board with seats that cannot be held by shareholders, codifying ethical constraints in governance documents, and separating financial incentive from ethics oversight. Anthropic is cited as a positive example.

**7. Skill-Preserving Workflow Design**
Rather than offloading all cognitive work to AI, design workflows with intentional friction that keeps human judgment sharp. The goal is for AI to challenge thinking and prompt learning — not to reduce humans to QA checkers. Applied particularly to domains where skill atrophy carries real-world risk (medicine, aviation, engineering).
