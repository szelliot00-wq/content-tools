# The Jevons paradox: why AI creates more demand - Martin Eriksson at ProductTank SF

Video ID: `z2UP8b9KMSM`

## Summary
Martin Eriksson, co-founder of ProductTank and author of *Product Leadership*, argues that AI is fundamentally underhyped — not because of what it replaces, but because of what it creates. Drawing on the Jevons paradox (efficiency increases total consumption, not decreases it), he makes the case that AI will explode demand for software, radiologists, product managers, and more rather than eliminate them. He introduces a "decision stack" framework to help product teams move beyond bolting AI onto existing features and instead rethink strategy at a fundamental level. The talk is most relevant to product managers, product leaders, and founders navigating how to position their organizations in an AI-accelerated world.

## Key insights

- **The Jevons paradox applies directly to AI.** When William Stanley Jevons observed in 1865 that more efficient steam engines *increased* coal consumption, he identified a pattern now repeating with AI: cheaper, faster capabilities don't reduce demand — they unlock vast new layers of it.

- **Radiologists are the proof-of-concept.** Geoffrey Hinton predicted in 2016 that radiologists would be obsolete within 5–10 years. Instead: 16% more radiologists exist in the US than 10 years ago, salaries rose 44%, and the Mayo Clinic went from 200 to 400 radiologists. AI made imaging cheaper → more scans happened → more findings → more follow-up → more demand for radiologists.

- **AI is underhyped long-term despite being overhyped short-term.** Amara's Law: people overestimate short-term impact and underestimate long-term effects. The panic about job losses mirrors every prior technology disruption (ATMs/bank tellers, cars/horses) and historically resolves into long-term abundance.

- **84% of the world has never used generative AI; only 0.04% have tried tools like Claude Code.** The "SaaS is dead / build everything yourself" narrative is a tech-bubble illusion. Most software users — restaurant managers, gym owners, accountants — will never want to build their own tools regardless of how good AI coding gets.

- **We're in the DOS era of AI.** The current prompt/chat interface is a primitive starting point. The cost of a given level of AI capability falls ~10x every 12 months (Sam Altman). Following the cost curve is the right bet — as Google did with Gmail's 1 GB storage in 2004 when competitors offered 2–4 MB.

- **Today's AI is the worst AI you will ever use.** It only gets better from here. Models are already testing around 140 IQ (Einstein was ~160), and unlike human biology, AI has no theoretical IQ ceiling. 90% of Claude Code was reportedly written by Claude itself.

- **Building is not the job.** AI creates a "dopamine hit" of endless building, but product managers' core job remains discovering products that are valuable, usable, viable, and feasible (Marty Cagan's framework). AI has transformed feasibility but the other three risks are *more* important, not less.

- **Greenfield vs. brownfield productivity gap is massive.** AI delivers ~10x productivity improvement on new code written from scratch, but only ~20% improvement on legacy/brownfield code with existing tech debt. This fundamentally changes the calculus on when to rebuild vs. patch.

- **SaaS for tech companies is at risk; SaaS for non-technical verticals is not.** A tech company paying $100K/year for an analytics tool may now consider building it themselves. A restaurant owner will not. Core B2B SaaS serving non-technical end markets is likely to see more competition and specialization, not collapse.

- **Probabilistic vs. deterministic software distinction matters.** AI agents are not appropriate for every use case and remain expensive at scale. "Boring old deterministic software that follows a workflow" is often the right call. Anthropic itself still uses Slack, Workday, and Figma.

- **The Intercom case study is the strategic gold standard.** When Intercom saw the threat to customer service, they didn't bolt a chatbot onto their existing product. They built Finn — a fully new brand and product — and shifted from per-seat pricing to outcome-based pricing ($0.99 per resolved ticket, with a $1M performance guarantee). Finn hit $100M ARR in under a year and reignited growth in the core Intercom product.

- **Kive (Swedish startup) shows the "start from scratch" strategic bet.** Originally using ML for asset management for film/photo productions, the founder threw out the entire product after seeing ChatGPT in 2022 and rebuilt around generative AI. Now Polestar generates all marketing assets via Kive without physical photo shoots.

- **Most companies apply AI only at the "opportunities" layer** — adding features, integrations, MCP servers. The companies actually winning are moving up the stack to rethink strategy: business model, customer problem definition, pricing, and competitive differentiation.

- **The "haystack" vs. "decision stack" problem.** Most organizations have a vision on a wall from 3 years ago, a strategy deck from an offsite 2 years ago, and some OKRs in a spreadsheet — with no coherent connection between them. This is the root cause of teams building without direction.

- **Speed without direction is useless.** Speed = no direction. Velocity = speed + direction. Momentum = velocity × mass (bringing the whole organization along). AI gives speed; strategy gives direction (velocity); alignment gives momentum.

## Use cases

- **Product managers** trying to justify moving AI conversations from feature-level to strategy-level within their organizations.
- **Product leaders and CPOs** evaluating whether their current business model still makes sense in an AI world, particularly around pricing (per-seat vs. outcome-based).
- **Founders and startup teams** deciding whether to rebuild from scratch vs. iterate on legacy platforms — the greenfield/brownfield productivity gap makes the calculus very different now.
- **B2B SaaS companies** rethinking differentiation: if software costs approach zero, what are your actual moats? (Data, network effects, brand, Hamilton Helmer's Seven Powers.)
- **Enterprise product teams** managing internal stakeholder alignment and lack of clear strategy, using the decision stack to make explicit choices and surface missing connections.
- **Anyone pitching strategic change upward** — techniques like proposing a straw-man strategy, presenting cost-of-not-doing-it, breaking OKRs into assumption trees, and asking "why" systematically.
- **Technical leaders** wrestling with legacy tech debt and evaluating whether to sunset and rebuild entirely given AI's greenfield productivity multiplier.
- **Investors and analysts** calibrating whether AI disruption narratives are overhyped (short term) or underhyped (long term).

## Patterns & frameworks

**Jevons Paradox**
When efficiency of a resource increases, total consumption of that resource goes up, not down. Applied to AI: as AI makes software cheaper and faster, demand for software — and for the humans who guide, interpret, and act on it — expands rather than contracts. The question to ask: *What would customers do with your product if it were 10x cheaper, faster, or more accessible?*

**Amara's Law**
People overestimate the short-term impact of new technologies and underestimate their long-term effects. Used to frame why current AI panic is both partially justified (short-term disruption is real) and partially misleading (long-term abundance is likely).

**The Decision Stack**
A five-layer hierarchy for organizational alignment, read top-down (each layer answers "how?" for the layer above) and bottom-up (each layer answers "why?" for the layer below):
1. **Vision** — Where are we going?
2. **Strategy** — How are we going to get there?
3. **Objectives** — What matters right now?
4. **Opportunities** — What actions will we take?
5. **Principles** — How do we choose between actions?

Most companies apply AI only at layer 4. Winning companies are rethinking layers 2–3.

**Cagan's Four Risks (Product Discovery)**
Value (is it desirable?), Usability (can customers use it?), Viability (can we operate it profitably?), Feasibility (can we build it?). AI has largely solved feasibility; the other three are now *more* critical and more differentiating than ever.

**Alignment × Autonomy 2×2 (Henrik Kniberg / Spotify Model)**
- Low alignment + Low autonomy = Micromanagement / no one cares
- High alignment + Low autonomy = Authoritative / conformist
- Low alignment + High autonomy = Chaotic startup / hope someone's working on the right thing
- High alignment + High autonomy = Innovative, creative, collaborative *(the target)*

Most AI product teams currently have high autonomy (speed) but low alignment (direction). The prescription: use strategy to add direction, use discovery (user research, market data) to maintain alignment.

**Cost Curve Betting**
Follow the trajectory of AI capability costs (falling ~10x/year) rather than current prices. The Gmail 1 GB launch in 2004 is the canonical example: Google bet on where storage costs *would be*, not where they were. The same logic applies to AI token costs and software development costs.

**Greenfield vs. Brownfield Productivity Split**
A practical heuristic for legacy platform decisions: AI yields ~10x improvement on net-new (greenfield) code; only ~20% improvement on existing legacy (brownfield) codebases with tech debt. When the delta is that large, rebuilding from scratch may now be economically rational where it previously was not.

**Outcome-Based Pricing as Strategic Signal**
Moving from input-based pricing (per seat, per user) to outcome-based pricing (per resolution, per result) is both a business model shift and a product alignment shift. It aligns incentives: the better the AI works, the more revenue is generated. Intercom's $0.99/resolution model with a $1M guarantee is the reference case.
