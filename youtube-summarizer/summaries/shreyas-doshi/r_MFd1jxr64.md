# The Great Manager Lie

Video ID: `r_MFd1jxr64`

## Summary
Shreyas Doshi argues that the common career advice "only work for great managers" is dangerously oversimplified and causes people to make poor company-joining decisions. His core argument is that great managers are a *lagging* indicator of company success, not a leading one — fast-growth companies rarely have polished management, yet those are precisely the companies with the most career upside. He resolves the apparent contradiction by introducing a nuanced framework: be permissive about manager quality when joining a new high-growth company, but be highly selective when transferring internally. The video is most relevant to experienced PMs and senior ICs evaluating job offers or internal moves.

## Key insights
- The "only follow great managers" advice is what Doshi calls the **Great Manager Lie** — it sounds reasonable but leads people to reject career-defining opportunities.
- Fast-growth companies (Google 2002, Facebook 2008, Stripe 2017, OpenAI 2020) almost never have strong management infrastructure during their highest-upside phases. Rejecting those offers due to manager quality would have been a catastrophic mistake.
- **Reorgs invalidate the manager premise**: even if you join for a great manager, reorganizations — which happen constantly — can remove that manager within 6 months, making the original rationale moot.
- Doshi knows "brilliant people who lost millions of dollars in potential equity appreciation" because they refused to report to someone 5 years younger than them.
- **Great managers are a lagging indicator** of company success, not a leading one. By the time a company has great managers, much of the equity upside is already gone.
- Major career inflections happen for four reasons: (1) career change (e.g., eng → PM), (2) joining a rocket-ship company, (3) getting new responsibility, or (4) having a transformational manager. The last one is the rarest.
- Doshi's own growth at Google came from **new responsibility** — when other PMs left, he absorbed key product areas and grew his influence that way.
- Binary thinking — "either great manager or toxic manager" — is a trap. The real spectrum includes: great → competent → competent but overwhelmed → hit-or-miss → absentee → counterproductive → toxic.
- At Stripe, Doshi's initial manager was absentee (managing many teams), and he was fine with that because he was senior enough not to need hand-holding — and that's *why* she hired someone experienced.
- Doshi once **hired his own manager** at Stripe Terminal — he layered himself by bringing in a manager from outside to handle activities he didn't want to do, demonstrating that senior ICs can treat managers as delegates rather than bosses.
- When evaluating managers for an internal transfer, Doshi's four criteria are: (1) will they make it easier for me to get things done, (2) can I delegate things I dislike to them, (3) can they recognize that I am top talent, (4) do they have credibility in the org and can they advocate for me effectively.
- This nuanced framework applies primarily to the **top 20–25% of PMs** — it is not universal advice.

## Use cases
- A senior PM or IC deciding between a hot early-stage company with inexperienced management and a mature company with polished managers.
- Anyone who has rejected or is tempted to reject an offer because the prospective manager seems young, inexperienced, or unavailable.
- PMs considering an internal team transfer and trying to weigh how much the new manager should factor into the decision.
- Anyone who has experienced a reorg and lost their "great manager" and is now rethinking how to make future decisions.
- Senior ICs who feel underserved by absentee managers and need a reframe on whether that situation is actually acceptable or even expected.
- People evaluating early-stage or fast-scaling startups (seed through Series C) where management structure is thin by definition.

## Patterns & frameworks

**The Manager Spectrum**
Rather than the binary great/toxic, Doshi maps a full continuum: great → competent → competent but overwhelmed → hit-or-miss → absentee → counterproductive → toxic. This prevents false dichotomies when evaluating a role.

**Context-Dependent Manager Tolerance Framework**
The core decision tool of the video. Two modes:
- *Joining a new company (tier 1 / fast-growth)*: Accept a wide range of manager types — down to absentee. Prioritize company trajectory over manager quality.
- *Internal transfer (staying at the same company)*: Be highly selective. Since company risk is already resolved, optimize aggressively for manager quality.

**Leading vs. Lagging Indicator Test**
Great managers signal that a company has already matured and systematized. Use this to gut-check "follow great managers" advice — if the management is already great, the highest-upside window may have already closed.

**Four Levers of Career Inflection**
A mental model for how major career jumps actually happen: (1) career change, (2) joining a rocket ship, (3) new responsibility, (4) transformational manager. Helps prioritize which lever to pull rather than defaulting to chasing managers.

**Manager-as-Delegate Reframe**
For senior ICs, a manager is not just someone you report to — they are someone you can offload work *to*. Doshi evaluating a manager by "what can I delegate to them" flips the conventional power dynamic and reframes the relationship as bidirectional.
