# Microsoft’s new AI models & bots dominate the internet

Video ID: `SvBheXuKY8s`

## Summary
This episode of *Mixture of Experts* covers three topics: a Cloudflare study showing AI bots now generate 57.4% of web requests, Microsoft's debut of its own foundation models (MAI-1 and MAI Image 1), and a Verge report on generative AI films at the Tribeca Film Festival. The panel of AI practitioners contextualizes each story, consistently pushing back on alarmist framings and offering nuanced takes on what these trends actually mean for the industry.

## Key insights
- **Bots dominating the web is not new.** Automated traffic has outnumbered humans for years via scrapers and search indexers. The shift is that AI agents now proxy human research queries, so individual humans trigger many more web requests than before.
- **The ad and journalism funding model is the real threat.** If agents consume content without humans ever visiting pages, the ad-supported economics behind news and online publishing break down — and no replacement model has emerged yet.
- **Websites may evolve to serve machines, not humans.** The rise of `llm.txt` files (machine-readable doc summaries) signals that content producers are already adapting how they publish for LLM consumption.
- **Microsoft's MAI-1 is a serious model, not a minor release.** It is a trillion-parameter mixture-of-experts model that activates ~35B parameters per task — placing it below the absolute frontier but competitive for a wide range of enterprise workloads.
- **Clean, licensed training data is a genuine competitive differentiator.** Microsoft's emphasis on fully licensed pre-training data addresses legal risk in regulated industries (legal, accounting, finance) where data lineage scrutiny is high — a gap neither OpenAI nor open-source alternatives cleanly fill.
- **Cost pressure is universal across enterprises.** Every large organization is actively trying to reduce token spend; model routing (sending easy queries to smaller, cheaper models) is the prevailing strategy, and a capable mid-tier model like MAI-1 fits directly into that architecture.
- **AI filmmaking is best understood as a new tool, not a new replacement.** Just as CGI enhanced rather than replaced filmmaking craft, generative video accelerates the path from creative vision to tangible output — but the review/editing burden remains large, and the storytelling craft itself is unchanged.
- **The software development analogy holds for creative work broadly.** Build time compresses dramatically with AI assistance, but planning (alignment on vision) and review become proportionally more critical — skipping planning just shifts the pain to a harder-to-fix review phase.
- **A genuinely new AI-native art form may emerge alongside evolved traditional formats.** The panel sees a parallel to surrealism emerging from early cinema: AI-generated video combined with world models could open entirely new expressive dimensions, not just replicate existing ones.
