# Should you let OpenClaw pen test your system? Plus: Cybersecurity for ephemeral software

Video ID: `TYpg5oxSQ6Y`

## Summary
This episode of IBM's Security Intelligence podcast explores three major cybersecurity topics: Sophos's experiment using the open-source AI agent OpenClaw to conduct penetration testing on a legacy on-premises network, Bruce Schneier's essay on cybersecurity challenges posed by AI-generated ephemeral software, and a Cipher Q report comparing ransomware incident growth (30%) to security spending growth (10%). Host Matt Kazinski is joined by IBM experts Claire Nunees, Dave McInness, and Kimmy Farington, who offer practical perspectives on each topic. The overarching theme is that AI is transforming both the attack and defense landscapes, and security teams must lean into experimentation rather than waiting on the sidelines.

## Key insights
- **OpenClaw as a pen tester showed real promise**, producing 23 actionable, high-quality findings in Sophos's experiment, suggesting AI agents can meaningfully augment traditional red team work.
- **Guardrails create a productivity paradox**: restricting AI agents to prevent damage also caused them to frequently refuse tasks, highlighting the ongoing challenge of balancing safety with usefulness.
- **"AI agents are the most helpful insider threats we've ever had"** — this framing captures why security teams must treat AI agents with the same scrutiny as any privileged user on the network.
- **Security teams are uniquely positioned to lead AI adoption** because they already deal with data overload, understand the need for guardrails, and are professionally trained to ask "how can this hurt us?"
- **Ephemeral/vibe-coded software is likely to accumulate, not disappear**: the assumption that AI-spun-up apps will be discarded after use is unrealistic, given that organizations already fail to clean up unused accounts and credentials.
- **Shadow ephemeral software is an emerging risk**, as non-technical users may build and share AI-generated apps containing sensitive data and unpatched vulnerabilities without any security oversight.
- **Shifting left with AI is promising but incomplete**: while powerful models like Claude and GPT can catch vulnerabilities earlier in development, prompts from non-security-savvy users will still produce flawed code.
- **Defense in depth now means AI watching AI**: layers of autonomous detection are needed to monitor AI-generated and AI-operated systems, since human-speed responses can't keep pace.
- **The ransomware spending gap statistic is directionally useful but methodologically flawed**: raw spending increases don't reflect how effectively money is used, and more spend doesn't automatically mean a stronger security posture.
- **Start with what AI can do autonomously today** — particularly tier 1 and tier 2 threat investigation — then expand to adjacent areas like identity threat detection and continuous controls governance rather than trying to solve everything at once.
- **The attackers won't wait**: both on AI adoption and ransomware, the defensive side risks falling permanently behind if organizations delay experimentation or underfund iterative security improvement.
