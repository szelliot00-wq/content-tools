# Post‑Quantum Security: How Lattice Cryptography Keeps Data Safe

Video ID: `ZRpcYSghGr8`

## Summary
This video explains that modern cryptography, which secures our sensitive data and the internet, relies on hard math problems that future quantum computers will easily solve. It introduces lattice cryptography as a quantum-safe solution, built on vastly more complex multi-dimensional math problems resistant even to quantum attacks. The presenter stresses the urgency for organizations to adopt these new post-quantum algorithms now to protect against the "harvest now, decrypt later" threat.

## Key insights
- Current classical cryptography (e.g., RSA) relies on math problems, like factoring large numbers, that powerful quantum computers will be able to solve in a matter of hours, making existing encrypted data vulnerable.
- Lattice cryptography is a quantum-safe solution based on significantly harder multi-dimensional math problems involving finding closest points in noisy, high-dimensional lattices, which even quantum computers cannot efficiently brute-force.
- The U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has standardized post-quantum cryptographic algorithms, many of which are lattice-based and available for deployment on existing classical systems today.
- Organizations must transition to post-quantum crypto immediately to counter the "harvest now, decrypt later" threat, where adversaries can collect currently encrypted data and decrypt it later once sufficiently strong quantum computers exist.
- Key steps for organizations include discovering all cryptographic uses, creating a crypto bill of materials (C-BOM), evaluating vulnerabilities, prioritizing remediation, implementing new algorithms, and aiming for crypto agility to easily adapt to future cryptographic changes.
