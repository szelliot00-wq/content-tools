# Q‑Day Explained: How Quantum Computing Threatens Today’s Cryptography

Video ID: `rTM95-gZtOs`

## Summary
This video explains "Q-Day," the anticipated moment when quantum computers become powerful enough to break today's encryption standards. It covers how both symmetric and asymmetric cryptography are threatened, why organizations cannot afford to delay transitioning to post-quantum cryptography, and introduces the alarming concept of "Harvest Now, Decrypt Later." The presenter argues that the danger is not purely future-facing — adversaries may already be collecting encrypted data today to decrypt it once quantum capability arrives.

## Key insights
- **Q-Day defined:** The day quantum computers grow strong enough to defeat classical cryptographic algorithms, rendering encrypted data, authentication systems, and digital signatures vulnerable.
- **Two types of cryptography at risk:** Symmetric encryption (e.g., AES) can be partially mitigated by doubling key sizes (128-bit → 256-bit) against Grover's algorithm, but asymmetric encryption (e.g., RSA) is completely broken by Shor's algorithm and requires entirely new post-quantum algorithms.
- **Post-quantum cryptography is necessary:** Simply increasing key sizes won't save asymmetric systems; organizations must migrate to newly developed quantum-safe algorithms.
- **Timeline uncertainty:** Most experts estimate Q-Day is 5–10 years away, with governments and regulators targeting 2030–2035 to deprecate old standards, but the exact date is unknowable — and may never be publicly announced.
- **Harvest Now, Decrypt Later (HNDL):** Adversaries are likely already collecting and storing encrypted data today, intending to decrypt it once quantum capability exists — meaning the breach is effectively already in motion.
- **You can't afford to wait:** Migrating thousands of cryptographic instances takes years; last-minute transitions will be exponentially more expensive due to resource scarcity and opportunity costs.
- **Wildcard risk:** A sudden research breakthrough could accelerate Q-Day dramatically, eliminating whatever preparation time organizations thought they had.
- **Stealth factor:** Whoever achieves Q-Day first (likely a nation-state) will keep it secret to maximize their advantage, meaning the world may not know it has happened for a significant period afterward.
