# The new post-quantum cryptography executive order. Plus: What is Q-Day, really?

Video ID: `RYUR9BdDgyI`

## Summary
This IBM Security Intelligence podcast episode covers the US government's new post-quantum cryptography executive order and the broader implications of "Q-Day" — the anticipated moment when quantum computers could break current public-key encryption. Guests Mason Molesky (IBM cyber policy), Suja Viswesan (VP Security Products), and Mark Hughes (Global Managing Partner, Cybersecurity Services) argue that Q-Day is better understood as an unfolding process than a single event, and outline what organizations should be doing now to prepare.

## Key insights
- **The executive order accelerates federal deadlines**: The Trump administration's EO moves the US government's quantum-safe migration target from 2035 to 2030–2031, establishes clear agency accountability, and requires federal contractors to comply via updated acquisition regulations.
- **"Harvest now, decrypt later" is already happening**: Adversaries are actively collecting encrypted data today with the intent to decrypt it once quantum capability matures — meaning sensitive data with long lifespans (healthcare, financial records) is already at risk.
- **Q-Day is a process, not an event**: Unlike Y2K, quantum risk will materialize in waves over years as different cryptographic systems become vulnerable at different times, making early, sustained action more important than waiting for a clear deadline.
- **Discovery is the critical first step**: Most organizations don't know where their cryptographic artifacts live across their hardware, software, and supply chains. Building a "cryptographic bill of materials" is the foundational prerequisite before any migration can begin.
- **This is a supply chain problem**: Even organizations that address their own systems remain exposed if vendors, open-source libraries, or partners haven't made the transition — interoperability failures can cascade.
- **Crypto agility is the real endgame**: Post-quantum cryptography (four NIST-standardized algorithms, developed with IBM's involvement) is necessary but not sufficient. The goal is the ability to swap cryptographic algorithms as easily as a software update, since algorithms will continue to evolve and new vulnerabilities will emerge.
- **Only ~30% of industry has started**: Most organizations are still in early strategize/discovery phases; those who start early will have significant cost and resilience advantages over last-minute adopters.
- **Quantum is also a major opportunity**: Beyond the security threat, quantum computing promises breakthroughs in drug discovery, protein folding, personalized medicine, complex financial modeling, and sensing — capabilities classical computers cannot approach.
- **Practical starting point**: Raise awareness at the executive level, immediately begin cryptographic discovery across the organization and supply chain, then strategize, modernize, govern, and remediate in a continuous cycle — not a one-time project.
