# You Are on the Dark Web: How Phishing Leads to Data Breaches

Video ID: `97DmVYRxiN0`

## Summary
This video explains what the dark web is, clarifying that it's a small, unindexed portion of the internet requiring special tools to access. It details how phishing attacks are the primary cause of data breaches that lead to personal information being sold there. The video then outlines how to assess the risk based on the type of data exposed and provides a comprehensive list of actionable steps to protect yourself.

## Key insights
- **The Web is like an iceberg:** The "surface web" (5%) is indexed by search engines. The "deep web" (95%) is unindexed content like bank portals or paywalled articles. The "dark web" is a tiny, intentionally hidden part of the deep web requiring a special browser (like Tor) to access.
- **Phishing is the #1 cause of data breaches:** Attackers trick users into revealing their login credentials, use them to access systems, steal data, and then sell or post that information on the dark web.
- **The risk depends on the type of data exposed:** A leaked email address is a low risk because it's often public. A leaked password, Social Security number, or credit card number is a very high risk.
- **Leaked passwords lead to "credential stuffing":** Attackers use stolen email and password combinations on many other websites, exploiting the common habit of password reuse to compromise more accounts.
- **Be cautious of alerts:** An email warning that your data is on the dark web could be a phishing attack itself, designed to scare you into clicking a malicious link. Always verify the source.
- **Take proactive steps to protect your accounts:**
    - Use a password manager to create unique, strong passwords for every site.
    - Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) whenever possible.
    - Adopt passwordless technology like passkeys for superior security.
- **Protect your financial identity:** In the US, you can place a free **credit freeze** to prevent fraudulent accounts from being opened in your name and monitor your credit by checking your free annual credit report.
- **Use free monitoring tools:** Services like "Have I Been Pwned?" can notify you for free if your email address appears in a known data breach, prompting you to take action.
