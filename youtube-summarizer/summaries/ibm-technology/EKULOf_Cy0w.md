# The future of software engineering, tokenmaxxing and AI in higher education

Video ID: `EKULOf_Cy0w`

## Summary
This episode of "Mixture of Experts," recorded during New York Tech Week at IBM's offices, covers three main topics: the evolving role of software engineers in an AI-driven world, the "tokenmaxxing" phenomenon and the challenge of measuring AI productivity, and Nvidia's new RTX Spark chip. A bonus segment with IBM's Chief Impact Officer explores how AI is disrupting higher education, from classroom assessments to curriculum design and industry partnerships.

## Key insights

- **Software is evolving through distinct generations:** From rule-based systems (SW 1.0) to ML-driven models (SW 2.0), foundation models (SW 3.0), and now agentic orchestration (SW 4.0), where human language becomes the interface between humans and AI agents.
- **Software engineer roles are shifting, not disappearing:** Developers are becoming agent builders and orchestrators; testers are becoming agent auditors. The focus is moving to verification, system architecture, security, and hardware-software co-design — skills LLMs still struggle with.
- **The "sandbox" for junior developers is vanishing:** Entry-level coding tasks are being automated, eliminating the traditional on-ramp for new engineers. This demands a rethinking of how software engineers are trained and onboarded.
- **AI tools are democratizing software development:** IBM's internal tool "Bob" acts as a senior engineer for juniors and a junior engineer for seniors, enabling non-engineering departments (finance, marketing, communications) to build software capabilities.
- **Tokenmaxxing is Goodhart's Law in action:** When token usage becomes a metric, it gets gamed — by users writing verbose prompts, by models generating unnecessary output, and by providers pricing in ways that incentivize consumption. Tokens are a narrow, easily manipulated proxy for productivity.
- **ROI measurement for AI workflows remains unsolved:** Clients want to know whether AI-enabled workflows are cheaper and faster than human-led ones. Speed-to-value (e.g., 8 months vs. 1 month) is emerging as one meaningful dimension, but comprehensive outcome metrics are still being developed.
- **Model routing and fit-for-purpose selection matter:** Not every task needs a frontier model. Intelligent orchestration — using small models, open-source models, or even regex for simple tasks — is a key lever for managing cost without sacrificing quality.
- **Nvidia RTX Spark signals a shift to edge AI:** Running 120B+ parameter models locally on a PC changes the privacy and security calculus for agentic workflows, while also reinvigorating the relevance of personal computing as a platform.
- **Universities should not ban AI — it has already sailed:** Students are universally using AI tools regardless of policy. The more productive path is integrating AI into curricula with proper governance, ethics frameworks, and evolved assessments.
- **Assessment in higher education must fundamentally change:** Schools are shifting toward oral defenses, hands-on demonstrations, and assignments that require students to explain and critique AI-generated output — mirroring real workplace expectations.
- **Industry partnerships are critical for higher ed relevance:** Companies like IBM are providing tools (SkillsBuild), guest lecturers, mentors, and talent pipelines to help universities keep pace with how rapidly workplace expectations are changing.
- **Students should focus on transferable skills over specific degrees:** As job roles evolve rapidly, practical, experiential, and collaborative skills — critical thinking, teamwork, human judgment — are more durable than any particular technical credential.
