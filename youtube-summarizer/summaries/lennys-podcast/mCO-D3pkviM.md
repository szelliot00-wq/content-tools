# “Taste is trainable” - Head of product at Notion (Max Schoening)

Video ID: `mCO-D3pkviM`

## Summary
This episode of Lenny's Podcast features Max Schoening, Head of Product at Notion, in a wide-ranging conversation about how AI is transforming product building, team structures, and individual roles. Max argues that the most important trait for surviving and thriving in the AI era is **agency** — the belief that the world is malleable and you can change it — more than any specific technical skill. He draws on his career spanning Google, Heroku, GitHub, two startups, and now Notion to discuss malleable software, taste, what makes great products, and how teams at Notion are already living in the AI-forward future. The episode is most relevant to product managers, designers, engineers, and founders who want to understand how their roles are evolving and how to build better products in an AI-native world.

---

## Key insights

- **Agency is the most important trait in the AI era.** Max argues that even as AI gives everyone skills at their fingertips, the differentiator is agency — the belief that the world around you is malleable and that you can change it. He frames this as "drive Notion like it's stolen" — act like an owner even if you didn't found the company.

- **The Steve Jobs awakening.** The most important mental shift is realizing "the world is made up by people no smarter than you." This realization, whether self-discovered or taught by a great mentor, unlocks agency. Max ties this directly to making things — tinkering, building, cooking — as the most reliable path to developing it.

- **The first 10% of every project is now free.** AI has essentially eliminated the cost and friction of exploring the first version of an idea. You no longer need to write a PRD when you can just build a janky demo. The old "demos not memos" principle from GitHub is now even easier to execute. However, he notes the last 10% is still 90% of the real work.

- **Designers and PMs at Notion are shipping code — but the reason matters.** Max built a small, LLM-friendly "playground" codebase optimized for prototyping AI/chat interfaces, designed to eliminate fear of the terminal. The goal was never production deployment — it was forcing people to think and design in the actual medium (code) rather than in static Figma mockups, which he calls "drawing dead fish" (referencing Brett Victor's talk).

- **Coding is about understanding the material, not delivery.** Max explicitly does not care whether designers or PMs ship code to production. What he cares about is that they understand the medium — especially agent loops — because you can only truly design something if you understand what it's made of. "The reason I care that people code is not because of the utility of shipping to production, but because it forces you to really interrogate the material."

- **Agency examples from Notion.** Two concrete examples: (1) Brian Leven, a designer/engineer hybrid who proactively recruited for the org without being asked. (2) Eric Lou, a PM who asked Max if he'd hire him in the first five employees of a startup, got told no, then systematically built the skills — moving from PRDs to Figma to prototyping in code — to change that answer.

- **The SaaS apocalypse is greatly exaggerated.** Max argues that the "as a service" part of SaaS is what truly has value — maintenance, specialists thinking hard about problems, and not having to tend a software garden yourself. He predicts tools will become more general (back toward '90s-style word processors, spreadsheets, FileMaker Pro) rather than disappearing. He tried rebuilding Notion in a weekend just to push frustration — and concludes people don't actually want to do that.

- **Malleable software** is software that works in the interest of the people using it, not the corporation making it. Most apps are "glued together" — UI, data, behavior locked in — and you can't change anything. Max sees AI as finally making malleable software practical, and sees Notion as an operating system that enables agents to roam a connected workspace — closer to the Unix environment that coding agents thrive in.

- **Taste is trainable, not innate.** Max defines taste precisely: the ability to run a virtual machine in your head where, given an idea, you can predict whether a specific in-group will like it or not. It's built through reps and feedback — exactly like training a model (input → reaction → backpropagation). He notes that taste is domain-specific and you need to decide what your in-group is. Japanese craftspeople are his analogy — just doing the thing for a long time.

- **Great products have one tiny core that is a superpower.** Not a suite of features — one exceptionally good thing. Examples: iPhone's multi-touch, GitHub's pull request, Notion's blocks and slash commands, Heroku's `git push heroku master`, Dropbox's menu bar sync icon (so reliable it was a better internet connectivity indicator than the Mac itself). Adding "just one more feature" to fix a product never works.

- **Being first doesn't matter — being right does.** AirPods weren't the first Bluetooth headphones. Apple wasn't the first MP3 player. Anthropic started after OpenAI with less funding and is now dominant. Durability beats virality.

- **Software is eating the world — faster.** Max's contrarian take on AI transforming roles: it's not that AI will suddenly transform marketing or sales next. It's that coding is getting exponentially cheaper, so software will simply penetrate every domain faster. HR teams are automating workflows because they no longer need to bug an engineering team. "Software is eating the world" just accelerates.

- **Model intelligence will hit a "retina display" moment.** After a certain threshold, you can't see the pixels anymore — you don't need them smaller. Max suspects intelligence will saturate for many knowledge work tasks, and then the optimization shifts to speed, cost, and local execution. He's more interested in AI as an exoskeleton than "a god in a box."

- **Token spend is a vanity metric.** Max equates obsessing over token spend to bragging about lines of code written. He acknowledges Meta's leaderboard approach has some logic (it gets people to change habits at scale), but the metric itself is not meaningful. He predicts in 6–12 months companies will start asking hard ROI questions about token spend.

- **Software quality is being neglected.** Despite the explosion in the *amount* of software being shipped, Max observes quality hasn't improved in 12 months. He uses the physical analogy of 3D printing (prototype with visible layer lines) vs. precision manufacturing for 100 million users — the "factory optimization" phase of engineering is largely absent from current discourse.

- **"Obviously good" as a quality bar.** Notion's internal quality standard is a sticker: "only make obviously good stuff." No one argued the first iPhone wasn't obviously good. No one argued ChatGPT wasn't obviously good. That's the bar. He pairs this with "incremental correctness" — iterate relentlessly rather than hiding in a cave until it's perfect.

- **Inclusivity isn't always the right goal.** Max's contrarian view: building for 8 billion users would alienate the 500 million power users who want something exceptional. Being exclusive and building for the "top of the class" is sometimes the right product strategy. He references TBPN (acquired for hundreds of millions with 8,000 listeners) as validation.

- **UBI hot take.** Knowledge work is already a form of universal basic income — we've invented an elaborate hierarchy of jobs that, relative to basic human needs, represent tremendous surplus. Max doesn't think this will go away; humans will always find ways to insert themselves into agentic loops.

- **Notion AI agent insight.** The agent works well partly because agents need context and hate walls between data repositories. A connected workspace (Notion) is the perfect environment — it resembles the Unix environment that coding agents thrive in. Notion also handles automatic permission management for enterprise search that others don't.

## Use cases

- **PMs and designers wondering if they should learn to code:** Use this to reframe the question — it's not about shipping to production, it's about understanding the material you're designing with, especially agent loops.
- **Leaders trying to change team behavior around AI adoption:** Use the "playground" codebase concept to lower the barrier to entry; remove fear of the terminal before asking people to contribute to production codebases.
- **Founders deciding what to build:** Apply the "tiny core superpower" framework — identify the one thing that must be exceptionally good before adding anything else.
- **Anyone evaluating whether to build vs. buy SaaS tools:** Use Max's "as a service = maintenance + specialists" framing to make a clear-headed cost/benefit decision rather than being seduced by "I'll just build it myself."
- **Product leaders setting quality standards:** Use "obviously good" as a shared internal language and benchmark. Pair with incremental correctness to avoid the "build in a cave" trap.
- **Teams debating whether to keep using Figma:** Use Max's nuanced framing — it's not Figma vs. code, it's about which medium forces you to think correctly about what you're designing. Both may be valid depending on what you're building.
- **Executives reviewing team performance:** Use the "Seeing Like a State" warning — your status reports may be giving you false legibility about how your teams actually work.
- **Anyone worried about job security in the AI era:** Use the agency framework — instead of trying to shield yourself from change, make things, tinker, and develop the muscle of treating the world as malleable.
- **Designers wanting to develop taste:** Increase reps with feedback loops. Expose yourself constantly to well-designed products. Take on side projects where you own the full thing end-to-end.
- **Companies evaluating AI ROI:** Use the 6–12 month warning as a planning horizon — start building measurement frameworks now before token spend becomes a contentious budget item.

## Patterns & frameworks

**1. The Playground Codebase**
A small, LLM-friendly, separate codebase optimized for prototyping (not production). Deliberately simple, "oneshotable," designed to eliminate fear of the terminal. Used at Notion to get designers and PMs onto the "treadmill" of coding without the intimidation of a decade-old production codebase. Once people are comfortable, they migrate to contributing to production.

**2. The Tiny Core Superpower**
Every great product has one small thing that is exceptionally good — not a feature suite. Everything else flows from it. Examples: iPhone's multi-touch, GitHub's pull request, Heroku's `git push heroku master`, Dropbox's sync icon. The anti-pattern is "if I just add one more feature, it will finally be great" — this never works.

**3. Agency as the Core Competency**
The belief that the world is malleable and you can change it. Built through making things (tinkering, building, cooking). Distinguishes people who will thrive in the AI era from those who won't. Not evenly distributed. Can be developed by simply starting to make things and noticing that the world responds.

**4. Taste as Virtual Machine Emulation**
Taste = the ability to mentally simulate how a specific in-group will react to an idea. Built through reps + feedback, exactly like model training. Domain-specific — you must first define your in-group. Trainable, not innate. The more reps, the more accurate the emulation.

**5. Incremental Correctness**
Iterate and ship continuously rather than perfecting in isolation. Pair with "obviously good" as the quality bar. The goal is to get something to react to as early as possible, then iterate. Opposes waterfall. Enables "shots on goal" — increasing the volume of attempts.

**6. Demos Not Memos (+ "Give Me Something to React To")**
Originally from GitHub product reviews: instead of writing documents, build something people can react to. Now supercharged by AI — the first 10% of any project is essentially free, so there's no excuse not to have a demo. Can now run 10 parallel explorations simultaneously.

**7. Malleable Software Framework**
Software that works in the interest of users, not corporations. Spectrum from locked apps (every layer glued together, no customization) to full Linux (total malleability, no guardrails). The goal is ownership over your computing life without sacrificing real-time collaboration and security. Notion as OS is the expression of this — a platform that enables agents and user customization.

**8. The Retina Display Intelligence Threshold**
Analogy: after pixel density exceeds human visual resolution, more pixels provide no value — optimization shifts to other dimensions (speed, cost). Applied to AI: for many knowledge work tasks, model intelligence will hit "good enough," after which speed, cost, and local execution become the relevant optimizations. Implies frontier model obsession is misplaced for most use cases.

**9. Jobs To Be Done (Max's Interpretation)**
Not strictly Christensen's framework, but used as a forcing function: "What does the user actually want to hire your product for, vs. what do you *want* them to want?" Also used to break the "employee mindset" in product reviews — forces you to be the user for a moment and ask honestly: "Would you even buy what you just made?"

**10. Seeing Like a State (Applied to Leadership)**
From James C. Scott's book: executives create legibility systems (status reports, metrics, dashboards) that give the illusion of understanding the system, but the act of creating that legibility strips out the actual reality of how teams work. Prescription: spend time directly with teams to cross-check the signal you're getting from your systems.

**11. "Obviously Good" Quality Standard**
An internal Notion heuristic (literally a sticker). The bar is: would anyone argue this isn't good? Used to cut through ambiguity about what "done" or "high quality" means. Pairs with the anti-pattern of perfecting in isolation — something can be obviously good at 80% complete.

**12. Shots on Goal**
Increase the volume of experiments and attempts. Now easier than ever because the cost of the first 10% of any project is near zero with AI. More shots = higher probability of finding the tiny core superpower. Counterbalance: feature count is a vanity metric just like lines of code.
