# The single most important skill outside of AI | Jessica Fain (Webflow, ex-Slack)

Video ID: `RP4vJeIb7WU`

## Summary
This video emphasizes that influence is the single most important skill for product leaders outside of AI, crucial for getting great ideas built and accelerating career growth. Jessica Fain, a product leader at Webflow, Box, and Slack, argues that product managers often forget their core skills of curiosity and empathy when dealing with executives, leading to misunderstandings and missed opportunities. The core argument is that by understanding executives' "strobe light" calendars, incentives, communication styles, and constraints, PMs can effectively gain buy-in, build trust, and drive significant product outcomes. With the rise of AI accelerating execution, the human skills of strategic decision-making, alignment, and influence become even more paramount.

## Key insights
- **Influence is paramount, not "politics"**: Influence is about ensuring good ideas survive and build momentum, not manipulating for personal gain. It's a fundamental aspect of building great products by securing buy-in and backing from key stakeholders and executives.
- **Executives operate on a "strobe light" calendar**: Leaders have extremely fragmented schedules, context-switching constantly. They may not have centered on your problem, so PMs must provide concise context (30-60 seconds) at the start of meetings, explaining "Why are we here? What happened last time? What are today's goals?"
- **Apply user empathy to executives**: Product managers excel at understanding users, but often forget these skills when interacting with leaders. Treat executives as key "users," understanding their motivations, incentives, and daily pressures.
- **Take ownership of influence failures**: As Annie Pearl (CPO, ex-Box) says, "It's not my fault, but it is my problem." If leaders don't buy into an idea, it's a failure of influence, not just a lack of executive vision.
- **Go in to learn, not to convince**: Approach executive meetings with a mindset of strengthening your plan through their domain expertise and context. Asking "That's so interesting. What led you to believe that?" helps uncover their underlying beliefs and co-create solutions.
- **Align with executive incentives**: Connect your proposed idea directly to their goals, OKRs, or pressures from the board. Ask probing questions like "Tell me what the board is pushing you on?" to understand their true priorities, moving beyond generic "what's top of mind?" questions.
- **Understand communication styles**: Executives respond differently to information (e.g., design, customer story, dashboard, experiment, silent pre-read docs vs. presentations). Adapt your communication format to match their preference.
- **Be proactive and responsive**: Don't wait for explicit mandates; pick up on subtle cues and "bread crumbs of opinions" from executives. Respond quickly (within days, not weeks) to feedback to maintain momentum and build trust.
- **Present options, not just one solution**: Offering multiple options (e.g., "Stuart plus two more" – their idea plus two alternatives) shows you've considered alternatives and opens a forum for discussion. Be ready to show supporting details in an appendix, rather than leading with excessive proof.
- **Ask for what you need to succeed**: If executive requests seem unreasonable given current resources, communicate the additional support required (e.g., "I need eight more people," more time, better alignment with other teams) to achieve the desired "10x" outcome.
- **Build trust through impact and de-risking**: Deliver results consistently. For novel or scary ideas, "shrink the change" by proposing small experiments, proof-of-concepts, or one-week sprints to build momentum and reduce perceived risk.
- **Demonstrate senior thinking by killing things**: Showing a willingness to deprioritize or kill projects, even those you've worked on, demonstrates alignment with broader company outcomes and builds significant trust.
- **AI amplifies the need for human influence**: While AI accelerates execution, it makes the "what to build" (strategy, prioritization) and "how to align people" (influence, judgment, taste) even more critical. PMs shift from being "Gantt chart masters" to strategic orchestrators.
- **Cultivate strategy clarity in the AI era**: With faster building cycles, mistakes compound quickly. Clear strategy, shared beliefs, and understanding "what works" (beyond what AI can generate) become essential for empowered teams.
- **Broaden your context and act like a CPO**: Understand the entire business ecosystem, industry, and organizational dynamics, not just your specific feature or goal. This demonstrates strategic thinking and commitment to company success, accelerating career growth.
- **Authenticity is key**: Influence is not about adopting a different persona; it's about leveraging your natural strengths and personality (whether introverted, data-driven, or a "people person") to build trust and persuade in a way that is true to you.

## Use cases
- Pitching a new product or feature to senior leadership for investment.
- Advocating for increased resources or headcount for your product team.
- Aligning your team's roadmap and OKRs with broader company strategic initiatives.
- Navigating conflicting feedback or opinions from different executives.
- Building a new product from scratch, especially one that requires significant cross-functional buy-in.
- Seeking promotion or demonstrating leadership potential in a product role.
- Facilitating strategic discussions where executive input is crucial.
- De-escalating executive concerns or fears about a high-risk project.
- Redefining product strategy or principles within an organization.
- Mentoring junior product managers on stakeholder management.

## Patterns & frameworks
- **Executive Calendar as a "Strobe Light"**: A metaphor describing the highly fragmented, context-switching, and urgent nature of an executive's daily schedule, highlighting why PMs need to be concise and provide immediate context.
- **Treat Executives as Key Users**: A mental model where product managers apply their core skills of curiosity and empathy, typically used for understanding end-users, to understand the motivations, pressures, and needs of their executive stakeholders.
- **"It's Not My Fault, But It Is My Problem" (Annie Pearl)**: A mindset for product leaders to take responsibility for influencing outcomes, even if the initial resistance or challenge isn't directly their "fault." It encourages proactive problem-solving.
- **"Stuart Plus Two More"**: A tactical approach to feedback where, after an executive provides a specific direction (e.g., for a design), the team returns with that exact request fulfilled ("Stuart") plus two additional, well-considered alternatives to foster further discussion and demonstrate thoroughness.
- **Mento Pyramid (Barbara Mento)**: A communication framework (mentioned but not elaborated by the guest) that suggests presenting information by starting with the recommendation, then supporting conclusions, and finally the underlying evidence – essentially flipping the traditional inductive presentation style.
- **Shrinking the Change (from "Switch" book)**: A change management strategy focused on making a daunting or high-risk initiative seem smaller and less intimidating. This is achieved by proposing experiments, small proof-of-concepts, or short-term sprints to build incremental trust and momentum, rather than demanding a large, long-term investment upfront.
- **Red Teaming**: A military tactic adapted for business, involving taking an adversarial or outsider's perspective to rigorously challenge your own ideas, identify potential weaknesses, and anticipate failure states, helping to build more robust plans.
- **"First the Guests" / "Be a Villager"**: A personal life motto translated to professional conduct, emphasizing being of service, welcoming, inclusive, and proactively building community. It suggests that putting others first can lead to personal and collective joy and success.
