# The most rational take on AI you’ll hear this year | Benedict Evans

Video ID: `BD3vLtWhT5A`

## Summary
Benedict Evans, former A16Z partner and independent tech analyst, argues that AI is exactly as transformative as the internet or mobile — no more, no less — and that we are roughly at the "1997 moment" of the internet cycle: exciting, mostly unproven, with most applications not yet built. The conversation covers job displacement fears, where value will accrue in the AI stack (spoiler: probably not the foundation model companies), the rise of anti-AI sentiment, and why the "task vs. job" distinction is the most underappreciated analytical frame for understanding AI's real impact. It is most relevant to product managers, founders, investors, and knowledge workers trying to make rational decisions amid AI hype.

## Key insights

- **AI is as big as the internet — and only as big as the internet.** Evans's "most controversial opinion" is that AI is a generational platform shift comparable to the internet or mobile, but not the industrial revolution. Treating it as either less or more than that distorts decision-making.
- **We are in 1997 for AI.** Most of what AI will enable hasn't been built yet. The companies and use cases that will define the era are unknown, just as no one in 1997 predicted Google, the iPhone, or Facebook. Calling individual races today (OpenAI vs. Anthropic) is like debating Excite vs. Yahoo in 1997.
- **Adoption is much wider than tech assumes, but much narrower than tech pretends.** Even among 13–18 year olds, only ~15–20% are daily AI users and ~20% weekly. The other ~60% are not using it. There is a very wide distribution of engagement that insiders consistently underestimate.
- **The "task vs. job" distinction is the key analytical frame.** The hard question is not "can AI do X% of this profession?" but "is the task the job, or is the task just a proxy for something else?" Elevator operators had a task (driving the car) that was the entire job — so it got automated away. McKinsey analysts produce a 75-slide deck, but the deck is not what you're paying for; you're paying for political navigation, customer discovery, and organizational diagnosis.
- **Professional services headcount is growing, not shrinking, despite AI.** Every major AI lab — OpenAI, Anthropic — is increasing headcount, including in forward-deployed engineering roles that look structurally like Accenture or Bain. This is because deploying AI inside enterprises requires project teams to map workflows, integrate systems, and manage change — work that companies don't have idle staff to do themselves.
- **Foundation model companies probably won't have pricing power.** Models appear to lack network effects (no winner-takes-all dynamic), lack radical differentiation from one another, and are trending toward commodity infrastructure. The analogy is the global mobile telecom industry: technically extraordinary, ~$1T in revenue, 15–20% capex/revenue, and stocks that have gone nowhere in 25 years because it's a commodity utility. All the value ended up further up the stack — in the apps, not the pipes.
- **The application layer and distribution are where value will accrue.** If the models are commodities, distribution becomes the primary moat. Google is spraying Gemini across Search, Android, and Chrome; Meta embedded its model across every surface; Apple has a billion edge-capable devices. A startup competing against this faces the same problem as a new browser competing with IE in the late 1990s.
- **Job displacement follows historical patterns, not apocalyptic ones.** Since 1800, every automation wave has destroyed visible jobs and created invisible new ones. The new jobs always sound implausible in advance ("railway engineer — why would that be a thing?"). The frictional pain is real and unevenly distributed, but the macro pattern is consistent: automation → price elasticity → new demand → new jobs → net wealth creation.
- **Enterprise adoption is structurally slow.** Enterprise software sales cycles run 18+ months. No large company is ripping out SAP and replacing it with an LLM in two weeks. The doomers who predicted mass layoffs within months "completely fail to understand how the world works." Sector-by-sector transformation will take 3–10 years.
- **The "percentage of profession exposed to AI" analysis is garbage.** Top-down breakdowns like "17% of a senior partner's work could be automated" are what Evans calls "the expert systems problem" — trying to decompose human judgment into logical steps, which failed in the 1980s and will fail again. You also can't predict which professions will be disrupted from the outside (e.g., in 1997, no one thought taxi drivers were internet-exposed).
- **AGI/superintelligence definitions are being retroactively redefined.** Terms keep shifting to mean whatever just started working. "AI is whatever machines can't do yet — once they can, people say that's just software." The more useful point: even if model improvement stopped tomorrow, current AI is already a transformative technology that will take a decade to fully deploy.
- **Anti-AI sentiment is a "big fuzzy mess" with legitimate and illegitimate threads.** Legitimate concerns include deepfake abuse (a 15-year-old can now generate pornographic images of every classmate in an afternoon — Photoshop couldn't do that), AI slop flooding content platforms, and job market uncertainty for young workers. Illegitimate concerns include data center water usage (0.017% of US water consumption per Livermore Lab estimates) and Facebook-sells-your-data style myths.
- **The recorded music revenue curve is a useful framework for AI's economic trajectory.** Revenue dropped ~50% from 2000–2015 (unbundling: "what if I don't have to pay $15 for a CD?"), then recovered to ~75% of peak via streaming (rebundling: "$15/month for all music"). AI will likely follow a similar arc — first doing old things more cheaply, then enabling entirely new things that redefine the question.
- **The right personal response to AI uncertainty is immersion, not resistance.** Going on Bluesky to shout about how evil AI is "gives you a great feeling of moral superiority" but won't help your career. Diving in, understanding what it can do today, and being able to demonstrate that competency in job interviews is the only actionable hedge.

## Use cases

- **Product managers** deciding where to invest roadmap time: use the "task vs. job" frame to evaluate which parts of your product are vulnerable to AI substitution vs. which parts are the actual value customers pay for.
- **Founders** deciding whether to build at the model layer vs. application layer: Evans's thesis strongly suggests the application layer has more durable value capture potential.
- **Investors** evaluating AI company valuations: the telecom/utility analogy is a sobering check on whether foundation model companies deserve software-like margins.
- **Knowledge workers** (lawyers, consultants, analysts) anxious about job security: the framework helps distinguish "is my task the job?" from "is there a deeper job underneath the task?"
- **Enterprise leaders** planning AI transformation roadmaps: the professional services / forward-deployed engineer insight explains why you will need external help to redesign workflows, even with AI doing the work.
- **Executives hiring or managing teams**: the advice on not reducing headcount based on naive "% of work automatable" calculations is directly applicable.
- **Parents and educators** thinking about preparing the next generation: Evans's honest answer is "we don't know," but the advice to immerse vs. resist applies to students entering the workforce.
- **Anyone consuming AI hype or doom**: the 1997 internet analogy is a reusable calibration tool for evaluating breathless predictions in either direction.

## Patterns & frameworks

**1. The 1997 Internet Analogy**
Frame: We are in 1997 for AI. It's real, it's transformative, most of the applications haven't been built yet, and picking individual winners is likely to be wrong. Use it to calibrate between hype (it's already everywhere) and dismissal (it's not real). Implication: presume radical uncertainty about specifics while accepting the macro shift.

**2. Task vs. Job**
Frame: Separate "the task" (the discrete activity AI can automate) from "the job" (the full value a person or firm is actually hired to deliver). Example: McKinsey's task is producing a PowerPoint; the job is navigating organizational politics and surfacing what customers actually think. When the task = the job (elevator operator), automation eliminates the role. When the task ≠ the job, automation may change the workflow but not eliminate the need.

**3. Price Elasticity / Jevons Paradox**
Frame: Making something cheaper doesn't necessarily reduce total spending on it — it often increases consumption. Applied to AI: automating parts of accounting, software development, or analysis historically led to *more* of those roles, not fewer, because lower cost unlocked new demand. The chart of US accountant employment going up through mainframes, PCs, ERP, and cloud illustrates this.

**4. Platform Shift Value Stack Migration**
Frame: In every major platform shift (mainframes → PCs → internet → mobile), the commodity layer (pipes, hardware, OS) captures less value over time while the application and experience layer captures more. The telecom analogy: mobile data consumption grew 1,500–2,000x since 2010; telecom stocks went nowhere. Use this to evaluate whether foundation model companies will extract value or cede it upstack.

**5. Old Thing More → New Thing → Redefined Question**
Frame (from Spotify/Airbnb examples): Technology adoption follows three phases. First, people use the new platform to do the old thing more efficiently (put a catalog online). Second, people build things only possible with the new platform (Uber). Third, the best companies redefine the question entirely (Spotify is not an online music store). The recorded music U-curve illustrates phase 1 (unbundling) and phase 3 (streaming rebundle). PM implication: don't stop at "do the old thing but more" — ask what wasn't possible before.

**6. Distribution as Moat in Commodity Markets**
Frame: When the underlying product is undifferentiated (browsers, AI models), distribution becomes the primary competitive variable. Microsoft won browsers via distribution (bundling with Windows) but it didn't matter because value moved upstack anyway. Google, Meta, and Apple are replicating this with AI. For startups, the implication is that "adequate product + massive distribution" beats "best product + limited distribution."

**7. Jagged Frontier**
Frame (referenced but not deeply elaborated): AI capabilities are uneven — excellent at some tasks, surprisingly poor at others — and it's non-intuitive which edge you're on. You can't reliably predict from the outside whether AI will work well for a given use case; you have to test it. This is why broad exposure and experimentation beats top-down analysis.
