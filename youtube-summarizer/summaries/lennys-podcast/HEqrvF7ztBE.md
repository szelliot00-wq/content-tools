# How I built a 1M+ subscriber newsletter and top 10 tech podcast | Lenny Rachitsky

Video ID: `HEqrvF7ztBE`

## Summary
This detailed interview features Lenny Rachitsky, creator of a newsletter with 1.2 million subscribers and a top 10 tech podcast, being interviewed by his wife, Michelle Rial. Lenny shares his journey from a product manager at Airbnb to a successful independent creator, offering insights into content creation, business growth, and personal well-being. The conversation delves into his creative process, strategies for managing stress and avoiding burnout, and the importance of drawing content from real-world experience, making it highly relevant for aspiring creators, product managers, and entrepreneurs.

## Key insights
- **Rapid Growth & Experience-Based Content**: Lenny started his newsletter in 2019, growing it to 1.2 million subscribers and a top 10 tech podcast. He attributes much of his content's success to sourcing advice directly from practitioners ("people on the ground doing the thing") and personal experience, a principle also highlighted by Michelle Rial in her children's book creation.
- **Origins of Confidence**: A profound, 3-hour psychedelic experience in Joshua Tree gave Lenny the deep confidence that he "had wisdom to share," leading him to seriously pursue writing.
- **The "Pull" to Create**: Lenny initially explored starting a company but was "pulled" towards writing. His friend, Lee Jacobs (a VC), advised him to double down on what he enjoyed and what people valued, leading to his commitment to the newsletter.
- **Lindy Effect & Monetization**: After consistently writing weekly for nine months, Lenny applied the "Lindy Effect" (the longer something has lasted, the longer it's likely to last in the future) to his newsletter. This confidence led him to introduce a paywall, which "worked" and generated "meaningful dollars."
- **Challenges of Success**: Despite loving his work, Lenny likens the weekly content creation cycle to the "Indiana Jones boulder" constantly chasing him, highlighting the treadmill-like pressure of consistent output. He also stresses the risk of "creating a job for yourself that you hate" by chasing all opportunities.
- **Stress Management Techniques**: Lenny attributes his low stress levels largely to genetics (70%) but also to learned optimism, influenced by a University of Pennsylvania happiness course. He also mentions exercise as a way to alleviate negative feelings and previously engaged in 10-day silent meditation.
- **Significant Stressful Moments**:
    - **Business**: A year ago, a product pass launch offering free access to multiple premium tools led to a "nightmare" of sophisticated fraud attacks, primarily from China, requiring intense, sleepless work with Stripe and Substack to prevent the business from falling apart.
    - **Personal**: The birth of his son involved a terrifying medical emergency where Michelle experienced an epidural complication that stopped her heart and lungs, requiring emergency intubation and intervention, which Lenny described as the "scariest moment of my life."
- **Creative Process - Michelle Rial (Charts)**: Her process involves a "perfect amount of coffee" (a single shot latte, avoiding the "Bomber Peak" of too much caffeine leading to erratic thoughts), having a time limit (e.g., two hours), writing down ideas from living life, and simplifying visualizations. She knows a chart is done when it makes *her* laugh or feel something.
- **Creative Process - Lenny Rachitsky (Newsletter)**: He performs "60 to 100 iterations" on his newsletter posts, constantly refining and improving the content before involving editors and copy editors. He aims for succinct communication and a high bar for quality.
- **Early Entrepreneurial Endeavors**: Prior to Airbnb, Lenny had several side projects, including "theatheistpot.com" (a Reddit for atheist news, monetized by ironic religious dating ads), "Tutorials" (a user-contributed how-to site ahead of its time, similar to TikTok/YouTube), and "Local Mine" (his startup acquired by Airbnb, an app for asking questions of people checked into locations, which he admits "nobody needs really").
- **Face Blindness**: Lenny shares he has face blindness and struggles to remember names, which has become more challenging with increased public recognition from his podcast.
- **Parenting as Product Management**: Lenny sees parenting as akin to product management, focusing on "influence" and using structured approaches (like charts and reading books on best practices) to achieve desired outcomes, contrasting with Michelle's more intuitive approach.

## Patterns & frameworks
- **Lindy Effect**: A concept where the future life expectancy of a non-perishable technology or idea is proportional to its current age. Lenny applied this to his newsletter, reasoning that after 9 months of consistent output, it would likely continue for at least another 9 months, giving him confidence to monetize.
- **Ikigai Venn Diagram (implied)**: Although not explicitly named, the discussion around finding something you "like doing," are "good at," "people like," and can "make money from" aligns with the principles of Ikigai, a Japanese concept for "a reason for being."
- **Indiana Jones Boulder**: A metaphor used by Lenny to describe the constant, relentless pressure of producing weekly content for his newsletter and podcast, where once one piece is out, the next is immediately due, creating a continuous "chase."
- **Bomber Peak (XKCD)**: A concept from XKCD (referenced by Michelle) describing the optimal amount of a substance (like alcohol or, in Michelle's case, coffee) that leads to peak insight and creativity before declining into erratic or panicked states.
- **"Following the Pull"**: A recurring theme where both Lenny and Michelle describe letting an intrinsic interest or an idea that "pulls" them guide their creative and career paths, rather than sticking strictly to predefined plans.
- **Experience-Driven Creation**: The fundamental pattern highlighted is that the "best stuff comes from actual experience." This applies to Lenny's practitioner-sourced content, Michelle's ability to write better children's books after becoming a parent, and her process of drawing chart ideas from "living life and noticing things."
