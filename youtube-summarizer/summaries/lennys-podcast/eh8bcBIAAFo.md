# The design process is dead. Here’s what’s replacing it. | Jenny Wen (head of design at Claude)

Video ID: `eh8bcBIAAFo`

## Summary
Jenny Wen, Head of Design for Claude Co-work, declares the traditional design process dead, especially with the rapid evolution of AI. She argues that designers no longer have the time for extensive mocking and must pivot to supporting rapid execution by engineers, often working directly in code. The role is shifting towards guiding vision over shorter timeframes (3-6 months), emphasizing adaptability, and building trust through speed and continuous iteration. This detailed conversation is highly relevant for designers, product managers, and engineering leaders navigating the impact of AI on product development processes and team dynamics.

## Key insights
-   **Traditional Design Process is Dead:** The classic design process of extensive research, discovery, and iterative divergence/convergence is no longer viable in the fast-paced AI era.
-   **Shift in Designer's Time Allocation:** A few years ago, 60-70% of a designer's time was spent on mocking and prototyping; now, it's closer to 30-40%. The remaining time is reallocated to direct collaboration with engineers, consulting, and even implementing last-mile polish in code.
-   **Engineering Drives Design Change:** The ability of engineers to rapidly spin up features using AI tools (e.g., "seven Claudes") forces designers to adapt, often "not blocking that, letting them cook."
-   **Shorter-Term Vision Setting:** Design vision has shifted from 2-10 year outlooks to 3-6 months, often communicated via prototypes rather than elaborate decks, to provide direction in a rapidly changing technological landscape.
-   **Non-Deterministic Nature of AI:** It's impossible to mock up all states or theorize all use cases for AI models. Real-world user interaction and data are crucial for discovery and iteration.
-   **Designers in Code:** Designers are increasingly using coding tools and working directly with engineers on implementation, polishing features, and prototyping in actual code.
-   **"Building Trust Through Speed":** When releasing early-stage products (e.g., "research previews"), it's crucial to commit to continuous iteration, respond to feedback, and rapidly ship improvements to maintain user trust.
-   **Human Value Shifts:** While AI will improve at taste, judgment, and design, human brains remain valuable for deciding *what* actually gets built, establishing accountability for decisions, and resolving disputes between stakeholders.
-   **The Future of AI Interfaces:** Chatbots and terminals offer immense flexibility and are here to stay. However, tactile UIs (like interactive widgets) are still important for efficiency and will increasingly be *generated* by AI models.
-   **Managers as ICs:** Design managers benefit from returning to individual contributor (IC) work to gain hard skills in the changing design process and build empathy for their teams.
-   **Manager's Role:** Beyond pure people management, effective managers provide clear direction and create an environment for teams to do their best work.

## Use cases
-   **Product Teams in AI-First Companies:** Adopting rapid iteration, direct engineering collaboration, and agile vision-setting.
-   **Designers Adapting to AI:** Learning new tools, embracing technical skills, and adjusting their workflow from extensive mocking to execution support.
-   **Product Managers:** Understanding the new speed of development and how to leverage designers for guidance rather than gatekeeping.
-   **Engineering Leaders:** Collaborating more closely with designers in the execution phase and recognizing designers' evolving technical contributions.
-   **Hiring Managers for Design Roles:** Identifying key archetypes (strong generalists, deep specialists, craft new grads) and assessing for adaptability and eagerness to learn.
-   **Design Management:** Re-evaluating management structures, encouraging IC involvement for empathy, and fostering psychologically safe, high-performing teams.
-   **Product Quality and Trust:** Strategies for launching early-stage products (research previews) while maintaining brand reputation through continuous improvement.
-   **AI Tool Development:** Insights into the future of human-AI interaction beyond simple chatbots, integrating generated UIs and interactive elements.
-   **Personal Productivity/Introspection:** Using AI tools like Claude Co-work to analyze personal notes and generate actionable insights or rubrics.

## Patterns & frameworks
-   **"The Design Process is Dead"**: A core argument challenging the traditional, linear design methodology (research -> discovery -> diverge/converge) as outdated for the speed and non-deterministic nature of AI development.
-   **Low-Leverage Time for Managers**: The counter-intuitive idea that managers taking on seemingly "low-leverage" tasks (e.g., deep product dogfooding, fixing minor bugs, personal gestures) can actually be highly impactful and build trust, familiarity, and a strong team culture.
-   **Psychological Safety via "Roasting"**: Jenny suggests that a team comfortable enough to lightheartedly "roast" each other (and their leader) is a sign of deep psychological safety, which, when balanced with high standards, fosters high-performing teams.
-   **Hiring Archetypes for the New Design World**:
    -   **Strong Generalists (Block-shaped):** Designers with 80th percentile skill in several core areas, making them highly adaptable and flexible to expand their roles.
    -   **Deep Specialists (Deep T-shaped):** Designers who are top 10% in a particular skill (e.g., highly technical, visual design, iconography), providing unique differentiation.
    -   **Craft New Grad:** Early-career individuals who are wise, humble, eager to learn quickly, and unburdened by old processes, making them valuable for adapting to new paradigms.
-   **Legibility Framework (Evan Tana)**: A 2x2 matrix where founders can be "illegible" or "legible," and ideas can be "illegible" or "legible." It suggests that true novelty and opportunity lie in "illegible ideas"—those on the frontier that aren't yet well understood or clearly articulated. Designers can play a crucial role in making these illegible ideas "legible" through storytelling, UX, and form factor.
