# The rise of taste, human authenticity and judgment in an AI world | Adam Mosseri (Head of IG)

Video ID: `yQ_EWmtfWvQ`

## Summary
Adam Mosseri, Head of Instagram, joins Lenny's podcast to discuss how AI is reshaping product teams, the rising premium on taste and human judgment, and Instagram's strategy around AI-generated content. The central argument is that as AI handles more execution, the highest-value human contributions shift to taste, vision, strategy, curation, and authentic creativity. The conversation is most relevant to product managers, designers, engineers, and tech leaders navigating how to structure teams and define their roles in an AI-first environment.

## Key insights
- **Team structure is shrinking dramatically.** Instagram is moving from ~13-person canonical teams (2–3 Android, iOS, server engineers + PM + designer + data scientist + researcher) to 6–7 person "pods" of 4–6 generalist engineers plus a "product staff" role that blends PM, design, data science, and research functions.
- **The "product staff" role is the new PM.** It's a generalist who can do basic versions of what a designer, data scientist, and researcher do, using modern AI tools. Specialists (senior data scientists, senior designers) are brought in only when genuinely needed.
- **Taste is the scarcest and most durable human skill.** In a world where building is cheap, deciding *what* to build matters more. Mosseri is "long on designers" because taste is hard to automate — but top designers may migrate into product staff roles rather than staying in traditional design tracks.
- **Hiring traits that are trending up:** curiosity, willingness to try things and make mistakes publicly, grit, quick learning, and self-awareness. Willingness to "sound like an idiot" — like learning a language — is a strong predictor of adaptation.
- **Trending down:** large organizational management skills. Fewer roles managing thousands of people as teams get smaller.
- **AI is reshuffling who succeeds.** People who were slow coders but had strong ideas can now execute them. Mosseri himself, a designer by training, is writing code for the first time in ~10 years using AI tools. The key is whose strengths align with what AI needs from humans.
- **Engineers now spend most of their time planning and reviewing code, not writing it.** This is a fundamentally different job — some engineers will thrive, others will hate it.
- **Token spend needs ROI discipline.** Meta ran into "token incinerators" — workflows that burned tokens without meaningful value. They've reined in costs by auditing dollars-in vs. value-out. Token caps don't exist yet at Meta but Mosseri expects them eventually, possibly proportional to an engineer's demonstrated ROI.
- **AI is good at strategy only with aggressive steering.** Lazily prompting AI for strategy yields predictable, generic outputs the competition would expect. Effective use requires feeding it all constraints: technology state, team motivations, competitive/regulatory/brand landscape. Models that push back are preferable to sycophantic ones.
- **Vision vs. strategy distinction:** Vision = articulation of the desired end state. Strategy = an *opinionated*, controversial path to get there. A strategy that a reasonable person can't disagree with probably isn't a real strategy — it's just competing on raw execution.
- **Best product leaders are curators, not visionaries.** They create environments where great ideas surface, then select and align people around the best ones. Curation of people, ideas, technologies, and strategies is the core skill — not being an "idea machine."
- **AI content is a tailwind for Instagram, not a headwind.** More content = more potential attention. But the key bet is that scarcity of authentic human creativity will make *people* more valuable — power has shifted from institutions to individuals (e.g., athletes more relevant than teams). Instagram's creator-first strategy positions it well.
- **Instagram algorithm misconception:** People assume deep semantic understanding of their interests. In reality, historically it's been large embedding models producing unreadable high-dimensional vectors — not "it knows you like surfing" but "it has a number that correlates with surfing." LLMs are now enabling the first human-readable descriptions of what these embeddings mean.
- **"Your Algorithm" feature:** Instagram is building a way for users to see and adjust the topics the algorithm thinks they're interested in — giving users agency back as recommendations dominate feeds.
- **Chronological feeds structurally fail at scale.** Incentivizes high-volume professional publishers (NYT can post 50x/day; a friend posts once a week), drowning out the people you actually care about. Algorithmic feeds increase overall satisfaction even if individual users initially prefer chronological.
- **TikTok's biggest advantage is exploration-based ranking** — surfacing content users didn't know they'd like (e.g., Afrobeats to a Bieber fan). Exploitation-based ranking (showing what users already like) is easier to move engagement metrics but limits small/niche creator breakout. Instagram has been investing heavily in exploration, originality, and recency.
- **Running experiments at 3B-user scale is inherently public.** Any test will be spotted and covered. The lesson: always have a communications plan *before* you know if you want to launch something. "Not if it leaks, when it leaks."
- **The Instagram video redesign backlash was overstated.** The full-screen video test went to only 4% of iOS users and was never planned for launch. It got conflated with Reels growth, recommendation changes, and creator reach concerns — creating a multiplier effect between creators and press.
- **Reels' biggest strategic mistake:** First version was built on top of Stories (2019). Stories had low read-through rates, so most Reels were never seen and then disappeared. The proper standalone Reels launched in mid-2020 — right as TikTok was exploding during the pandemic. Mosseri believes an earlier standalone launch could have significantly limited TikTok's growth.
- **AI content labeling philosophy:** Don't filter AI content; disclose it. Label camera-captured (non-AI) content long-term may be more practical than labeling AI content, as detection gets harder as models improve. At the account level, fake AI-persona spam accounts are a real problem requiring action.
- **Screen time with kids (ages 10, 8, 6):** Kids earn iPad time by doing homework. No social media. Parents should approve every app. Supportive of no-phones-in-classrooms policies. Concern about kids *not* learning AI tools is as real as concern about over-reliance. His 10-year-old is building a 19-level platformer game using Claude Code via vibe coding.

## Use cases
- **Product managers restructuring teams** around AI productivity — understanding what the "pod + product staff" model looks like in practice at scale.
- **Designers anxious about their role** — reassurance that taste is durable, but preparation to expand into product strategy and generalist territory.
- **Data scientists and researchers** evaluating whether to transition into broader generalist product roles as mechanical parts of their jobs get automated.
- **Startup founders deciding team composition** — how to think about specialist vs. generalist hiring in a world where AI handles more execution.
- **Hiring managers updating interview criteria** — what traits to weight more (curiosity, grit, self-awareness, willingness to experiment) and less (large-org management ability).
- **Product leaders using AI for strategy** — how to prompt effectively by front-loading all relevant constraints rather than asking lazily.
- **Platform/content companies assessing AI content risk** — framework for thinking about AI-generated content as opportunity (more supply) vs. threat (authenticity gap), and how to handle labeling.
- **Engineers adapting to AI-assisted development** — reframing the job from "writing code" to "planning and reviewing code" and evaluating whether that suits them.
- **Parents and tech leaders thinking about kids and technology** — a grounded framework of boundaries + digital literacy + earning screen time + vibe coding as a constructive entry point.
- **Comms and PR teams at large tech companies** — the "when it leaks, not if" communications planning approach for experiments at scale.

## Patterns & frameworks

**Pods model (Meta/Instagram 2026)**
Small autonomous teams of 4–6 generalist engineers + 1 product staff + on-demand specialists. Replaces the ~13-person canonical team. Reduces coordination overhead, speeds decision-making, and reduces design-by-committee. Specialists (senior data scientist, senior designer) are consultants to multiple pods rather than embedded members.

**Vision → Strategy distinction**
Vision = the desired end state of the product or world. Strategy = an *opinionated, controversial* path to achieve it. A real strategy must be one a reasonable person could disagree with; otherwise it's just "be great" — competing on execution alone with no differentiation. Both are where human judgment is most irreplaceable.

**Product leader as curator (not visionary)**
The best product leaders create environments where great ideas surface from the team, then select, refine, and align people around the best ones. Curation applies to ideas, people, technologies, and strategies. Requires having enough of your own ideas to have taste, but not treating ideation as your primary value-add.

**Exploration vs. exploitation ranking**
Exploitation-based ranking: show users more of what they already demonstrably like — easier to move engagement metrics. Exploration-based ranking: surface content users might like but haven't discovered yet — harder, but critical for breaking niche/small creators and keeping the platform culturally fresh. TikTok pioneered exploration; Instagram has been catching up.

**Centaur vs. reverse centaur (Cory Doctorow)**
Centaur = human upper body, horse lower body — human in control, AI as the powerful legs. This is the desirable model. Reverse centaur = horse head, human body — AI sets direction, human executes its bidding. The risk of outsourcing strategy to AI. Gig economy workers (Uber, DoorDash) already live in reverse centaur mode. The goal is to remain the centaur.

**Embedding space → LLM description bridge**
Historically, recommendation algorithms produced uninterpretable high-dimensional vectors. New approach: map user interaction history into embedding space, then ask an LLM to describe what region of that space the user occupies in plain language (e.g., "deep pour-over coffee snobbery"). Enables human-readable algorithm transparency and user-adjustable interest profiles.

**Experiment comms playbook**
At scale, any test will be discovered and covered. Before launching any potentially controversial test: define the message, decide proactive vs. reactive comms, and be ready to explain *before* you even know if you want to ship it. Conflation risk (multiple simultaneous changes getting blamed on one) is a key failure mode to pre-empt with clear, segmented messaging.
