# Why AI could replace 50% of product managers | Nikhyl Singhal (Meta, Google)

Video ID: `yUohoaC8_Hs`

## Summary
This episode of Lenny's Podcast features Nikhyl Singhal — former exec at Meta and Google, CPO at Credit Karma, four-time founder, and leader of the Skip community for senior product leaders — discussing the seismic shifts happening in product management due to AI. Singhal argues that the traditional "information mover" PM is becoming obsolete, while "builder" PMs with strong judgment are thriving like never before. The conversation covers the good (higher comp, more joy, more open roles, broader career paths) and the alarming (massive layoffs, diversity setbacks, relentless pace, forced reinvention). It is most relevant to product managers at all levels, product leaders, and tech professionals trying to understand and navigate the AI-driven transformation of their careers.

## Key insights

- **The "information mover" PM is becoming a dinosaur.** Singhal estimates roughly 50% of current PMs built their careers around moving and framing information up and down the org — writing status reports, translating team output for leadership, managing backlogs. AI is eliminating this work, making these PMs increasingly irrelevant.

- **"Builder" PMs are having the time of their lives.** PMs who got into product because they love building things now have direct access to tools (Claude, Codex, Lovable, etc.) that let them ship without waiting on engineers or designers. Comp is at all-time highs for this group, and they have more job offers than ever.

- **There are more open PM roles globally than at any point in the last 3+ years.** Lenny's job market report showed the highest number of open PM roles since COVID, contradicting the narrative that AI is simply eliminating the PM function.

- **Massive shedding followed by targeted rehiring is coming.** Singhal predicts that in the next 12–24 months, companies will shed large numbers of staff (e.g., 30,000) and rehire a much smaller, AI-first cohort (e.g., 8,000). The 8,000 will all be builders with AI-native skills.

- **Judgment is the paramount remaining skill.** As AI handles mechanical product work, the irreplaceable skill becomes judgment: deciding what to build, evaluating whether a change is good or bad for the product system, determining what's worth releasing, and maintaining brand consistency and sustainability at scale.

- **The product operating system is being rebuilt internally.** Senior PMs and CPOs are using AI tools to automate their internal workflows — product reviews, standups, status reports, matching community members, recruiting pipelines. The thing PMs are "building" is often the internal system that makes their team faster, not just the customer-facing product.

- **The "shadow superpower" trap.** The PMs who mastered the old system find it hardest to reinvent themselves, because their current approach is still working for them. Conversely, those who are struggling have more incentive to change. This is why high performers are paradoxically at risk.

- **Personal brand and prestigious logos matter less now.** What used to signal PM competence — working at Google, Meta, Apple — is becoming less relevant because the way product is built at those companies may be out of date. Interviewers are now asking scenario- and tool-based questions: "What tools do you use? How do you think? What's your judgment?" not "Tell me about what you shipped 5 years ago."

- **The PM role is expanding into every industry and function.** Singhal sees PMs as "dandelion seeds" — agents of change who will spread into marketing, sales, HR, and non-tech industries. One community member interviewed for a CHRO role. 14 of his 125 community members became founders in the last 12 months (up from 1 in the first 3 years).

- **Diversity is taking a step back.** Because the AI wave is concentrated in the Bay Area, companies are hiring fewer people who look and act like themselves. Women in their "power years" who are also raising children have less time to spend nights and weekends on Claude or Codex. Singhal sees this as a serious and underacknowledged problem.

- **Finding a "moment of joy" is the key psychological catalyst.** Everyone who successfully crosses from fear to engagement has a personal story — building something small that worked, staying up all night on a side project, creating a tool their partner uses. This moment is what breaks the activation energy barrier and creates momentum.

- **The reinvention period is finite, not forever.** Singhal compares this to the shift from hardware product management to internet product management. It was chaotic for a couple of years, then stabilized. The current chaos is similarly bounded — probably 2 years — after which new norms will emerge.

- **Engineers are converging on PM skills.** As coding becomes automated, what remains for engineers is judgment: what to build, whether it's good, where the system should go. Engineers have a natural edge in systems thinking and obsolescence mindset. The lines between PM, engineer, and designer are blurring.

- **Design is at risk of being conflated with production.** Companies are conflating design with pixel generation (production work) rather than taste-making. The number of design roles is plateauing. Singhal worries the industry is under-valuing taste in this era.

- **Bad software will be fixed at scale.** Singhal predicts that within 2 years, the vast majority of neglected, poorly maintained software (home automation apps, mainframe-based enterprise systems, etc.) will be improved because any company can now "sit down and tell Claude to fix it."

- **The "equally disappoint everyone" prioritization model.** For mid-career PMs in their 30s with kids, aging parents, health demands, and a rapidly changing job, the honest mental model is: you have 6–12 hours of capacity against 20 hours of demand. The goal is to disappoint no single group catastrophically, distributing the shortfall evenly.

- **Swallowing ego is required.** Singhal urges PMs not to hold out for roles at their prior level. Taking something smaller to stay current and get on the right "boat" is the smart long-term play. The cream rises once you're on the right side of the transition.

## Use cases

- **Mid-career PMs (30s–40s) feeling stuck or anxious** about whether their skills are still relevant and whether they should change jobs or directions.
- **Senior PMs and CPOs** deciding how to restructure their teams, what to automate internally, and how to lead through this transition.
- **PMs who are "information movers"** — those who built careers on stakeholder management, status reporting, and organizational navigation — who need an honest assessment of their situation.
- **PMs considering founding a company or moving into a non-PM executive role** (CEO, CHRO, head of growth, etc.).
- **Engineers or designers** considering moving into product, as the role's definition is expanding and the skills from those disciplines are increasingly valued.
- **Product leaders building or restructuring teams** — understanding what to hire for (builders with judgment, AI-first candidates) and what roles to eliminate.
- **Job seekers preparing for PM interviews** — understanding that interviewers now care about tools, current methods, and judgment scenarios more than past logos or shipped features.
- **Leaders worried about team burnout** — understanding how to create "moments of joy" as a retention and engagement strategy during this chaotic period.
- **Professionals in non-tech industries** watching AI arrive in their sector and needing a change agent with product skills.

## Patterns & frameworks

**1. The Builder vs. Information Mover Split**
Singhal divides the PM population into two camps: "builders" (who love creating things and getting hands-on) and "information movers" (who built careers on communication, synthesis, and organizational navigation). His core argument is that AI is rapidly eliminating the value of the latter while dramatically amplifying the value of the former. This is a lens for self-assessment: which camp are you in, and is that where you want to be?

**2. The "Obsolete Yourself" Engineering Mindset**
Derived from a mentor's definition of a great engineer ("someone who obsoletes themselves from everything they do"), this is the operating philosophy Singhal applies to his own work. The framework: identify anything you do repeatedly → ask whether AI can do it → build or prompt until it can → move to the next thing. Applied to PM work: automate status reports, matching, recruiting pipelines, product reviews, and any other mechanical process.

**3. The "Moment of Joy" Threshold Model**
Singhal describes a consistent psychological pattern among PMs successfully making the AI transition: they experience one personal, often small, breakthrough moment (built an app for their spouse, automated their inbox, controlled their home lights via code). This moment converts fear into engagement and is self-reinforcing. The practical implication for leaders: engineer these moments for your team. For individuals: don't wait for work to provide the moment — create it on a side project.

**4. The "Shadow Superpower" Trap**
A named mental model: the better you are at the old system, the harder it is to see the need for a new one — because everything still seems to be working. This creates a dangerous blind spot for high performers. The framework asks: are you resisting change because you've genuinely evaluated the new system and found it lacking, or because the old system is still rewarding you personally?

**5. The "Equally Disappoint Everyone" Prioritization Algorithm**
A framework for navigating the power years (30s–40s) when demands from work, kids, parents, health, friends, and finances all exceed available time. Rather than optimizing for one area and neglecting others catastrophically, the model is to distribute disappointment evenly — no single domain gets dramatically more or less than others. This is presented as a realistic, honest operating model, not an ideal one, and is the context in which asking someone to also "stay current on AI" is especially hard.

**6. The "Skip Job" Career Framework**
The organizing philosophy of Singhal's Skip community: the best career decision is not optimizing for your next move but for the move after that — the "skip job." In the current environment, this means accepting short-term sacrifices (smaller title, lower immediate leverage) to position yourself on the AI-first side of the industry transition, because that's where the skip opportunity lives.

**7. The Shed-and-Rehire Cycle Prediction**
A near-term structural prediction: companies will shed large numbers of staff (legacy, non-AI-native) and rehire a smaller, AI-first cohort at higher compensation. The ratio he cites is illustrative: shed 30,000, rehire 8,000. The rehired group shares a profile: builder mentality, comfort with AI tools, strong judgment. This is both a warning and a targeting framework — understand which cohort you want to be in and work backward.

**8. The Chaos-Then-Stabilization Historical Analogy**
Singhal draws a parallel to the transition from hardware/enterprise product management to internet product management (circa Google's APM program). That transition looked like total chaos — roles were undefined, skill sets mismatched, incumbents disadvantaged — but stabilized into a new normal within a few years. The current AI transition is mapped onto this pattern: expect ~2 years of chaos, then a new stable operating model will emerge. This framework is meant to make the disruption feel bounded and survivable rather than permanent.
