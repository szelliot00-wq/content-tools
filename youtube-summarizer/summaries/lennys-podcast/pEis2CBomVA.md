# Negotiation Expert: How non-executives are quietly earning exec-level salaries | Jacob Warwick

Video ID: `pEis2CBomVA`

## Summary
This video features Jacob Warwick, a professional negotiator who helps senior tech execs, athletes, and celebrities navigate complex career negotiations. He argues that non-executives can earn exec-level salaries by understanding and applying advanced negotiation tactics. Warwick emphasizes moving beyond traditional, confrontational negotiation to a collaborative, value-based approach, focusing on solving the company's pain points and leveraging psychological principles. The advice is highly relevant for product managers, engineers, designers, and anyone seeking to significantly increase their compensation by challenging salary norms and building confidence in their self-worth.

## Key insights
-   **Significant Missed Compensation:** People often leave 20% or more on the table by not negotiating, even with a simple push-back like "What's the chance there could be a little more?" Warwick's clients typically see 40% increases, with some reaching 100-400% by breaking salary bands.
-   **Negotiation Starts Early:** Your public narrative (LinkedIn, resume), headshot, and early conversations with recruiters set perceptions. Position yourself as a valuable, unique solution, not a commodity. The less information you share early about past compensation, the better, as it allows you to control the narrative later.
-   **Avoid Email for Critical Negotiations:** Email removes tone, body language, and immediate feedback. Always aim for a video call or in-person meeting to better control the conversation and read the room.
-   **Negotiate with the Decision-Maker:** Don't play "telephone" with recruiters who may miscommunicate your requests. Seek direct conversations with the hiring manager or the person who controls the P&L/budget.
-   **Don't Anchor Too Early (or at all):** Avoid stating your desired salary range upfront. Instead, respond with "What did you have in mind?" or "I don't discuss compensation until I understand the full scope of the role." Anchoring high can be effective if you must give a number, as studies show those who anchor egregiously high win more often.
-   **Focus on Value and Pain Points:** Treat your job search like an enterprise sales process. Understand the company's core problems, articulate how you will solve them, and quantify the value you can create. This shifts the conversation from your past accomplishments to your future impact.
-   **"Sell the Vacation":** Help the hiring manager visualize a future where the problems you're discussing are solved because you are there. Connect your contributions to their personal success and relief from pain.
-   **Patience is Key ("Haste equals risk"):** Slow down the negotiation process. This allows you to gather more information, build rapport, and project scarcity and thoughtfulness, increasing your leverage.
-   **Collaboration, Not Confrontation:** Frame the negotiation as a joint problem-solving effort. You and the company are on the same side, working together to achieve mutual success.
-   **Leverage Emotional Appeals (Pathos):** While logic and credibility are important, tapping into emotions (e.g., giving positive reputations, appealing to shared experiences, creating a sense of urgency about missing out on you) can be more powerful in negotiation.
-   **Get Creative with Compensation:** Beyond base salary and stock, explore performance incentives, milestone triggers (e.g., "If the company grows to X ARR, I get Y"), or even unusual perks if standard comp is capped (e.g., the G-Wagon story).
-   **Authenticity and Ownership in Setbacks:** If negotiations go sideways or you make a mistake, approach with honesty and take extreme ownership. Being transparent about bad advice or missteps can rebuild trust and salvage deals (e.g., the CMO who got her rescinded offer back by admitting she got bad advice).
-   **"If you were in my shoes, what would you do?":** A powerful question to use when negotiations stall, prompting the other party to empathize and suggest solutions from their perspective.
-   **Fight for a "Rising Tide":** Pushing for better compensation doesn't just benefit you; it can raise the precedent for future hires and improve company-wide policies, making it easier for others.

## Patterns & frameworks
-   **Value-Based Negotiation:** Focus on clearly articulating the pain you will solve and the value you will create for the company, rather than simply comparing against market benchmarks. This involves asking discovery questions to understand their deepest needs.
-   **Selling the Vacation:** A psychological tactic where you guide the other party to visualize a desirable, pain-free future that you will help them achieve. By associating you with this positive outcome, you become indispensable.
-   **Information, Timing, and Power:** The core pillars of negotiation. Gaining more information about the company's problems, budget, and motivations, and controlling the timing of key discussions, creates leverage and power.
-   **Collaborative Negotiation:** Shifting the mindset from an adversarial "you vs. me" to a "we vs. the problem" approach. This fosters information sharing and joint problem-solving, making it easier to break through impasses.
-   **Reciprocity Principle (from Robert Cialdini):** Giving value (e.g., making introductions, offering insights) before asking for something in return. This creates an obligation in the other party to reciprocate.
-   **Tactical Empathy (Chris Voss):** Actively listening and demonstrating understanding of the other party's emotions, motivations, and perspective, even if you don't agree. This helps build rapport and find common ground.
-   **Frictionless Process:** Designing interactions (like interviews) to be cognitively easy and compelling for the other party. This involves leading the conversation, asking strategic questions, and guiding them toward desired conclusions.
-   **Giving Positive Reputation:** Ascribing a positive characteristic or value to the other party (e.g., "I appreciate your advocacy for women"). This creates a psychological obligation for them to act in a way that upholds that reputation.
-   **Never Split the Difference:** A principle advocating against automatically compromising in the middle. Instead, understand the true value and push for what is deserved, asking questions like "Was that a mistake?" if an offer is lower than expected.
-   **Ethos, Pathos, Logos (Aristotle's Rhetoric):** While executives often rely on Logic (Logos) and Credibility (Ethos), Warwick emphasizes leveraging Emotion (Pathos) to close deals and influence outcomes, as emotions often drive decisions more than pure logic.
