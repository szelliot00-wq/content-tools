# How to build a company that withstands any era | Eric Ries, Lean Startup author

Video ID: `PoJ1vTdHpks`

## Summary
Eric Ries, author of *The Lean Startup*, returns to discuss his new book *Incorruptible: Why Good Companies Go Bad and How Great Companies Stay Great*. The core argument is that company success itself becomes a liability — attracting financial pressures that corrupt original missions — and that founders must proactively build structural and cultural protections before it's too late. Ries draws on hundreds of years of corporate history, case studies from Novo Nordisk to Anthropic, and his own firsthand experience advising founders to present a practical blueprint. The video is most relevant to founders, product leaders, board members, and anyone building or working inside a company they care about preserving.

## Key insights

- **Success is the threat, not competition.** The thing that destroys great companies is rarely a better competitor — it's financial gravity: the temptation to extract value from a successful business rather than continue creating it. The more golden the goose, the greater the temptation to butcher it.

- **"The force that no one controls but everyone obeys"** drags organizations toward mediocrity. Ries calls this corruption — not legal corruption, but structural degradation, like corroded metal bolts in a bridge. The question isn't whether gravity exists, but whether you've used stainless steel.

- **Only 20% of founders remain CEO 3 years after IPO**, according to Harvard Law School data. Yet every lawyer, VC, and banker tells each founder they are the exception. Statistically, you are almost certainly in the 80%.

- **"It is always too early until it's too late."** Every stage of company growth — incorporation, seed, Series A, growth round, IPO prep — someone will tell you to wait until later to implement mission protections. By IPO, it's too late. The window closes gradually and then suddenly.

- **Standard Delaware charters are a trap.** The phrase "any lawful act or activity" sounds open-ended but legally means "maximize shareholder returns." Founders often sign documents they've never read, unknowingly giving up control of their company's purpose.

- **The Vector/Philip Morris case study.** UK inhaler company Vectura was purchased by Philip Morris for just 15 cents more per share than a competing bid (not even a dollar). The board said they had a fiduciary duty to accept the highest bid. Philip Morris spent £1.1 billion, took a £900 million write-down within three years, and disposed of the company for parts. The company no longer exists.

- **Shareholder primacy is only ~40 years old.** For most of corporate history, companies were required to declare a specific beneficial public purpose. Before the 1980s, changing a company's purpose away from its stated mission could result in the corporate death penalty — courts voiding the charter. This is a new and aberrant idea, not a natural law.

- **Novo Nordisk was founded in 1920s Denmark** by Marie and August Krogh specifically to prevent insulin from being exploited (decades before Martin Shkreli). They used a two-tiered industrial foundation structure — a for-profit owned and governed by a nonprofit foundation. Companies with this structure are **6x more likely to survive to year 50** and have superior return on invested capital. Novo Nordisk is now worth hundreds of billions of dollars.

- **Trustees of the Novo Nordisk Foundation once intervened** to prevent the for-profit from being sold out. That intervention ultimately created over **$500 billion in shareholder value**.

- **Zeiss (German optics) had this foundation structure in 1885.** These structures predate most modern "best practices" pushed by lawyers and bankers.

- **The "harder is easier" principle.** Committing to quality, ethics, safety, and integrity upfront creates trust — and trust is the most underrated asset in business. Trustworthiness lowers customer acquisition costs, increases loyalty, improves employee alignment, and reduces friction everywhere. But it requires doing the hard thing even when the ROI isn't immediately visible.

- **Cloudflare's encryption story.** A junior engineer pointed out that a better internet should be an encrypted internet — but SSL encryption was Cloudflare's #1 revenue driver (paid upgrade). Matthew Prince said "let's figure it out." They hand-rolled their own software, negotiated with certificate authorities, drove their costs to near-zero, and gave encryption away for free. Short-term: premium conversion rates dropped. Long-term: top-of-funnel increased by an order of magnitude. Cloudflare is now a $70 billion company. They are widely credited with creating the encrypted internet we take for granted.

- **Groupon's email frequency collapse.** Andrew Mason held firm at one email per day — the product's core value proposition. Executives used data/ROI language to justify testing two emails. It made more money short-term. Then three emails. Then eight. The trust that made Groupon special was gradually destroyed.

- **Anthropic's structural protections** include: (1) Public Benefit Corporation status from inception, (2) the Long-Term Benefit Trust (LTBT) — a two-tiered governance structure where directors on the for-profit board are appointed by and accountable to outside trustees who are AI safety experts with **no equity** in Anthropic. These trustees have an incentive to see the mission done properly, not to maximize growth. Ries played a small advisory role when Dario Amodei was a first-time founder and Anthropic was not yet a hot company.

- **Anthropic's mission protections are why they can say no.** When Anthropic refused to release a model it deemed too dangerous, or turned down a $200M Pentagon contract, it cost them enormously. That courage is structurally enabled — investors cannot simply oust the CEO on a moment's notice.

- **At the Vatican AI governance conference**, Ries looked down the panel — Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Cohere, Palantir, etc. — and realized **not a single one uses standard governance**. Every major AI company has deliberately chosen alternative governance because the technology is too consequential.

- **The "culture bank" concept (from Todd Park/Howard Schultz).** Every action that defends company values at a cost is a deposit. Every greedy or self-interested organizational action is a withdrawal. The rule: only make deposits, never intentionally make withdrawals. This creates a compounding trust asset.

- **Mission-aligned companies operate like a flow state.** Decision-making is faster, meetings are fewer, alignment is natural. Clay Christensen: "It's easier to do the right thing 100% of the time than 98% of the time" — because once you allow exceptions, every exception requires a meeting.

- **The quarterly report vs. manslaughter thought experiment.** Google filing its quarterly report on time is essentially 100% certain because there's a massive institutional apparatus ensuring it. "Don't be evil" was just a slogan — no apparatus. If something matters, show me the apparatus.

- **Organizations are the oldest form of artificial intelligence** — emergent intelligences governed by the same principles as transformer models. The ant colony solving the piano-movers puzzle: one ant cannot solve it, but 1,000 ants solve it and appear to display intelligence. More ants = faster solution. More humans = worse solution, *unless they are carefully aligned*. This is why organizational design matters deeply for AI development.

- **Conway's Law applied to AI alignment.** Software products carry the organizational imprint of their makers. The org chart is visible in the architecture diagram. Human values flow from parent to child system. You cannot solve AI alignment if you haven't solved human alignment inside your organization.

## Use cases

- **Pre-incorporation founders** who have not yet raised money — the easiest and highest-leverage moment to implement all of these protections at near-zero cost.
- **Series A–C founders** who need to retroactively advocate for mission protections to their boards and investors before IPO prep begins.
- **Founders approaching IPO** who want to understand what governance structures they may have already signed away, and what can still be done.
- **Product managers** deciding how to prioritize features, set OKRs, or push back on extractive short-term metrics that erode product quality.
- **Employees and job candidates** who want to evaluate whether a company is genuinely mission-driven or just "mission hopeful."
- **Board members** who want to understand their fiduciary responsibilities beyond shareholder primacy.
- **AI company founders and leaders** who must grapple with the governance implications of building consequential technology.
- **Investors** who want to understand why mission-protected structures often outperform conventional governance over the long term.
- **Leaders at any level** who feel the tension between "doing the right thing" and short-term ROI pressure, and want a framework for defending principles.
- **People working in or studying corporate governance**, M&A, or nonprofit/for-profit hybrid structures.

## Patterns & frameworks

**1. Harder Is Easier**
The principle that committing upfront to quality, ethics, safety, and integrity — even when costly — creates compounding trust that makes everything easier over time. You cannot do it for the reward; you must do it for the thing itself. The Cloudflare encryption example is the canonical illustration.

**2. The Figure It Out Principle**
When a principled commitment creates a seemingly impossible constraint (e.g., "give away our most profitable product"), great leaders respond with "let's figure it out" rather than using difficulty as an excuse to abandon the principle. The tension is a teaching moment for the organization about what it truly stands for.

**3. Ethos + Integrity = Incorruptibility**
The master formula of the book. *Ethos* = internal alignment and character (purpose, mission, values embedded in operations). *Integrity* = structural resistance to external pressure and internal temptation. Neither alone is sufficient; both together create durable organizations.

**4. Mission Drive (vs. Mission Hopeful)**
The audit of whether a company is genuinely mission-driven. The test: is there any way someone in the company can profit by betraying the mission? If yes, the mission is a candy coating on an extractive engine. True mission drive means you structurally cannot profit except by achieving the mission. Operationalized as OKRs/accountability systems for fiduciary commitments.

**5. The Culture Bank**
Deposits = actions that defend company values at a cost (sacrifice in service of the mission). Withdrawals = greedy or self-interested organizational actions. The Todd Park Rule: only make deposits, never intentionally make withdrawals. Mistakes happen, but intentional withdrawals are prohibited.

**6. The Invisible Leader (Mary Parker Follett, ~1920)**
The most consequential decisions in any organization are made when no manager is present. The real leader is the *common purpose* — the shared sense of mission internalized by everyone. A founder's job is to cultivate this invisible leader, because no amount of "founder mode" can substitute for purpose that lives in the organization's DNA.

**7. The Spiritual Holding Company**
Ries's omnibus term for the category of governance structures that give a mission guardian legal power over a for-profit entity. Variants include: nonprofit industrial foundation (Novo Nordisk, Zeiss), perpetual purpose trust / PPT (Patagonia, Anthropic LTBT), employee ownership trust (John Lewis Partnership), employee voting trust (Alibaba), cooperatives (Mondragon), and evergreen/no-investor models. All share the structure of a non-economic entity with mission oversight authority over a for-profit operation.

**8. Mission Guardian**
A specific person or entity whose explicit job is to ensure the organization remains mission-locked. Options: founder control (good temporary bridge, but fragile and exhausting), nonprofit foundation, perpetual purpose trust, employee ownership. The key requirement: the guardian must have *legal power* to act, not just moral authority. Without enforcement capability, it's just a slogan.

**9. The Director's Oath**
Analogous to the Hippocratic Oath for doctors — a formal commitment written into the corporate charter that all board directors must affirm as a precondition of service. Ries argues directors make far more consequential decisions than nurses, yet are held to no comparable ethical standard.

**10. The Public Benefit Corporation (PBC) Filing**
A two-page legal filing in Delaware that replaces "any lawful act or activity" with a specific stated purpose. Described as the single easiest, lowest-cost, zero-downside action any founder can take. It solves one specific problem: if sued for breaching fiduciary duty to investors, the company can point to the charter investors agreed to. All major AI labs use this structure.

**11. "It Is Always Too Early Until It Is Too Late"**
A pattern observed across hundreds of companies: at every stage (incorporation, seed, Series A, growth, IPO prep), advisors will tell you mission protections are premature. By the time you have leverage to implement them (IPO), it's too late. The only solution is to act immediately, regardless of stage.

**12. The Five Horsemen of Organizational Decay**
The five things that get cut first when financial pressure mounts, and where betrayal is least immediately visible: (1) Safety, (2) Performance, (3) Quality, (4) Design, (5) Innovation. These are the canaries in the coal mine for mission drift.
