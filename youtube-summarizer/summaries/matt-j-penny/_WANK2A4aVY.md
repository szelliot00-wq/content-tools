# Using AI But Your Business Isn't Improving? Here's Why

Video ID: `_WANK2A4aVY`

## Summary
This video addresses a common mistake business owners make when implementing AI: starting with a exciting tool and then trying to find a use for it, rather than starting with a clearly defined business problem. The presenter argues that this "working forwards" approach wastes time, money, and creates a disjointed "Frankenstein business" that doesn't actually improve performance. To counter this, the presenter outlines a four-step backwards-working framework: define your goal, audit what you're already doing, then find the simplest AI solution, and finally review and iterate. The video is most relevant to small business owners, agency founders, and entrepreneurs who are experimenting with AI but not seeing meaningful business results.

## Key insights
- **The core trap:** Most business owners see an AI tool, get excited, and ask "how can I use this?" — this is "working forwards" from the solution rather than "working backwards" from the problem.
- **Mark's cautionary tale:** A real agency founder (anonymized as "Mark") spent 3 weeks and thousands of dollars building a custom AI chatbot for lead generation. It worked technically but closed zero leads, while his core operational problems remained completely unsolved.
- **The hammer analogy:** Adopting a tool before defining a problem is like "buying a hammer and wandering around your house looking for a nail." It feels productive but is actually a form of procrastination.
- **Opportunity cost is real:** Every hour spent building something that doesn't address the core business constraint is an hour not spent fixing what is actually holding the business back.
- **The "feels productive" danger:** Building AI tools gives a false sense of progress and cleverness, making this trap particularly hard to recognize from the inside.
- **Two primary business goals — and you usually can only pursue one intensely at a time:** (1) Growth, which requires reinvestment and accepts lower profitability, or (2) Profitability, which requires cost-cutting and less aggressive marketing spend.
- **The Hormozi-inspired escalation model for Step 2:** Before trying something new, ask in order — Can you do more of what already works? Can you do it better? Only if neither is possible should you explore something entirely new, because new approaches carry inherent risk and unknowns.
- **Simplicity over sophistication in AI systems:** The presenter explicitly recommends finding "the dumbest way to do it with AI." Both deterministic systems (e.g., coded workflows like n8n) and non-deterministic systems (e.g., Claude-based workflows) break — simpler systems break less often and deliver more consistency.
- **Consistency beats cleverness:** When using AI to scale or improve a business process, consistency of output is more valuable than marginal gains in intelligence or sophistication.
- **Be surgical, not exploratory:** The goal is to identify one specific constraint, one specific workflow, and one specific tool — not to experiment broadly with whatever is trending.
- **Review loops are non-negotiable:** If a solution is technically functional but not hitting business goals, you must be willing to pivot and try something else rather than defending the build.

## Use cases
- **Agency owners** who are considering or have already built AI tools (chatbots, automations) but haven't seen revenue or operational improvement.
- **Entrepreneurs in growth mode** who need to decide whether to focus AI efforts on acquiring more leads or reducing operational costs — not both simultaneously.
- **Business owners with a working lead generation or delivery process** who want to use AI to scale volume or improve quality without starting from scratch.
- **Founders overwhelmed by AI news and tool launches** who feel pressure to adopt the latest technology but lack a structured way to evaluate whether it's relevant to their business.
- **Operations or process leads** looking to automate a specific workflow and needing a decision framework to choose the right tool and validate results.
- **Consultants or advisors** helping clients implement AI who need a repeatable, client-facing framework to diagnose problems before recommending solutions.
- **Anyone who has built an AI solution that "works technically" but hasn't moved business metrics**, and needs a structured way to diagnose and fix the disconnect.

## Patterns & frameworks

### "Working Backwards" vs. "Working Forwards"
**What it is:** A mental model for how to approach AI adoption. "Working forwards" means starting from a tool or technology and looking for a problem to solve with it. "Working backwards" means starting from a desired business outcome and only then identifying the tool that bridges the gap.
**How it works:** Before touching any technology, you must clearly define what outcome you want and what constraint is preventing it. The tech decision comes last, not first.

---

### The Four-Step AI Implementation Framework
**What it is:** A structured, sequential process the presenter uses with consulting clients to ensure AI investments are tied to real business outcomes.
**How it works:**
1. **Define your goal** — Get specific about whether you're optimizing for growth or profitability this quarter. Clarity here prevents chasing two conflicting targets.
2. **Audit what you're already doing** — Map your current process and apply the escalation model: Can you do *more* of it with AI? Can you do it *better*? Only if neither applies should you consider something entirely new.
3. **Build the simplest solution** — Research tools and approaches, but deliberately aim for the least complex implementation that achieves the goal. Simplicity improves reliability and consistency.
4. **Review and iterate** — Once live, measure against your original business goal (not just technical performance). If it's not working, pivot without attachment to the build.

---

### The Escalation Model (Hormozi-influenced)
**What it is:** A risk-tiered decision ladder for deciding how to apply AI to a business process.
**How it works:** Start with the lowest-risk option and only escalate if it's not viable:
- **More** — Can AI help you do more of what already works?
- **Better** — Can AI improve the quality or efficiency of what already works?
- **New** — Only if more and better aren't possible, explore a new approach — with the explicit acknowledgment that new approaches carry unknown risks and unproven outputs.

---

### "Simplest Viable System" Principle
**What it is:** A design philosophy for AI workflow implementation.
**How it works:** Whether building deterministic (coded, rule-based) or non-deterministic (LLM-driven) systems, complexity increases failure rate. The presenter recommends deliberately choosing the simplest tool or workflow that achieves the business goal, prioritizing consistency of output over sophistication of method.
