# The $1000 AI Product Strategy people paid for

Video ID: `l5ORfS9h6hs`

## Summary
This video emphasizes that product strategy is the most crucial skill for product managers in the AI era, as AI's efficiency in feature delivery shifts the focus from outputs to strategic direction. The speaker, a former VP of Product, presents a tactical, 7-step framework for building a simplified, visualized, and continuously iterated product strategy, advocating for a move away from lengthy, static documents towards dynamic, impactful approaches. This methodology is designed to empower PMs at all levels to drive alignment, quantify business impact, secure resources, and lead effectively.

## Key insights
- **AI's Impact on Product Management:** AI tools (e.g., GitHub Copilot, Cursor, which ~60% of engineers use) dramatically increase engineer and designer productivity, demanding PMs provide clearer strategic direction to ensure they build the *right* solutions. AI also enables PMs to get closer to product building through AI prototypes, copy iteration, and writing AI evaluations (evals) to control for AI hallucination. However, AI cannot replace true human strategic thinking due to its inability to synthesize complex, real-world context.
- **The "Strategy Crisis":** Traditional product strategies (e.g., 9-page documents) are too long, vague, detached, and static, leading to a "drowning in documents, starving for direction" scenario where most team members cannot articulate their team's strategy.
- **Cost of Product Teams:** A typical product team of 5 engineers, 1 designer, and 1 PM costs roughly $1.4 million annually (e.g., $200k fully loaded for an engineer), highlighting the critical need for a strong strategy to ensure a positive ROI.
- **Modern Strategy Principles (2025):** Strategies must be continuously iterated (not annual), visualization-first (prototypes, mock-ups, videos over documents), and informed by the latest data. They should focus on clear choices, explicitly stating what *not* to do (e.g., Netflix prioritizing streaming over gaming/social in 2009).
- **"Small S" vs. "Big S" Strategy:** While "Big S" strategy (corporate-level) is driven by CEOs/GMs, "small s" strategy (team-level) should be owned by individual PMs, lead PMs, and group PMs to unlock their team's potential.
- **The Power of Snap Strategy:** A "one-day snap strategy" (often 80% accurate) can be drafted in under a movie's length (e.g., 2 hours for a job interview) using the 7-step framework, then iteratively refined over a week (strategy sprint) and validated over a month (design sprints) to produce a robust, company-wide strategy.
- **Quantifying Impact is Crucial:** Strategy must connect product metrics (e.g., activation rate) to top-line revenue (ARR/EBITDA) or bottom-line cost savings (e.g., reducing build time for Fortnite from 15 to 13.5 hours saved $1.5M/year in developer productivity), which is essential for securing resources from CEOs and CFOs.
- **Problem/Outcome-Focused Roadmaps:** Effective roadmaps should commit to "problems and outcomes" (objective, key result, problem) rather than traditional "features + dates" (Gantt charts, which consistently fail) or "themes + timeframes" (now, next, later).
- **Effective Communication:** Strategy must be communicated constantly and told as a memorable story (Current Reality -> Opportunity -> Approach -> Destination), adapted for different audiences (one-pager for executives, visual roadmap for teams, future benefits for customers).

## Use cases
- **Individual Product Managers:** Building a new product strategy from scratch, refining an existing strategy, defining team objectives and roadmaps, getting out of a "feature factory" environment by justifying strategic choices, and securing buy-in for initiatives.
- **Product Leaders (Directors, VPs, CPOs):** Setting strategic direction for multiple teams, allocating headcount and resources effectively, driving cross-functional alignment across the organization, and communicating high-level ("Big S") strategy.
- **Engineers and Designers:** Understanding the strategic rationale behind their work, enabling more autonomous decision-making, and leveraging AI tools (like Bolt, V0, Lovable) to quickly visualize and prototype strategy elements without requiring dedicated design sprints.
- **Startups:** Developing product-market fit with a clear, focused strategy.
- **Growth-stage companies:** Scaling product efficiently and strategically.
- **Enterprise companies:** Managing complex product portfolios and stakeholder needs with a unified vision.
- **Job Seekers:** Crafting a "snap strategy" during interviews to demonstrate strategic thinking and secure roles (e.g., the speaker's 2-hour strategy for a VP position).
- **Personal Goal Setting:** Applying the core principles of product strategy to one's personal life for clarity and execution (e.g., defining a personal mission, measurable goals, and biggest pain points).

## Patterns & frameworks
-   **Big S vs. Little S Strategy (Chundra Janikumar Raman, via Lenny's newsletter):**
    *   **Big S Strategy:** Top-level, corporate strategy driven by CEOs/GMs, focusing on the overall market and competitive landscape (e.g., Netflix's shift from DVDs to streaming).
    *   **Small S Strategy:** Product strategy at the individual team or product level, driven by PMs, Lead PMs, and Group PMs, focusing on how a specific product or feature contributes to the larger vision.
-   **Seven-Step Product Strategy Framework (Ed Biden):** A continuous, iterative process designed to build a focused strategy quickly:
    1.  **Challenge (Objective):** Define a clear objective using the "Mission + Measure" formula.
    2.  **Users:** Understand target users deeply through tools like Customer Journey Maps and Jobs to be Done.
    3.  **Superpowers:** Identify and leverage the organization's unique competitive advantages using Hamilton Helmer's Seven Powers.
    4.  **Vision:** "Show don't tell" the future product experience using visuals like storyboards, prototypes, videos, and mock-ups, often created with AI tools.
    5.  **Pillars:** Select 2-4 "big bets" (high-impact, high-effort initiatives) and intentionally decide what *not* to pursue.
    6.  **Impact:** Quantify the expected business impact of the strategy in terms of revenue growth or cost savings.
    7.  **Roadmap:** Plan the execution path based on problems to solve and desired outcomes, rather than fixed features and dates.
-   **Objective Formula: Mission + Measure:** A simple yet powerful way to define an objective by combining the overarching mission with a specific, measurable goal (e.g., "Increase 30-day retention from 18% to 22% by Q3").
-   **Customer Journey Map:** A tool for understanding user needs by visualizing their interactions with a product or service over time, emphasizing their journey and goals over static demographic segmentation.
-   **Jobs to be Done (Clayton Christensen):** A framework that focuses on the fundamental problem or "job" a customer is trying to solve, rather than just their demographics or a product's features (e.g., a milkshake's "job" as a long-commute sidekick). The video provides a canvas for mapping job performer, aspirations, related jobs, target job, steps, emotional/social aspects, outcomes, and differentiators.
-   **Hamilton Helmer's Seven Powers Framework:** A model for identifying sustainable competitive advantages (superpowers):
    1.  **Scale Economies:** Cost advantages from large production/operations.
    2.  **Network Economies:** Value increases with more users (e.g., Airbnb's host/guest network).
    3.  **Counterpositioning:** A new business model that an incumbent struggles to adopt (e.g., Airbnb vs. traditional hotels).
    4.  **Switching Costs:** Difficulty/cost for customers to change providers.
    5.  **Branding:** Strong, recognizable brand creating preference (e.g., Airbnb becoming a verb).
    6.  **Cornered Resource:** Exclusive access to a valuable asset.
    7.  **Process Power:** Superior internal processes leading to efficiency or quality.
-   **Four Major Types of Visuals for Vision:** The primary ways to "show" the future experience: Storyboards, Prototypes, Videos (e.g., Fortnite season teasers), and Mock-ups.
-   **AI Prototyping Tool Decision Tree:** Guides tool selection based on coding skill and project needs:
    *   **Cursor:** For coders who want to code on the project.
    *   **Replit:** For building a full-functioning app with database and deployment capabilities.
    *   **Lovable.dev:** The most user-friendly option for non-technical prototyping.
    *   **V0 (by Vercel):** For more technical users wanting additional capabilities and hosting.
    *   **Bolt.new:** For more powerful prototyping, especially for mobile apps.
-   **Strategy Sprint (1 week):** A structured approach to quickly draft and validate a strategy: Day 1 (Prep & context), Day 2 (Problems & opportunities), Day 3 (Strategic options), Day 4 (Validate & refine), Day 5 (Document & communicate).
-   **Design Sprints (1 month):** For deeper validation, mapping challenges, sketching competing solutions, deciding on the best approach, building prototypes, and testing with customers.
-   **Storytelling Arc:** A narrative structure for communicating strategy that resonates with audiences: Current Reality -> Opportunity -> Approach -> Destination.
-   **Anandati's 4B Framework (SVP of Product at Swiggy):** Categorizes initiatives to facilitate strategic choices and transparently communicate what initiatives are being deprioritized or cut:
    *   **Big Bets:** High-impact, high-effort, transformative initiatives.
    *   **Brilliant Basics:** Foundational features that must work flawlessly.
    *   **Bread and Butter:** Core, established offerings that generate consistent value.
    *   **Breaking Bad:** Initiatives or debt that should be phased out or removed.
-   **5-Second Strategy Test:** A quick self-assessment for strategy effectiveness: Can your team (designers, engineers) explain it in 30 seconds, use it to make decisions, and leverage it to say "no" to things? If not, the strategy needs refinement.
