# I Quit My High Paying Product Job to Bet on Myself

Video ID: `T4pvKvAE_SA`

## Summary
A senior product manager with over a decade of experience — including a seven-figure compensation year at Roblox — explains why he quit his job to become a bootstrapped solo founder and creator. The video presents five personal principles that guided this decision, centered on reclaiming time, identity, and creative agency. It blends career philosophy with practical self-reflection tools and is most relevant to mid-to-senior tech workers feeling the tension between compensation and fulfillment.

## Key insights
- He made seven figures at Roblox but still quit, framing money past a threshold as a poor trade for time and autonomy
- He calls out "product management theater" — the pattern of internal document polishing, stakeholder alignment theater, and ladder-climbing over actual product work — as a morale drain that exists at most large companies
- He was waking up at 6am unpaid for years to write his newsletter, which now has 100,000+ subscribers — he uses this as evidence that his zone of genius was never the day job
- The traditional career ladder pushes builders toward management, but he argues builders should resist this and stay hands-on; he even checked in code himself as a PM using AI assistance
- He distinguishes bootstrapping from VC-backed founding: bootstrapping lets you hit profitability and stop, whereas investors force a 10x–100x growth mandate regardless of your preferences
- He references the "Wait But Why" life-in-weeks visualization to underscore that being in his early 40s means roughly half his life is already over
- He hadn't had breakfast with his kids in months while balancing both jobs; after quitting, he now walks his 8-year-old daughter to school — 20 minutes each way — which he calls the highlight of his day
- He emphasizes that most co-workers don't stay in your life after employment ends, making work relationships a poor substitute for family and close friends
- He didn't quit until his creator business revenue roughly matched his product salary — advising others to build on the side before leaping
- His future focus is three things: a YouTube/newsletter AI learning resource, a membership site (behindthecraft.com) with AI tools and his "personal AI operating system," and building AI products publicly while sharing wins and mistakes

## Patterns & frameworks

**Zone of Genius (Gay Hendricks)**
Divides your work into four zones:
- *Zone of Genius*: what you're great at AND love doing — maximize time here
- *Zone of Curiosity*: what you're not yet good at but love learning — invest in this
- *Zone of Excellence*: what you're great at but don't enjoy — a trap; lucrative but draining
- *Zone of Incompetence*: what you're bad at and don't want to improve — eliminate

His application: building products and creating content = genius; AI agent development = curiosity; PM theater (OKR alignment, stakeholder management) = excellence/incompetence. The framework is used as a decision filter for major career moves.

**Product Management Theater**
An informal pattern he names to describe when PM work drifts from customer-facing output to internal performance: polishing decks, alignment meetings, managing up. He treats it as a diagnostic signal — the more your day looks like this, the further you are from your zone of genius.

**Bootstrapper vs. VC Founder distinction**
A mental model for choosing the right type of entrepreneurship: bootstrapping optimizes for autonomy and sustainability; VC backing optimizes for scale but removes control. Key insight: VC funding structurally prevents you from "stopping when it's good enough."

**Side-project-first transition**
A staged approach to leaving employment: build and monetize a side project until its income approximates your salary, *then* quit. Reduces financial risk and forces clarity on what the business actually is before you depend on it.
