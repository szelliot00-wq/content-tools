# 5 Underrated Google AI Tools You Need to Try in 16 Minutes (Pomelli, Stitch, Genie, Flow, and More)

Video ID: `BQ_mgojj0Mo`

## Summary
This video provides a hands-on review of five underrated Google AI products from Google Labs: Pomelo, Stitch, Project Genie, Flow, and Notebook LM. The presenter tests each tool with real-world examples, comparing strengths and weaknesses against competitors like Claude and Cloudinary Design. A recurring theme throughout is the "90/10 problem" — AI gets you 90% of the way there, but the final 10% of polish still requires manual editing, which Google's current tools make frustratingly difficult. The video is most relevant to small business owners, product managers, designers, content creators, and anyone exploring practical AI tools for marketing, design, video, and learning.

## Key Insights

- **Pomelo** extracts a brand's "DNA" (colors, fonts, logos, images) from a website URL in roughly 20 seconds and uses it to generate marketing campaigns and product photo shoots. It works best for businesses with strong product photography (e.g., physical or e-commerce stores). Content-first brands with weak visual libraries (like newsletters) get noticeably worse results.
- **Pomelo's photo shoot feature** lets you upload a product image and generate professional lifestyle photos around it (e.g., an egg tart placed in a scene with an Asian mom). Assets can be downloaded immediately for website or campaign use.
- **Stitch** is Google's AI UI/UX design tool, comparable to Claude's design tool. It generates a design system first (colors, fonts, components) before producing app screens — mimicking a real designer's workflow. It uses Gemini Flash under the hood for speed.
- **Stitch vs. Claude Design**: Claude's tool asks clarifying questions before generating (primary focus, vibe, device frame, which screen first), while Stitch one-shots the design from a single prompt. This is a significant UX gap, especially for non-technical users who submit vague prompts.
- **Stitch also generates a predictive heat map** showing where users are likely to click on any generated screen — a unique and useful feature for UX validation.
- **Project Genie** generates interactive 3D worlds navigable via keyboard, built frame-by-frame through AI image generation as you move. It takes about 30 seconds to generate a world from a text prompt (e.g., "neon cyberpunk city with a girl walking through crowds"). It is described as the most visually impressive product in Google Labs.
- **Genie is a tech demo, not a product**: The presenter explicitly pushes back on claims that Genie will disrupt gaming, noting that great games rely on core loops, progression systems, social dynamics, and multiplayer — none of which AI has figured out yet.
- **Flow** is described as Google's best interface for generating images and videos, powered by Imagen (referred to as "Nano Banana"), Gemini, and Veo working together in one canvas. Its standout feature is **frame-to-frame video generation** — you select a start image and an end image, and Flow generates a video that transitions between them.
- **Flow example**: A corgi walking down a Japanese street (start frame) transitioning to a corgi meeting a husky (end frame) produced a believable, smooth video. A more complex transition (diver swimming to the city of Atlantis) failed, resulting in a fade-out/fade-in rather than a natural transition.
- **Flow also generates audio and music** alongside video — a feature the presenter notes is underappreciated.
- **Notebook LM** is the most well-known of the five tools and is now integrated into the main Gemini app (the others are still Labs-only). It accepts multiple source documents and generates a variety of learning assets: infographics, slide decks, video summaries, mind maps, and audio podcast-style overviews.
- **Notebook LM's most famous feature** is the audio overview — a simulated conversation between two podcast hosts based on your source material — and users can now interject with their own questions.
- **Notebook LM limitation**: The slide decks are text-heavy, and there is no clean export to Google Slides or Google Docs for manual editing. The only editing mechanism is re-prompting the AI, which is inefficient for small tweaks.
- **Google's overall AI strategy critique** centers on three gaps: (1) Labs tools take too long to reach the main Gemini app (Notebook LM took over half a year), (2) Stitch is siloed instead of being integrated into AI Studio and Project Antigravity where it could serve as a visual front door to full-stack code generation, and (3) none of the tools allow direct manual editing of AI-generated output.
- **Competitive comparison**: Anthropic is shipping updates to Claude almost daily, while Google moves slowly from Labs to its main product surface — a strategic risk in the current AI race.
- **The coding race context**: The presenter identifies coding as the "crown jewel" of the current AI race and argues that Google is currently behind Codex and Claude Code. Stitch, if integrated into AI Studio/Antigravity, could differentiate Google by letting users design visually first and then generate full-stack code in one click.

## Use Cases

- **Small business owners** running physical stores or e-commerce shops who need marketing assets (campaigns, product photography) without a design team — Pomelo is purpose-built for this.
- **Startup founders or solo creators** who need to rapidly prototype a mobile or web app UI without hiring a designer — Stitch generates design systems and app screens from a single prompt.
- **UX researchers and product managers** who want a quick heat map to validate where users will click on a screen — Stitch's heat map feature applies here.
- **Game designers or creative technologists** who want to explore AI-generated interactive environments for prototyping or experimentation — Project Genie, used as a tech demo.
- **Video creators and marketers** who want to produce short AI video clips with controlled start and end scenes without complex video editing software — Flow's frame-to-frame video feature.
- **Students, researchers, and knowledge workers** who need to quickly synthesize large amounts of text (transcripts, papers, reports) into multiple learning formats (slides, infographics, audio, video) — Notebook LM.
- **Content creators and educators** who want to repurpose long-form content into shareable visual formats like infographics or slide decks — Notebook LM's output generation.
- **Product strategists and AI practitioners** evaluating Google's competitive position in AI and identifying gaps in their product integration strategy.
- **Newsletter writers and personal brand creators** considering Pomelo should note it performs poorly for content-first brands with limited product imagery.

## Patterns & Frameworks

### The 90/10 Rule (AI Polish Gap)
**What it is:** A recurring mental model used throughout the video. AI tools can reliably generate roughly 90% of a finished output (a marketing campaign, a slide deck, a video), but the final 10% — fixing specific copy, adjusting design details, swapping images — requires manual human editing.
**How it works:** The presenter applies this lens to every tool reviewed. The problem Google specifically has is that its tools force users to re-prompt the AI to make any change, even minor ones, instead of allowing direct manual editing. The prescription is that Google must build manual editing interfaces (or clean export paths) into all its AI-generation tools.

### Brand DNA Extraction (Pomelo's Core Loop)
**What it is:** A repeatable process where Pomelo scrapes a business website, extracts visual and identity elements (colors, fonts, logo, imagery), and uses that "DNA" as the foundation for all generated marketing assets.
**How it works:** Paste URL → AI extracts brand DNA in ~20 seconds → User confirms → AI generates themed marketing campaigns and product photo shoots based on the extracted identity. Quality of output is directly proportional to the richness of the visual assets available on the source website.

### Design-First, Code-Second Integration (Proposed Framework for Stitch)
**What it is:** A product integration pattern the presenter advocates for, where visual design tools feed directly into code generation tools.
**How it works:** User designs a prototype visually in Stitch → One click exports or passes the design into AI Studio or Project Antigravity → Full-stack code is generated from the visual prototype. The presenter frames this as the missing competitive differentiator that could help Google close the gap with Codex and Claude Code in the coding AI race.

### Frame-Anchored Video Generation (Flow's Creative Pattern)
**What it is:** A video creation workflow unique to Flow where the user defines the narrative boundaries of a video by selecting a start frame and an end frame, then letting AI generate the transition.
**How it works:** Generate images → Select one as the start frame, one as the end frame → Prompt the transition → Flow generates a video connecting the two scenes. Works best when the two scenes are visually and contextually compatible (e.g., same character, same environment). Fails when the scenes require a complex spatial or contextual leap (e.g., underwater to underwater city).

### Labs-to-Product Integration Speed (Strategic Framework for Google)
**What it is:** A strategic critique framing Google's competitive risk as a product velocity problem — great experiments exist in Labs but take too long to reach mainstream users in the Gemini app.
**How it works:** The presenter benchmarks Google against Anthropic (shipping to Claude almost daily) and notes that Notebook LM took over six months to move from Labs to the Gemini app. The recommended pattern is to adopt the Labs spirit of rapid shipping and apply it to the main Gemini product surface, closing the gap between experimentation and accessible consumer features.
