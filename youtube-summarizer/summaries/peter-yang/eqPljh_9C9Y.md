# Figma CEO on How to Get Good at Design in the AI Era | Dylan Field

Video ID: `eqPljh_9C9Y`

## Summary
This video transcript features Dylan Field, CEO of Figma, discussing the evolving role of design and product management in the AI era. Field argues that "design is the new code," where visual-first creation will allow direct manipulation to production, urging product managers and leaders to become "makers" rather than just document creators. He emphasizes the critical importance of human taste, craft, and point of view to navigate the infinite possibilities AI generates, advocating for a product development process centered on rapid divergence and convergence, continuous learning, and direct customer feedback. This discussion is highly relevant to product managers, designers, engineers, and leaders aiming to thrive and innovate in a fast-paced, AI-driven landscape.

## Key insights
- **Design as the New Code**: Field posits that design is becoming the "new code," where development will be visual-first, enabling direct manipulation on a canvas and even pull requests directly to production. This approach is considered superior to prompting or even traditional code editing for many tasks.
- **The "Maker" PM and Leader**: Product managers should evolve from primarily creating documents and slide decks to actively "making things." Leaders who are also makers inspire their organizations and drive inflection, shifting company culture to value tangible creation.
- **Defining Taste, Craft, and Point of View**:
    - **Taste** is about navigating possibilities, having clear preferences, and articulating what you're aiming for.
    - **Craft** involves pushing beyond where others stop, considering details from macro to micro levels, and ensuring all levels of abstraction fit together.
    - **Point of View** means expressing a unique insight or take on the world through a product, moving conversations forward, and having a clear vision for the future, even if it's not universally agreed upon.
- **AI as a Starting Point, Not a Final Answer**: AI models excel at generating vast "possibility spaces" or acting as "clay" to be molded. Their outputs are rarely final solutions; human judgment, craft, and intention are crucial for selecting, refining, and pushing these outputs to meaningful conclusions.
- **Non-Linear Product Development ("Hops")**: The journey from idea to product is no longer a linear sequence of stages (ideation, alignment, design, production) but a series of "hops" involving continuous divergence and convergence. Products are "never done" in the digital world, allowing constant updates and iteration.
- **Importance of Round-Tripping (Design <-> Code)**: It is essential to enable seamless, high-quality, and efficient round-tripping between design tools (like Figma) and code environments. This allows teams to start anywhere and move fluidly between visual design and implementation.
- **Maximizing Learning through Rapid Feedback**: To accelerate product development, teams should prioritize rapid cycles of learning, emphasizing direct customer feedback and prototyping over extensive internal reviews. Leaders should not be bottlenecks, and teams can use multiple prototypes (e.g., "five ideas") to gather diverse feedback without implying commitment.
- **Balancing Roadmaps**: Organizations must balance long-term, deterministic projects with the need to react rapidly to evolving technologies and market trends. The focus should be on the magnitude of impact rather than just the number of features (e.g., Figma shipped ~200 features last year, aiming for greater impact this year).
- **Tension Between Structure and Freedom**: Design tools face an inherent tension between providing structure (like Auto Layout in Figma) for consistency and productivity, and allowing creative freedom. The ideal is a system where users can easily move in and out of structure, gaining utility without being bound by it.
- **Hiring for the AI Era**: Figma seeks individuals who are "makers," enjoy learning technology, are not philosophically opposed to AI, and possess strong craft and judgment. This includes engineers who understand AI's utility and designers who are technical and can apply "bleeding-edge" thinking to new paradigms.
- **A Visual Renaissance**: Field expresses hope that the AI era will usher in a "renaissance period" for visual design, aesthetics, and interaction paradigms. Lower technical barriers will allow more people to explore and create, moving beyond current stylistic ruts in the industry.

## Use cases
- **Product Managers**: Transitioning from documentation-heavy roles to hands-on "making" and rapid prototyping.
- **Designers**: Utilizing AI tools for divergent exploration and idea generation, then applying human craft and judgment for refinement.
- **Engineers**: Integrating AI tools for coding assistance while maintaining core engineering wisdom and guidance.
- **Product Leaders**: Fostering a culture of rapid iteration, direct customer engagement, and empowering teams to be makers, while balancing long-term vision with short-term adaptability.
- **Startups**: Leveraging AI to accelerate idea exploration, prototyping, and rapid learning cycles to find product-market fit faster.
- **Teams with Bureaucratic Processes**: Streamlining product development by reducing excessive internal reviews and encouraging direct-to-customer feedback loops.
- **Companies Adopting AI Tools**: Developing strategies for integrating AI outputs as starting points into existing creative and development workflows, rather than expecting final solutions.
- **UX Writers/Copywriters**: Using AI to overcome writer's block and identify common phrasing, then infusing unique voice and point of view.

## Patterns & frameworks
- **Design is the New Code**: This is a core mental model suggesting a future where visual design tools and processes will directly translate into functional software, diminishing the role of traditional code as the primary blueprint.
- **Taste, Craft, and Point of View**: A three-part framework for evaluating and cultivating quality in creative and product work:
    -   **Taste**: The ability to discern and articulate preferences among many possibilities, guiding design direction.
    -   **Craft**: The dedication to meticulous detail and comprehensive thinking, ensuring all elements and abstraction levels of a product fit together flawlessly.
    -   **Point of View**: The expression of a unique perspective or insight about the future through the product, moving industry conversations forward rather than simply agreeing with existing norms.
- **Divergence and Convergence (Diamond Shapes / Hops)**: This describes an iterative product development process that moves beyond linear stages. It involves:
    -   **Divergence**: Broadly exploring a wide range of possibilities, ideas, or solutions.
    -   **Convergence**: Narrowing down and selecting the most promising options to refine.
    -   This cycle is repeated through "hops" between various artifacts (design, code, ideation, prototypes), allowing constant exploration and refinement, akin to "diamond shapes" stacked on top of each other.
- **AI as a Starting Point / Clay**: This is a recommended mindset for engaging with AI tools. Instead of expecting a perfect, final output, AI-generated content (whether visual designs, code, or text) should be treated as initial raw material or "clay" that requires significant human expertise, critical judgment, and iterative refinement to achieve a desired, high-quality outcome.
